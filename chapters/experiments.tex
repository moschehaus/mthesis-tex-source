\chapter{Numerical experiments}
\label{chap:EXPERIMENTS}
This chapter consists of numerical experiments using the methods described in the previous chapters. Namely, we compare the classical SPH method \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE} and the pressure-potential temperature density-free formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-theta} with their perturbation/well-balanced variants \Cref{sec:PERTURBATION-CLASSICAL-SPH} and \Cref{sec:WB-DI-PTH} respectively.

\section{Preservation of hydrostatic balance (stationary case)}
\label{sec:STATIONARY}

As established in \Cref{sec:WB-WAVES}, the preservation of the hydrostatic balance on \emph{the discrete level} is essential in simulations of internal gravity waves. In this section, we compare the ability of the used formulations to preserve the initial hydrostatic state.

\subsection{Classical SPH}
\label{sec:EXP-STAT-CLASSICAL}

	\caption{Time evolution of the spurious velocity field for the stationary hydrostatic balance using the standard SPH formulation, vertical axis is logarithmic. The maximum and root-mean-square velocities exhibit serious growth, sitting at approximately $\qty{10}{\metre\per\second} - \qty{15}{\metre\per\second}$ at $t < \qty{50}{\second}$ and diverging rapidly at $t \approx \qty{50}{\second}$. In contrast, the corresponding well-balanced (WB) variant maintains these norms at machine epsilon throughout the simulation.}
	\label{fig:STATIONARY-NORMS}
\end{figure}

The plots \Cref{fig:STATIONARY-BLOWUPS} show the spurious velocity field at the instant the maximum particle velocity reaches about half the speed of sound (Mach number of $\text{M }\approx 0.5$), which is roughly $\abs{\vb{v}} \approx \qty{120}{\meter\per\second}$. This threshold is chosen such that we can clearly make a distinction between numerical noise in the simulation and a serious breakdown of the hydrostatic equilibrium.
