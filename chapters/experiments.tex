\chapter{Numerical experiments}
\label{chap:EXPERIMENTS}
This chapter consists of numerical experiments using the methods described in the previous chapters. Namely, we compare the classical SPH method \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE} and the pressure-potential temperature density-free formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-theta} with their perturbation/well-balanced variants \Cref{sec:PERTURBATION-CLASSICAL-SPH} and \Cref{sec:WB-DI-PTH} respectively.

\section{Preservation of hydrostatic balance (stationary case)}
\label{sec:STATIONARY}

As established in \Cref{sec:WB-WAVES}, preservation of the hydrostatic balance on \emph{the discrete level} is essential in simulations of internal gravity waves. In this section, we compare the ability of the used formulations to preserve the initial hydrostatic state.

To describe the velocity field quantitatively, we plot the \emph{maximum particle velocity magnitude}, that is

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:L_infty}
\abs{\vb{v}}_{\infty} \coloneq \max_{\alpha\in \{1, \dots, N\}} \abs{\vb{v}_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

and also the \emph{root-mean-square velocity} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:L_2}
\abs{\vb{v}}_2 \coloneq \frac{1}{N}\sum_{\alpha=1}^N \abs{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}^{2}.
\end{equations}

The measures $\abs{\vb{v}}_{\infty}, \abs{\vb{v}}_{2}$ are discrete counterparts of the $L_{\infty}$ and $L_{2}$ norms.

Figures \Cref{fig:STATIONARY-NORMS} depict the time evolution of the velocity field of an atmosphere initialised from hydrostatic balance. They show the \emph{failure} of the standard formulations to \emph{preserve the hydrostatic balance}. On the contrary, the perturbation/well-balanced variants retain the hydrostatic balance at $\epsilon_{\text{mach}}$ level. Qualitatively, figures \Cref{fig:STATIONARY-NORMS} show that both the standard SPH and the potential-temperature formulations are not well-balanced, however the standard SPH performs considerably worse than the pressure-potential temperature formulation. For standard SPH, the magnitude of the spurious velocity reaches $\approx \qty{e20}{\metre\per\second}$, whereas with the pressure-potential temperature formulation, the magnitudes are not greater than $\approx \qty{e2}{\metre\per\second}$.

The plots \Cref{fig:STATIONARY-BLOWUPS} show the spurious velocity field at the instant the maximum particle velocity reaches about half the speed of sound (Mach number of $\approx 0.5$), which is roughly $\abs{\vb{v}} \approx \qty{120}{\meter\per\second}$. This threshold is chosen such that we can clearly make a distinction between numerical noise in the simulation and a serious breakdown of the hydrostatic equilibrium.
Notice the velocity field at the top part of the computational domain. During the development of the simulation code, an issue with the top boundary condition has been faced---if the top of the domain was chosen as open, in the very first instants of the simulation the whole system abruptly collapsed. The reason is that with an open lid, the particles near the boundary have \emph{vacancies in their kernel support}---they are influenced by an \emph{artificial vacuum}. This issue is common in SPH and the standard countermeasure is to add layers of \emph{ghost particles} \autocite{zhangBoundaryConditionsWeaklycompressible2025,adamiGeneralizedWallBoundary2012}. The result of introducing ghost particles, however, is the \emph{reflection} at the top boundary, which is clearly visible in the flow field.

From \Cref{fig:STATIONARY-STILNESS} it is evident that the perturbation SPH and the well-balanced pressure-potential temperature formulations \emph{preserve the hydrostatic balance exactly.} No spurious velocity field is present.

\section{Mountain waves (evolutionary case)}
\label{sec:EVOLUTIONARY}

We have shown in the previous section that both the standard SPH and the pressure-potential temperature formulations fail to preserve hydrostatic balance, whereas the perturbation/well-balanced variants succeed. As we initialise the non-stationary simulations from the hydrostatic balance and wish to capture internal gravity waves, only the \emph{perturbation SPH} and the \emph{well-balanced pressure-potential temperature} formulations have been used in the non-stationary cases.

The panels in \Cref{fig:WAVE-EMERGENCE-PTH} illustrate the emergence and early upward propagation of mountain waves using the well-balanced pressure-potential temperature formulation. As noted in \Cref{sec:FLOW-CORRUGATION}, stationary mountain waves take time to fully develop. Unfortunately, we cannot observe the propagation of these waves further, as the simulation is ultimately disrupted by \emph{tensile instability}, with its onset visible in \subref{fig:pth-wave-60}. The final section is dedicated to discussing this tensile instability in more detail.

As shown in \Cref{fig:WAVE-EMERGENCE-SPH}, in our experiments, the perturbation SPH method fails to capture the mountain wave dynamics. While the well-balanced pressure-potential temperature formulation successfully propagated the wave upward, the perturbation method yields a noisy unstructured flow field. 

We hypothesize that the reason lies in poor conservative properties of the perturbation SPH method. As we mentioned in \Cref{sec:GENERAL-CLASS}, a consistent use of \emph{adaptive smoothing lengths} requires the $\grad h$ to be be present in the momentum equation. In our case, as described in \Cref{sec:ADAPTIVE-SMOOTHING}, a simpler approach to $h-$adaptivity has been used together with the perturbation SPH method---in particular no $\grad h$ terms has been included in the formulation.\sidenote{It is also not straightforward how the terms could be included. Recall that they appeared naturally when deriving the SPH equations from the discrete Lagrangian in \Cref{sec:GENERAL-CLASS}. However, the standard derivation presented in \Cref{sec:CORNERSTONES} does \emph{not account} for variable $h$ at all.}

\section{Tensile instability}
\label{sec:INSTABILITY}

The phenomenon observed in our simulation of mountain waves that led to a complete numerical breakdown is well-known in the SPH community and is called \emph{tensile instability} \autocite{morrisStudyStabilityProperties1996,schuesslerCommentsSmoothedParticle1981,phillipsNumericalMethodThreedimensional1985,sunDeltaALESPHModelImproved2017,priceSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2012a}. Tensile instability is a \emph{geometric instability} that arises when the interaction between particles causes them to \emph{artificially} attract rather than repel, leading into unphysical particle clumps separated by empty void \autocite{morrisStudyStabilityProperties1996}. In our simulations, the particles lost their regular spacing, introducing yet another spurious unphysical effect that escalated into a total divergence of the velocity field.

		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{crash_SPH_0064}
		\caption{Perturbation SPH formulation, $t = \qty{770}{\second}$. }
		\label{fig:SPH-CRASH-770}
	\end{subfigure}
	\caption{Snapshots displaying the tensile instability in the perturbation SPH formulation. Even though this formulation was unable to capture the mountain wave dynamics, the terrain arguably introduces an irregularity in the flow. This loss of regularity might trigger the tensile instability, as visible in the particle clumping (left). The clumping triggers a sudden, explosion-like divergence in the maximum velocity (right).}
	\label{fig:SPH-CRASH}

As seen in \Cref{fig:PTH-CRASH}, the onset of the instability in the well-balanced PTH formulation is \emph{fundamentally different} from standard SPH case. Here, one gets the impression that the irregular configuration of the particles is \emph{triggered} directly by the \emph{emerged mountain wave}. The \emph{physical} density and pressure gradients introduced by the upward-propagating wave distort the particle configuration in the upper atmosphere, eventually creating clumps. Conversely, in the perturbation SPH formulation, as shown in \Cref{fig:SPH-CRASH}, the instability is triggered by the accumulation of spurious background errors.

		\label{fig:PTH-CRASH-725}
	\end{subfigure}
	\caption{Snapshots of the tensile instability in the well-balanced PTH formulation. Unlike the perturbation SPH case, the instability is triggered locally near the mountain by the physical perturbations in pressure and density caused by the propagating mountain wave. The oscillatory movements distort the particle distribution and lead to a rapid explosion-like divergence.}
	\label{fig:PTH-CRASH}
\end{figure}

To suppress this instability, a standard remedy is the $\delta$-SPH formulation \autocite{sunDeltaALESPHModelImproved2017}. The core of $\delta$-SPH lies in introducing an \emph{artificial diffusion} term in the evolution equations that acts against the forming of clumps, \ie acts repulsively. This procedure regularizes the density distribution, improves the particle distribution and helps in preventing the breakdown.
