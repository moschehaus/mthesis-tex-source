\section{Observed phenomena}
\label{sec:PHENOMENA}

\subsection{Internal gravity waves}
\label{sec:internal-gws}

\emph{Internal gravity wave}: is a type of wave phenomenon arising under certain (although common) atmospheric conditions\sidenote{More detail on these conditions is given in the section \Cref{chap:FLOWS}.}. Orographic flows typically induce two types of internal gravity waves: \emph{mountain waves} and \emph{lee waves} \autocite[p.~60]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. Although IGWs generated by a flow over terrain influence mainly the dynamics of atmosphere locally near that terrain, their contribution \emph{cannot be neglected} also in global climate and weather forecasts, due to the energy transfer across layers of the atmosphere that is typical for gravity waves \autocite[p.~58]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}.

\subsection{Downslope windstorms}
\label{sec:downslope-windstorms}

Under specific physical conditions\sidenote{To be transparent, it should be noted that the model of orographic flow under our study is \emph{unable} to describe downslope windstorms on its own, as it deals only with \emph{dry air.} The physics responsible for the emergence of downslope windstorms is the difference between \emph{dry} and \emph{moist} adiabatic lapse rates. Nevertheless, our model is capable of capturing mountain waves that interfere with downslope windstorms.}, air carried by a flow over a mountain obstacle can arrive warmer and drier at the lee side of the mountain, manifesting as \emph{warm} gusts of air descending from mountains into valleys. These types of winds are called \begin{inparaenum} \item chinook in North America; \item f\"ohn in Europe. \end{inparaenum} 
The interference of such winds with mountain \textcolor{gray}{and lee} waves can manifest as gusts of hurricane-level force \autocite[p.~307]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}, posing a great threat to the inhabitants and infrastructure of nearby regions.

\subsection{Clear-air turbulence}
\label{sec:CAT}
As mentioned, IGWs serve as an important mechanism of energy transfer across layers of the atmosphere. If particular conditions are met, it can happen that the wave propagating upwards grows in amplitude. Breakdown of these waves can produce a specific kind of turbulence, the so called \emph{clear-air turbulance.} This turbulence is extremely dangerous to aerial transport, as its presence is not accompanied by any kind of visual effects, hence the name \autocite[p.~58]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. A model that accurately captures mountain waves \emph{could} be helpful in predicting areas of CAT.

\section{Governing equations}
\label{sec:GOV-EQUATIONS}

The crucial part about consistent mathematical modelling of physical processes is deciding \emph{which equations to solve.} The atmospheric science community adopts various models ranging in complexity and the amount of physical phenomena it is able to capture: one could be interested in \viz global circulation, cloud microphysics (evaporation, condensation, precipitation), chemical mixing \& transport, radiative processes. In this work, we are interested mostly in \emph{fluid dynamics}, that is the description of the flow.

\subsection{Mechanics}
\label{sec:MECHANICS}

Recall the fundamental balance laws governing fluid flows

\begin{description}
	\item[Balance of mass]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MASS-BALANCE}
	\tdot{\rho} + \rho \div \vb{v} = 0 \eqcomma
	\end{equations}
	\item[Balance of linear momentum]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE}
	\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} = \div \cstress + \rho \vb{b} \eqend
	\end{equations}
\end{description}

In \Cref{eq:MASS-BALANCE}, \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE} $\vb{v}$ is the velocity field, $\rho$ is the \textcolor{gray}{mass} density, $\cstress$ is the Cauchy stress tensor and $\vb{b}$ is the \textcolor{gray}{specific} density of body forces. The dot operator stands for the material (convective) time derivative, that is 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\varphi} = \begin{cases} \partial_t \varphi & \text{in the Lagrangian frame,} \\ \partial_t \varphi + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \varphi & \text{in the Eulerian frame.} \end{cases}
\end{equations} 


To close the system, a constitutive relation for the Cauchy stress tensor has to be provided.\sidenote{As well as boundary and initial conditions; these will be discussed in \Cref{sec:IBCONDITIONS}.} The fluid of our interest is \emph{air}, which is commonly modelled as an \emph{inviscid compressible Newtonian fluid}, for which\sidenote{In the case of compressible Newtonian fluids, a general constitutive closure for the Cauchy stress tensor reads as \begin{equations}[single,*]
\cstress = -p \identityM + \lambda \left(\div \vb{v}\right)\identityM + 2 \mu \symvgrad,
\end{equations}
where $\symvgrad = \sym \grad \vb{v}$ and $\lambda, \mu$ being the viscosity coefficients. More precisely, $\mu$ is the \emph{shear viscosity} and $\lambda$ contributes to the \emph{bulk viscosity} $\zeta = \frac{3 \lambda + 2 \mu}{3}$. In the case of an \emph{inviscid} fluid, we put $\mu = 0 = \zeta,$ \ie the fluid does not pose any resistance to shear deformation nor compression/expansion.}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CAUCHY}
\cstress = - p \identityM,
\end{equations}

where $p$ is the thermodynamic pressure and $\identityM$ is the identity matrix. This constitutive asumption is standard in the field of internal gravity waves modelling \autocite{teixeiraPhysicsOrographicGravity2014,smithInfluenceMountainsAtmosphere1979,frittsGravityWaveDynamics2003,doyleIntercomparisonTREXMountainwave2011}. 

While it is standard to neglect bulk and shear viscosity, \ie the \emph{molecular viscosity}, some attention should be payed to turbulence and the \textcolor{gray}{effective} \emph{eddy viscosity} frequently used in its modelling \autocite[p.~5]{smagorinskyGENERALCIRCULATIONEXPERIMENTS1963}\autocite[p.~93]{popeTurbulentFlows2000}. At this point, a second important distinction should be made: not only are we interested in the flow solitarily, \emph{we declare internal gravity waves to be our main interest.} When dealing with internal gravity waves however, the Reynolds number of the flow is so great that even the effective eddy viscous forces are unimportant \autocite[p.~30]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}. Hence, ultimately we do not assume \begin{inparaenum} \item any kind of \textcolor{gray}{physical} viscosity\sidenotemark, \item turbulence parametrization. \end{inparaenum} \sidenotetext{The used discretization schemes however deploy some \emph{numerical} viscosity -- a standard stabilization technique.}

Finally, let us address the body forces $\vb{b}.$ In middle atmosphere dynamics, there are usually two main contributions: \begin{inparaenum} \item the Earth's gravity $\vb{g}$ \item Coriolis force \end{inparaenum}. Within our work, we \emph{neglect} the Coriolis force, and thus only assume the gravity; a discussion on the effects of the Coriolis force on internal gravity waves can be found in\autocite[p.~206]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}.

With the form of the Cauchy stress tensor \Cref{eq:CAUCHY} and the simplified assumption of present body forces the balance of momentum \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE} hence reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MOMENTUM_EQ}
\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} = - \grad p + \rho \vb{g},
\end{equations}

where $p$ is the \emph{thermodynamic} pressure. 

\subsection{Thermodynamics}
\label{sec:THERMODYNAMICS}

Our balance laws \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE}, \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM_EQ} have been formulated for a general inviscid compressible Newtonian fluid. To specify the continuous media of our interest, one has to specify the \emph{equations of state}, in particular provide a relation for the pressure $p$ and for the \textcolor{gray}{specific density of} internal energy $e$. As stated previously, the amount of attention we wish to pay to thermodynamics is limited, and as such we restrict ourselves to the thermodynamics of an ideal gas:

\begin{description}
	\item[Thermal equation of state]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:THERMAL}
	p\left(\rho, T\right) = R \rho T,
	\end{equations}
	\item[Caloric equation of state]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:CALORIC}
	e\left(\rho, T\right) = c_V T,
	\end{equations}
	\item[Entropy equation]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:ENTROPY}
	\eta\left(\rho, T\right) = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R T}{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}\right).
	\end{equations}
\end{description}

In the above equations, $T$ is the thermodynamic temperature, $R$ is the specific gas constant, $c_V$ is the specific heat at constant volume per unit mass, $\eta_{\text{ref}}$ is the reference value of \textcolor{gray}{the specific density of} entropy, $\gamma$ is the Poisson ratio. For dry air the values of these approximately are $R = \qty{287}{\joule \kilogram^{-1} \kelvin^{-1}}, c_V = \qty{717}{\joule \kilogram^{-1} \kelvin^{-1}}, \gamma = \frac{7}{5}.$

\subsubsection{Potential temperature}
\label{sec:POTENTIAL_TEMP}

In continuum thermodynamics of fluids, it is generally convenient to describe thermodynamic states using the density $\rho$ and \emph{thermodynamic} temperature $T$. In atmospheric sciences, it is common (and not entirely unphysical) to assume some processes to be \emph{adiabatic} or more precisely, \emph{isentropic} (meaning that they are adiabatic and also \emph{reversible}.) \sidenote{Although a simplification, the assumption of adiabaticity allows explaining various phenomena including \viz \begin{compactitem}
	\item downslope windstorms;
	\item katabatic winds;
	\item propagation of acoustic waves. 
\end{compactitem}
}

In such cases, it is convenient to introduce a \emph{conservative} thermodynamic variable -- \emph{potential} temperature $\theta$; let us introduce it now.
Consider a fluid in a thermodynamic state $(T, p)$ described by temperature $T$ and pressure $p$ that undergoes isentropic evolution to the state $(\theta, p_0).$ Then from \Cref{eq:ENTROPY} it follows

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R T}{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}\right) = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R \theta}{\rho_0^{\gamma-1}}\right),
\end{equations}

and so

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{T}{\rho^{\gamma-1}} = \frac{\theta}{\rho_0^{\gamma-1}}.
\end{equations}

Expressing now $\theta$ with the aid of the definition of the Poisson ratio $\gamma = \frac{c_p}{c_V}$, with $c_p$ being the specific heat at constant pressure per unit mass, and the Mayer relation $c_p - c_V = R$ one obtains

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T\left(\frac{\rho_0}{\rho}\right)^{\gamma - 1} = T\left(\frac{\rho_0}{\rho}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_V}} = T\left(\frac{p_0}{R \theta} \frac{R T}{p}\right)^{\gamma -1},
\end{equations}

where we have used the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} $p_0 = R \rho_0 \theta.$ Solving for $\theta$ now gives

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T \left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

and since $\frac{\gamma -1}{\gamma} = \frac{c_p - c_V}{c_V} \frac{c_V}{c_p} = \frac{R}{c_V} \frac{c_V}{c_p} = \frac{R}{c_p},$ we have arrived at a common expression

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:POTENTIAL}
\theta = T\left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}}.
\end{equations}

The quantity $\theta$ is called the \emph{potential temperature}. Let us summarize its meaning: it is the thermodynamic temperature an ideal gas (like \emph{dry air}) would possess if \emph{adiabatically} moved from the thermodynamic state $(T, p)$ to the state $(\theta, p_0).$ In meteorological context, the reference pressure $p_0$ is always taken to be $p_0 = \qty{1013.25}{\hecto \pascal},$ \ie atmospheric pressure at sea level. Potential temperature is especially useful, since it is \emph{conserved} under \emph{isentropic flows}. Indeed, under the isentropic constraint from \Cref{eq:ENTROPY} it follows

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\text{const} \, = \eta =  \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R \theta}{\rho^{\gamma-1}}\right),
\end{equations}

and so 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\theta = \frac{\rho_0^{\gamma - 1}}{R}\exp\left(\frac{\eta - \eta_{\text{ref}}}{c_V}\right).
\end{equations}

The right-hand side contains only constants, so we see

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:THETA-CONSTANT}
\theta = \, \text{const},
\end{equations}

and in particular

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:THETA-ADVECTED}
\tdot{\theta} = 0.
\end{equations}

\emph{Under isentropic flows, $\theta$ is conserved.}
\subsection{Hydrostatic balance}
\label{sec:HYDROSTATICS}

Let us summarize the governing equations, describing \emph{solely} the flow of inviscid dry air

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}
\dot{\rho} + \rho \div \vb{v} &= 0, \\
\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \grad p + \rho \vb{g}, \\
\tdot{\theta} &= 0,\\
p(\rho, T) &= R \rho T.
\end{equations}

Let us look for \emph{stationary solutions of} the system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} in the case of a \emph{trivial velocity field} $\vb{v} = \vb{0},$ \ie in the case of a static atmosphere. Choosing an inertial frame of reference $\left(\vb{e}_x, \vb{e}_z\right)$ such that $\vb{g} = - g \vb{e}_z$ the equations read as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho} = \partial_t \rho + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho = \partial_t \rho = 0, \\
	\grad p = - \rho g \vb{e}_z, \\
	p = R \rho T
\end{equations}

From the first equation we see $\rho\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \rho\left(\vb{x}\right).$ Writing now the balance of momentum in component, we arrive at 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HYDROSTATIC-P}
\partial_{z} p = - \rho g.
\end{equations}

This relation between the pressure $p$ and density $\rho$ is known as the \emph{hydrostatic balance} and will be extensively in the following.
If we now moreover assume the atmosphere is isothermal, that is $T = \, \text{const},$ then $\grad p = R T \grad \rho$ and we are left to solve

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\partial_{x}\rho &= 0,\\
	\partial_{z}\rho &= - \frac{g}{RT}\rho,
\end{equations}

solution of which reads as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\rho\left(z\right) = \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{g}{RT} z\right),
\end{equations}

with $\rho_0 = \rho(z = 0)$ being the boundary condition that has to be specified. Using the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} and the definition of the potential temperature \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL}, we also obtain

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HYDROSTATIC}
\rho(z) &= \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
p(z) &= p_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s} \right),\\
\theta(z) &= T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
T &= \, \text{const},\\
\vb{v} &= \vb{0}.
\end{equations}

where $p_0 = R T \rho_0, H_s = \frac{RT}{g} = \frac{\rho}{\abs{\partial_{z}\rho}}$ is the scale height of an isothermal atmosphere. This static solution to the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} is referred to as the \emph{isothermal hydrostatic balance}. In our numerical simulations, the isothermal hydrostatic balance will serve as an initial state. Note that the solution has been obtained in the case of an \emph{isothermal} atmosphere, which of course is a considerable simplification -- our Earth's atmosphere is \emph{not isothermal.} It is nevertheless advantageous to depart from the isothermal hydrostatic assumption, take it as a \emph{background} atmospheric state, and treat deviations from it as \emph{perturbations.}

\subsection{Boussinesq approximation}
\label{sec:BOUSSINESQ}

In the study of atmospheric gravity waves, yet another significant approximation is often made — the \emph{Boussinesq approximation} — which is by now standard in both textbooks on geophysical fluid dynamics \autocite{vallisAtmosphericOceanicFluid2017,holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013} and in the numerical-methods literature on atmospheric flows \autocite{durranNumericalMethodsFluid2010,teixeiraPhysicsOrographicGravity2014}. 
We have discussed that a suitable system of equations describing the dynamics of a \textcolor{gray}{dry ideal} gas is the \emph{compressible} Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}. The compressible Euler system is, to a sufficient degree, a physically consistent model, which has been proven throughout the history of science numerous times by solid comparison with experimental data. For instance, the Euler system is capable of predicting the existence of \emph{acoustic waves} with correct propagation speeds. 
The atmosphere is however such a complicated system that one would like to devise a simplification of the Euler system. The simplification should be such that the resulting system \emph{captures} the phenomena we are interested in -- internal gravity waves -- but \emph{filters} out at least some\sidenote{There are numerous types of \emph{atmospheric waves}, though the forcing mechanism is often the Earth's rotatino and the Coriolis force, which we neglect. The examples include
\begin{compactitem}
	\item Rossby waves;
	\item Kelvin waves;
	\item inertio-gravity waves.
\end{compactitem}
} of the phenomena we are not interested in -- acoustic waves. In this section, a set of simplified governing equations that filters out the propagation of acoustic waves will be derived. 

Consider the following splitting of our field variables $\rho$ and $p$

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\rho &= \rho_0 + \rho_{1},\\
	p &= p_0 + p_1.
\end{equations}

In both cases, the variable with the subscript zero stands for a \emph{background} \textcolor{gray}{mean} value, whereas the variable with the subscript one stands for the \emph{perturbation} from that background value. Assuming that the background pressure and density solve the hydrostatic \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC-P}, that is 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	p_0\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = p_0\left(z\right),\\
	\vb{e}_z\partial_{z} p_0 = \rho_0 \vb{g},
\end{equations}

the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} + \tdot{\rho_1} + \left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\div \vb{v} &= 0,\\
	\left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \vb{e}_z \partial_{z} p_0 - \grad p_1 + \rho_0 \vb{g} + \rho_1 \vb{g}, \\
\end{equations}

can be simplified to 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} + \tdot{\rho_1} + \left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\div \vb{v} &= 0,\\
	\left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \grad p_1 +  \rho_1 \vb{g}. \\
\end{equations}

Upon dividing by $\rho_0$, we arrive at

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:boussinesq_continuity}
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right)\div \vb{v} &= -\frac{\tdot{\rho_0}}{\rho_0} - \frac{\tdot{\rho_1}}{\rho_0}, \\
\label{eq:boussinesq_momentum}
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right) \tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} \vb{g}.
\end{equations}

Let us now introduce an approximation based on \emph{order-of-magnitude analysis.}\sidenote{A mathematically inclined reader should be warned that this "analysis" should be thought of only as \emph{line of reasoning} leading to the simplified system of equations. Once the system is attained, experimental evidence should support its validity.} Assume for a moment the existence of some wave phenomena propagating in the atmosphere with \textcolor{gray}{mean} amplitude $\lambda_z$. As the wave is only a perturbation \textcolor{gray}{of density and pressure} existing on the \emph{isothermal hydrostatic} background, it is natural to compare its amplitude $\lambda_z$ and the scale height of an isothermal atmosphere  $H_s = \frac{RT}{g} = \frac{\rho_0}{\abs{\partial_{z}\rho_0}}.$ Our assumption is that the amplitude of the wave is \emph{many orders smaller} than the scale height, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{\lambda_z}{H_s} = \epsilon \ll 1.
\end{equations}

At this point, the so-called \emph{Boussinesq approximation}\sidenote{Joseph Valentin Boussinesq introduced his approximaton to tackle buoyancy driven flows. Lord Rayleigh famously used Boussinesq's approximation for his analysis of Bénard cells in the problem of thermal convection of fluids -- the \emph{Rayeleigh - Bénard} thermal convection.} \autocite[p.~4]{spiegelBoussinesqApproximationCompressible1960} \autocite[p.~26]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} is introduced; it is assumed that

\begin{compactenum}
\item the ratio of the density variation $\rho_1$ and the background density $\rho_0$ is of order $\epsilon$, \begin{equations}[single,*]
		\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} = \bigO{\epsilon},
	\end{equations}
\item the density variations affect the buoyancy term $\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} \vb{g}$ substantially more than the inertial term $\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_{0}}\tdot{\vb{v}}.\sidenotemark$
\end{compactenum}
\sidenotetext{In the forthcoming section, we will show that buoyancy is in fact the driving force of internal gravity waves. Keeping the buoyancy term is essential.}

With these approximations, the following manipulation is carried out. In the balance of linear momentum \Cref{eq:boussinesq_momentum}, the inertial forces proportional to the density ratio on the left-hand side are dropped, whereas the buoyancy force at the right-hand side is kept present, obtaining

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho} \vb{g}.
\end{equations}

The treatment of the continuity equation will require a more involved order-of-magnitude analysis. Denoting $\vb{v} = u \vb{e}_x + w \vb{e}_z,$ and $U$ to be the characteristic scale of the velocity magnitude \textcolor{gray}{(\eg its mean)}, it holds

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} &= \partial_t \rho_0 + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_0 = \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_0 \propto U \partial_{z} \rho_0, \\ 
	\tdot{\rho_1} &= \partial_t \rho_1 + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_1 \propto \frac{U}{\lambda_z}\rho_1 + U \frac{\rho_1}{\lambda_z} = \rho_1 \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}},\\
	\div \vb{v} &= \partial_{x} u + \partial_{z} w \propto \frac{U}{\lambda_z} + \frac{U}{\lambda_z} = \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}}.
\end{equations}

Using these scaling relations, the balance of mass \Cref{eq:boussinesq_continuity} becomes

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right) \div \vb{v} \propto& \left(1 + \epsilon\right)\bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}}, \\
	- \frac{\tdot{\rho_1}}{\rho_0} - \frac{\tdot{\rho_0}}{\rho_0} \propto& - \epsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}} - U\underbrace{\frac{\partial_{z} \rho_0}{\rho_0}}_{= \frac{1}{H_s}} \propto - \varepsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}} - \frac{U}{\lambda_z} \underbrace{\frac{\lambda_z}{H_s}}_{\propto \epsilon} \\
	\propto& - \epsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}},
\end{equations}

and so we see that the \emph{in the leading term approximation} it holds

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\div \vb{v} = 0.
\end{equations}

Let us summarize the governing equations 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BOUSSINESQ-TOTAL}
\tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho} \vb{g},\\
\div \vb{v} &= 0.
\end{equations}

Equations \Cref{eq:BOUSSINESQ-TOTAL} are the standard form of the Boussinesq approximation of the Euler system used throughout the atmosphere-dynamics and geophysical-fluid-dynamics literature \autocite{vallisAtmosphericOceanicFluid2017,holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013,sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010,nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013,durranNumericalMethodsFluid2010}. Note that under the deployed assumptions, the flow of a \emph{compressible} fluid is effectively described as the flow of an \emph{incompressible} fluid. 
Note that the incompressibility condition $\div \vb{v} = 0$ immediately \emph{filters out} sound waves -- acoustic waves are longitudinal \emph{compression} waves, \ie they propagate only becuase the fluid parcel is allowed to locally compress/expand. The incompressibility condition enforces that the motion is isochoric, meaning cannot be any local compression nor expansion. One could equivalently assume sound waves propagate at an infinite speed, leaving the domain instantly.

At the end of this section, let us present an interesting result for the potential temperature \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL}. Through differentiating one obtains

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z} \theta_0 = \frac{1}{T_0} \partial_{z} T_0 - \frac{R}{p_0 c_p} \partial_{z} p_0.
\end{equations}

Using the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} we can also obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{T_0} \partial_{z} T_0 = \frac{1}{p_0} \partial_{z} p_0 - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z}\rho_0,
\end{equations}

and so in total

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z} \theta_0 = \frac{1}{\rho_0}\left(\frac{\rho_0}{p_0} \frac{c_V}{c_p} \partial_{z} p_0 - \partial_{z} \rho_0\right).
\end{equations}

Realizing that

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SOUND-SPEED}
\frac{c_V}{c_p} \frac{\rho_0}{p_0} = \gamma \frac{\rho_0}{p_0} = \frac{1}{c^{2}},
\end{equations}

where $c$ is the \emph{speed of sound} in the fluid, we can write

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z}\theta_0 =  \frac{1}{\rho_0}\left(\frac{1}{c^{2}}\partial_{z}p_0 - \partial_{z}\rho_0 \right).
\end{equations}

In the Boussinesq approximation, we have reasoned that $c = \infty$, and so we are left with

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:POT-TEMP-BOUSSINESQ}
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{\theta_0}z = - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z}\rho_0.
\end{equations}

This simple relation will prove useful to us in the next section \Cref{sec:STRAT}. 

\section{Mountain waves}
\label{sec:MTN-WVS}

So far, our exploration of internal gravity waves has been rather formal; the goal of this section is to show that the system of governing equations \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} allows for solutions in the form of \emph{waves.}\sidenote{Nappo reminds us to stay aware of the limitations of the linear theory -- \textbf{quotation:} \autocite[p.~30]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} \textit{"However, the possibility of waves is not a sufficient condition for waves".} 
There are certain cases when linear theory predicts the emergence of waves, yet in the real \emph{nonlinear} world, there are none, and vice versa. \autocite[p.~30]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. } 
The reader should be disclosed that the goal of this thesis lies in the development \emph{numerical methods} -- not in meteorology nor PDE analysis. The following derivations will thus lack some mathematical rigor\sidenote{Mainly in the sense we assume a priori sufficient regularity (differentiability and integrability) of the functions.} and few steps will be skipped completely. Our objective here is to mainly present that our model is in fact capable of capturing \emph{mountain waves}\sidenote{Keep in mind we have made quite a few (although common) simplifications along the way
	\begin{compactitem}
	\item not tracking any phase changes (\ie precipitation);
	\item no treatment of turbulence;
	\item not including the Coriolis force;
	\item assumption of adiabaticity of all processess.
\end{compactitem}}

and also to motivate the use of some techniques used in \Cref{sec:EXTRA}.

\subsection{Stratification and the Brunt–Väisälä frequency}
\label{sec:STRAT}

Taking a look at the relations describing hydrostatic balance \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC}, we see that density, pressure and potential temperature are functions of the \emph{altitude only}. In particular, they are constant on the horizontal planes. Physical quantities dependent of altitude are said to be \emph{stratified}.

In the case of isothermal hydrostatic balance, it also holds

\begin{compactitem}
\item $\partial_z \rho < 0, \partial_{z} p < 0$: density and pressure \emph{decrease} with altitude;
\item $\partial_z \theta > 0$: potential temperature \emph{increases} with altitude.
\end{compactitem}

The atmosphere is said to be \emph{stably stratified} if the potential temperature increases with algtitude \autocite[p.~25]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:STABLE-STRAT}
\partial_{z} \theta > 0.
\end{equations}

It is immediately obvious that the isothermal hydrostatic atmosphere \emph{is} stably stratified. Let us introduce at this point a of presentation a quantity which will prove useful in the forthocoming chapters. In a isothermal hydrostatic atmosphere, 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\partial_{z}\theta(z) = \frac{R}{c_p H_s} T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right) = \frac{R}{c_p H_s} \theta(z) > 0,
\end{equations}

and so we see that the quantity 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta} \partial_{z} \theta = \frac{R}{c_p H_s}.
\end{equations}

is positive and independent of altitude. It is thus sensible to define

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BRUINT}
N^{2} = \sqrt{\frac{g}{\theta}\partial_{z}\theta},
\end{equations}

as the \emph{Brunt–Väisälä} frequency. Meaning of this quantity will be made clearer in \Cref{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}. Notice finally that due to \Cref{eq:POT-TEMP-BOUSSINESQ}, under the Boussinesq approximation the Brunt–Väisälä frequency \Cref{eq:BRUINT} becomes

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BV-BOUSSINESQ}
N^{2} = - \frac{g}{\rho_0}\partial_{z} \rho_0.
\end{equations}

\subsection{Buoyancy oscillations}
\label{sec:BUOYANCY}

When an air parcel hits a mountain barrier, the topography can act as a ramp and eject the air parcel into higher altitude. In the case of a \emph{stably stratified atmosphere}, the buoyancy force decreases with altitude, and thus the net force acting on the air parcel points downwards. On its way to lower altitude, the air parcel overshoots its equilibrium position, which in turn leads to the \emph{increase} of buoyancy force, resulting in a total acceleration pointing upwards. This buoyancy-driven oscillatory motion can be thought of as a prototype of an \emph{internal gravity wave}. When the initial displacement from the equilibrium state of the air parcel is caused by a flow over topography, the resulting gravity wave is called \emph{a mountain wave.} \autocite[p.~143]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010} A different type of gravity waves associated with orographic flows are \emph{lee waves}: gravity waves trapped between the ground and upper levels of the atmosphere due to reflection \autocite[p.~60]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}.

\subsection{The Taylor-Goldstein equation}
\label{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}

As usual, let us equip a two-dimensional domain with the coordinate system $\left(\vb{e}_x, \vb{e}_z\right).$ Denote the velocity field by $\vb{v} = u \vb{e}_x + w \vb{e}_z$ and introduce the splitting

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	u\left(t, x , z\right) &= u_0(z) + u_1(t, x, z), \\
	w\left(t, x , z\right) &= w_0(z) + w_1(t, x, z), \\
	\rho\left(t, x , z\right) &= \rho_0(z) + \rho_1(t, x, z), \\
	p\left(t, x , z\right) &= p_0(z) + p_1(t, x, z). \\
\end{equations}

We introduce some approximations: \begin{inparaenum} \item the background state is hydrostatic, \item the Boussinesq approximation \end{inparaenum}, under which the governing equations \Cref{eq:BOUSSINESQ-TOTAL} read as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:APPROX}
\partial_t u_1 + u_0 \partial_{x} u_1 + w_1 \dv{u_0}{z} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0}\partial_{x}p_1,\\
\partial_t w_1 + u_0 \partial_{x} w_1 &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z} p_1 - \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}g, \\
\partial_x u_1 + \partial_{z} w_1 &= 0, \\
\partial_t \rho_1 + u_0 \partial_x \rho_1 + w_1 \dv{\rho_0}{z} &= 0.
\end{equations}

Next, the \emph{planar-wave ansatz} is assumed for the perturbed quantites\sidenote{This procedure is common in physics. Whereas a mathematician would presumably derive a wave equation/ hyperbolic equation and then \emph{prove the existence} of solution, in physics one starts from the \emph{assumption of existence} of wave-like solutions, manipulates the equations and derives expressions for the frequency $\omega$ and the wave vector $\vb{k}$; if the expressions match empirical data, the derivation is taken to be correct.}

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ}
u_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{u}(z)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
w_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{w}(z)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
\rho_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{\rho}(z)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
p_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{p}(z)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
\end{equations}

where the quantites with tildas are \textcolor{gray}{complex} amplitudes, $\omega$ is the wave frequency and $k$ is the wave vector. Plugging \Cref{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ} into \Cref{eq:APPROX}, the following system of \emph{algebro-differential equations} are obtained:\sidenote{Notice that the planar-wave ansatz produced from the original system of partial differential equations a system of algebro\textcolor{gray}{-differential} equations. This approach is very similar to the technique of Fourier transform.}

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ALG}
\I \left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{u} - \tilde{w} \dv{u_0}{z} &= \I\frac{1}{\rho_0}k \tilde{p},\\
\I\left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{w} &= \frac{1}{\rho_0} \dv{\tilde{p}}{z} + \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}g,\\
\I k \tilde{u} + \dv{\tilde{w}}{z} &= 0,\\
\I\left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{\rho} + \tilde{w} \dv{\rho_0}{z}  &= 0.
\end{equations}

It is useful to set $\Omega = \omega - u_0 k$ as a type of \emph{reduced frequency.}\sidenote{It is in fact a frequency of the wave relative to the air flow.} Moreover, the \emph{Brunt–Väisälä} can be introduced in the last equation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\dv{\rho_0}{z} = - \frac{\rho_0}{g}N^{2},
\end{equations}

due to the application of the Boussinesq approximation and the derived expression \Cref{eq:BV-BOUSSINESQ}. All in all, the system \Cref{eq:ALG} can be rewritten to the form

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ALG1}
\I \Omega\tilde{u} - \tilde{w} \dv{u_0}{z} &= \I\frac{1}{\rho_0}k \tilde{p},\\
\I \Omega\tilde{w} &= \frac{1}{\rho_0} \dv{\tilde{p}}{z} + \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}g,\\
\I k \tilde{u} + \dv{\tilde{w}}{z} &= 0,\\
\I \Omega \tilde{\rho} -\tilde{w} \frac{\rho_0}{g}N^{2} &= 0.
\end{equations}

We do not carry out the computations in detail, as it consists of rather uninteresting yet time-consuming algebraic manipulation. To retain credibility, we only outline the process and reference the kind reader to \autocite[p.~34]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} for further details.

\begin{compactenum}
\item observe that all equations in \Cref{eq:ALG1} contain the complex amplitude of the vertical velocity perturbation $\tilde{w}$;
\item the system \Cref{eq:ALG1} is rewritten as a single ODE for $\tilde{w}(z)$ of second order;
\item the substitution
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
		\label{eq:W-HAT}
		\hat{w}(z) = \tilde{w}\exp\left(-\frac{z}{2 H_s}\right),
	\end{equations}
	is made
\item rewrite the second order ODE for $\tilde{w}(z)$ as a second order ODE for $\hat{z}.$
\end{compactenum}

After the proposed manipulation, we arrive at 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}
\hat{w}'' + \left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c- u_0\right)^{2}} + \frac{u_0''}{c - u_0} - \frac{1}{H_s}\frac{u_0'}{c-u_0} - \frac{1}{4 H_s^{2}} - k^{2}\right)\hat{w} = 0,
\end{equations}

where $c = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{\Omega}{k} + u_0$ is the phase speed of the wave. The equation \Cref{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN} is called the \emph{Taylor-Goldstein} equation. 
Observe the structure of th Taylor-Goldstein equation in a \emph{special case} when the bracketed term on the left-hand side is constant and equal to $m^{2} \in \C$, that is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c- u_0\right)^{2}} + \frac{u_0''}{c - u_0} - \frac{1}{H_s}\frac{u_0'}{c-u_0} - \frac{1}{4 H_s^{2}} - k^{2}\right) = m^{2}.
\end{equations}

In this case, the T-G \Cref{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN} equation becomes

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SIMPLE-TAYLOR}
\hat{w}'' + m^{2} \hat{w} = 0,
\end{equations}

with the known solutions

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\hat{w}(z) = C_{+} \exp\left(\I m z\right) + C_{-} \exp\left(-\I m z\right).
\end{equations}

Coming back to the physical velocity perturbation using \Cref{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ} and \Cref{eq:W-HAT}, we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:W-1-SOLUTION}
w_1(t, x, z) = \hat{w}(z) \exp\left(\frac{z}{2H_s}\right) \exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right).
\end{equations}

If $m$ is a real number, then \Cref{eq:W-1-SOLUTION} describes a planar wave propagating in the $\vb{e}_x$ direction with the \emph{horizontal} wave number $k$, frequency $\omega$, whose vertical amplitude oscillates harmonically with the \emph{vertical} wave number $m$ in the $\vb{e}_z$ direction. This solution is reffered to as a \emph{propagating gravity wave.} \autocite[p.~35]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} 

If $m$ is \emph{not a real number}\sidenote{If $m$ has a nonzero imaginary part, then $m = \Re m + \I \Im m$, and so $\exp(\pm\I mz) = \exp(\pm \I\Re m \. z) \exp(\mp \Im m \. z)$.}, the solution is still a planar wave propagating in the direction of $\vb{e}_x,$ but the vertical amplitude of the velocity perturbation is exponentially damped; it can be shown that these waves do \emph{not} vertically transport energy and as such are called \emph{evanescent gravity waves.
} \autocite[p.~35]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}
