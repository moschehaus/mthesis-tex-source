\section{Observed phenomena}
\label{sec:PHENOMENA}

\subsection{Internal gravity waves}
\label{sec:internal-gws}

\emph{Internal gravity wave} is a wave phenomenon arising under certain (although common) atmospheric conditions.\sidenote{More detail on these conditions is given in the section \Cref{chap:FLOWS}.} Orographic flows typically induce two types of internal gravity waves: \emph{mountain waves} and \emph{lee waves} \autocite[p.~60]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. Gravity waves generated by a flow over terrain influence mainly the dynamics of atmosphere locally near that terrain, but their contribution \emph{cannot be neglected} also in global climate and weather forecasts, due to vertical energy transfer. This propagation of energy across layers of the atmosphere is typical for gravity waves \autocite[p.~58]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}.

\subsection{Downslope windstorms}
\label{sec:downslope-windstorms}

Under specific atmospheric conditions, air carried by a flow over a mountain obstacle can undergo a severe transformation: descending at the lee side as warm, dry and violent gusts of air. These types of winds are called \begin{inparaenum} \item chinook in North America; \item f\"ohn in Europe. \end{inparaenum} Note that downslope windstorms are far from being a meteorological curiosity: they can manifest as gusts of hurricane-level force \autocite[p.~307]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}, posing a great threat to the inhabitants and infrastructure of nearby regions.

Research suggests that \textcolor{gray}{nonlinear} mountain wave dynamics is essential in the development of downslope windstorms \autocite{clarkCriticalLevelReflection1984,durranAnotherLookDownslope1986,clarkEvolutionStabilityFiniteAmplitude1977}, although the explanation of this phenomenon requires a consistent treatment of a variety of mechanisms.
\begin{itemize}
	\item resonance of mountain waves \autocite{clarkCriticalLevelReflection1984};
	\item dissipative hydraulic transition between supercritical and subcritical flow regimes \autocite{durranAnotherLookDownslope1986};
\end{itemize}
Both mechanisms are inherently nonlinear and coupled: wave breaking aloft creates the conditions for a transition, which amplifies the near-surface lee winds and thereby reinforces the mountain wave amplitude.

\subsection{Clear-air turbulence}
\label{sec:CAT}

As mentioned, IGWs serve as an important mechanism of energy transfer across layers of the atmosphere. If particular conditions are met, it can happen that the wave propagating upwards grows in amplitude. Breakdown of these waves aloft can produce a specific kind of turbulence known as \emph{clear-air turbulence.} This turbulence is seriously dangerous to aerial transport, as its presence is not accompanied by any visual cues, hence the name \autocite[p.~58]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. A model that accurately captures mountain wave propagation \emph{could} aid in forecasting regions of CAT.\sidenote{Our model does not include any \emph{turbulence parametrisation}, \ie it cannot capture CAT directly. Clear-air turbulence can nonetheless emerge at regions of gravity wave breaking \autocite[p.~6]{frittsGravityWaveDynamics2003}, so robust modelling of gravity waves is a sensible first step toward handling CAT.} 


\section{Governing equations}
\label{sec:GOV-EQUATIONS}

The crucial part about consistent mathematical modelling of physical processes is deciding \emph{which equations to solve.} The atmospheric science community adopts different models ranging in complexity and the amount of physical phenomena they capture: one could be interested in \viz global circulation, cloud microphysics (evaporation, condensation, precipitation), chemical mixing \& transport, radiative processes. In this work, we are interested solely in \emph{fluid dynamics}, that is the description of the flow.

\subsection{Mechanics}
\label{sec:MECHANICS}

Recall the fundamental balance laws governing fluid flows

\begin{description}
	\item[Balance of mass]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MASS-BALANCE}
	\tdot{\rho} + \rho \div \vb{v} = 0 \eqcomma
	\end{equations}
	\item[Balance of linear momentum]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE}
	\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} = \div \cstress + \rho \vb{b} \eqend
	\end{equations}
\end{description}

In \Cref{eq:MASS-BALANCE}, \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE} $\vb{v}$ is the velocity field, $\rho$ is the \textcolor{gray}{mass} density, $\cstress$ is the Cauchy stress tensor and $\vb{b}$ is the \textcolor{gray}{specific} density of body forces. The dot operator stands for the material (convective) time derivative, that is 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\varphi} = \begin{cases} \partial_t \varphi & \text{in the Lagrangian frame,} \\ \partial_t \varphi + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \varphi & \text{in the Eulerian frame.} \end{cases}
\end{equations} 


To close the system, a constitutive relation for the Cauchy stress tensor has to be provided.\sidenote{As well as boundary and initial conditions.} The fluid of our interest is \emph{air}, which is commonly modelled as an \emph{inviscid compressible Newtonian fluid}, for which\sidenote{In the class of compressible Newtonian fluids, a general constitutive closure for the Cauchy stress tensor reads as \begin{equations}[single,*]
\cstress = -p \identityM + \lambda \left(\div \vb{v}\right)\identityM + 2 \mu \symvgrad,
\end{equations}
where $\symvgrad = \sym \grad \vb{v}$ and $\lambda, \mu$ being the viscosity coefficients. More precisely, $\mu$ is the \emph{shear viscosity} and $\lambda$ contributes to the \emph{bulk viscosity} $\zeta = \frac{3 \lambda + 2 \mu}{3}$. For an \emph{inviscid} fluid, we put $\mu = 0 = \zeta$, \ie the fluid does not pose any resistance to shear deformation nor compression/expansion.}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CAUCHY}
\cstress = - p \identityM,
\end{equations}

where $p$ is the thermodynamic pressure and $\identityM$ is the identity matrix. This constitutive assumption is standard in the field of internal gravity waves modelling \autocite{teixeiraPhysicsOrographicGravity2014,smithInfluenceMountainsAtmosphere1979,frittsGravityWaveDynamics2003,doyleIntercomparisonTREXMountainwave2011}. 

While it is standard to neglect bulk and shear viscosity, \ie the \emph{molecular viscosity}, the role of turbulence and the \textcolor{gray}{effective} \emph{eddy viscosity} frequently used in its modelling deserves attention \autocite[p.~5]{smagorinskyGENERALCIRCULATIONEXPERIMENTS1963}\autocite[p.~93]{popeTurbulentFlows2000}. Here, a second important distinction must be made: not only are we interested in the flow itself, \emph{we declare internal gravity waves to be our main interest.} Moreover, for internal gravity waves the Reynolds number of the flow is so great that even the effective eddy viscous forces are unimportant \autocite[p.~30]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}. Hence, ultimately we assume neither \begin{inparaenum} \item any kind of \textcolor{gray}{physical} viscosity\sidenotemark, nor \item turbulence parametrization. \end{inparaenum} \sidenotetext{The used discretization schemes nonetheless deploy some \emph{numerical} viscosity --- a standard stabilization technique.}

Finally, let us address the body forces $\vb{b}.$ In middle atmosphere dynamics, there are two main contributions: \begin{inparaenum} \item the Earth's gravity $\vb{g}$ \item Coriolis force \end{inparaenum}. Within our work, we \emph{neglect} the Coriolis force, and thus only assume the gravity; a discussion on the effects of the Coriolis force on internal gravity waves can be found in\autocite[p.~206]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}.

With the form of the Cauchy stress tensor \Cref{eq:CAUCHY} and the simplified assumption of present body forces the balance of momentum \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE} hence reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MOMENTUM_EQ}
\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} = - \grad p + \rho \vb{g},
\end{equations}

where $p$ is the \emph{thermodynamic} pressure. 

\subsection{Thermodynamics}
\label{sec:THERMODYNAMICS}

Our balance laws \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE}, \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM_EQ} have been formulated for a general inviscid compressible Newtonian fluid. To specify the continuous media of our interest, we need to specify the \emph{equations of state}, in particular provide a relation for the pressure $p$ and for the \textcolor{gray}{specific density of} internal energy $e$. As stated, the amount of attention we wish to pay to thermodynamics is limited, and as such we restrict ourselves to the thermodynamics of an ideal gas:

\begin{description}
	\item[Thermal equation of state]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:THERMAL}
	p\left(\rho, T\right) = R \rho T,
	\end{equations}
	\item[Caloric equation of state]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:CALORIC}
	e\left(\rho, T\right) = c_V T,
	\end{equations}
	\item[Entropy equation]\mbox{}
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:ENTROPY}
	\eta\left(\rho, T\right) = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R T}{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}\right).
	\end{equations}
\end{description}

In the above equations, $T$ is the thermodynamic temperature, $R$ is the specific gas constant, $c_V$ is the specific heat at constant volume per unit mass, $\eta_{\text{ref}}$ is the reference value of \textcolor{gray}{the specific density of} entropy, $\gamma$ is the Poisson ratio. For dry air the values of these approximately are $R = \qty{287}{\joule \kilogram^{-1} \kelvin^{-1}}, c_V = \qty{717}{\joule \kilogram^{-1} \kelvin^{-1}}, \gamma = \frac{7}{5}$.

\subsubsection{Potential temperature}
\label{sec:POTENTIAL_TEMP}

Continuum thermodynamics of fluids generally describes thermodynamic states using the density $\rho$ and \emph{thermodynamic} temperature $T$. In atmospheric sciences, it is common (and not entirely unphysical) to assume some processes to be \emph{adiabatic} or more precisely, \emph{isentropic} (meaning that they are adiabatic and \emph{reversible}.) \sidenote{Although a simplification, the assumption of adiabaticity allows explaining many phenomena including \viz \begin{compactitem}
	\item linear mountain waves;
	\item propagation of acoustic waves;
	\item buoyant convection in a dry, unstably stratified atmosphere (thermals).
\end{compactitem}
}

The description of these cases would benefit from \emph{conservative} thermodynamic variable---\emph{potential} temperature $\theta$; let us introduce it now.
Consider a fluid in a thermodynamic state $(T, p)$ described by temperature $T$ and pressure $p$ that undergoes isentropic evolution to the state $(\theta, p_0).$ Then from \Cref{eq:ENTROPY} it follows

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R T}{\rho^{\gamma - 1}}\right) = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R \theta}{\rho^{\gamma-1}}\right),
\end{equations}

and so

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{T}{\rho^{\gamma-1}} = \frac{\theta}{\rho_0^{\gamma-1}}.
\end{equations}

Expressing now $\theta$ with the aid of the definition of the Poisson ratio $\gamma = \frac{c_p}{c_V}$, with $c_p$ being the specific heat at constant pressure per unit mass, and the Mayer relation $c_p - c_V = R$ we get

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T\left(\frac{\rho_0}{\rho}\right)^{\gamma - 1} = T\left(\frac{\rho_0}{\rho}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_V}} = T\left(\frac{p_0}{R \theta} \frac{R T}{p}\right)^{\gamma -1},
\end{equations}

where we have used the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} $p_0 = R \rho_0 \theta$. Solving for $\theta$ now gives

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T \left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

and since $\frac{\gamma -1}{\gamma} = \frac{c_p - c_V}{c_V} \frac{c_V}{c_p} = \frac{R}{c_V} \frac{c_V}{c_p} = \frac{R}{c_p}$, we have arrived at a common expression

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:POTENTIAL}
\theta = T\left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}}.
\end{equations}

The quantity $\theta$ is called the \emph{potential temperature}. Let us summarize its meaning: potential temperature is the thermodynamic temperature an ideal gas (like \emph{dry air}) would possess if \emph{adiabatically} moved from the thermodynamic state $(T, p)$ to the state $(\theta, p_0)$. In meteorological context, the reference pressure $p_0$ is always taken to be $p_0 = \qty{1013.25}{\hecto \pascal}$, \ie atmospheric pressure at sea level. Potential temperature is especially useful, since it is \emph{conserved} under \emph{isentropic flows}. Indeed, under the isentropic constraint from \Cref{eq:ENTROPY} it follows

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\text{const} \, = \eta =  \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{R \theta}{\rho^{\gamma-1}}\right),
\end{equations}

and so 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\theta = \frac{\rho_0^{\gamma - 1}}{R}\exp\left(\frac{\eta - \eta_{\text{ref}}}{c_V}\right).
\end{equations}

The right-hand side contains only constants, so we see

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:THETA-CONSTANT}
\theta = \, \text{const},
\end{equations}

and in particular

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:THETA-ADVECTED}
\tdot{\theta} = 0.
\end{equations}

\emph{Under isentropic flows, $\theta$ is conserved.}
\subsection{Hydrostatic balance}
\label{sec:HYDROSTATICS}

Let us summarize the governing equations, describing \emph{solely} the flow of inviscid dry air

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}
\dot{\rho} + \rho \div \vb{v} &= 0, \\
\rho \tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \grad p + \rho \vb{g}, \\
\tdot{\theta} &= 0,\\
p(\rho, T) &= R \rho T.
\end{equations}

Let us look for \emph{stationary solutions of} the system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} when the \emph{velocity field is trivial} $\vb{v} = \vb{0}$, \ie for a static atmosphere. Choosing an inertial frame of reference $\left(\vb{e}_x, \vb{e}_z\right)$ such that $\vb{g} = - g \vb{e}_z$ the equations read as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho} = \partial_t \rho + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho = \partial_t \rho = 0, \\
	\grad p = - \rho g \vb{e}_z, \\
	p = R \rho T
\end{equations}

From the first equation we see $\rho\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \rho\left(\vb{x}\right)$. Writing now the balance of momentum in component, we arrive at 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HYDROSTATIC-P}
\partial_{z} p = - \rho g.
\end{equations}

This relation between the pressure $p$ and density $\rho$ is the \emph{hydrostatic balance} and will be deployed extensively in the following.
If we now moreover assume the atmosphere is isothermal, that is $T = \, \text{const}$, then $\grad p = R T \grad \rho$ and we are left to solve

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\partial_{x}\rho &= 0,\\
	\partial_{z}\rho &= - \frac{g}{RT}\rho.
\end{equations}

The solution reads as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\rho\left(z\right) = \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{g}{RT} z\right),
\end{equations}

with $\rho_0 = \eval{\rho(z)}_{z = 0}$ being the boundary condition that has to be specified. Using the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} and the definition of the potential temperature \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL}, we also infer

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HYDROSTATIC}
\rho(z) &= \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
p(z) &= p_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s} \right),\\
\theta(z) &= T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
T &= \, \text{const},\\
\vb{v} &= \vb{0}.
\end{equations}

where $p_0 = R T \rho_0, H_s = \frac{RT}{g} = \frac{\rho}{\abs{\partial_{z}\rho}}$ is the scale height of an isothermal atmosphere. This static solution to the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} is known as the \emph{isothermal hydrostatic balance}. In our numerical simulations, the isothermal hydrostatic balance will serve as an initial state. Note that the solution has been obtained for an \emph{isothermal} atmosphere, which of course is a considerable simplification---our Earth's atmosphere is \emph{not isothermal.} Nonetheless, simulations commonly initialize from the isothermal hydrostatic assumption, take it as a \emph{background} atmospheric state, and treat deviations from it as \emph{perturbations.}

\subsection{Boussinesq approximation}
\label{sec:BOUSSINESQ}

In the study of atmospheric gravity waves, yet another significant approximation is often made — the \emph{Boussinesq approximation} — standard by now in both textbooks on geophysical fluid dynamics \autocite{vallisAtmosphericOceanicFluid2017,holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013} and in the numerical-methods literature on atmospheric flows \autocite{durranNumericalMethodsFluid2010,teixeiraPhysicsOrographicGravity2014}. 
We have discussed that a suitable system of equations describing the dynamics of a \textcolor{gray}{dry ideal} gas is the \emph{compressible} Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}. The compressible Euler system is, to a sufficient degree, a physically consistent model, which has been authenticated by solid agreement with experimental data. For instance, the Euler system is capable of predicting the existence of \emph{acoustic waves} with correct propagation speeds. 
The atmosphere is, however, such a complicated system to describe that a simplification of the Euler system is desirable. Specifically, the simplified system should \emph{captures} the phenomena we are interested in --- internal gravity waves --- but \emph{filter} out at least some\sidenote{There are numerous types of \emph{atmospheric waves}, though the forcing mechanism is often the Earth's rotation and the Coriolis force, which we neglect. The examples include
\begin{compactitem}
	\item Rossby waves;
	\item Kelvin waves;
	\item inertio-gravity waves.
\end{compactitem}
} of the phenomena we are not interested in --- acoustic waves. In what follows, we derive a set of simplified governing equations that filters out the propagation of acoustic waves. 
Consider the following splitting of our field variables

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\rho &= \rho_0 + \rho_{1},\\
	p &= p_0 + p_1.
\end{equations}

In both cases, the variable with the subscript zero stands for a \emph{background} \textcolor{gray}{mean} value, whereas the variable with the subscript one stands for the \emph{perturbation} of that background value. Assuming that the background pressure and density solve the hydrostatic balance \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC-P}, that is 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	p_0\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = p_0\left(z\right),\\
	\vb{e}_z\partial_{z} p_0 = \rho_0 \vb{g},
\end{equations}

the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} + \tdot{\rho_1} + \left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\div \vb{v} &= 0,\\
	\left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \vb{e}_z \partial_{z} p_0 - \grad p_1 + \rho_0 \vb{g} + \rho_1 \vb{g}, \\
\end{equations}

simplifies to

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} + \tdot{\rho_1} + \left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\div \vb{v} &= 0,\\
	\left(\rho_0 + \rho_1\right)\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \grad p_1 +  \rho_1 \vb{g}. \\
\end{equations}

Upon dividing by $\rho_0$, we arrive at

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:boussinesq_continuity}
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right)\div \vb{v} &= -\frac{\tdot{\rho_0}}{\rho_0} - \frac{\tdot{\rho_1}}{\rho_0}, \\
\label{eq:boussinesq_momentum}
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right) \tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} \vb{g}.
\end{equations}

Now we introduce an approximation based on \emph{order-of-magnitude analysis.}\sidenote{This \enquote{analysis} serves as a heuristic, a \emph{line of reasoning}, to derive the simplified system of equations. Once the system is attained, experimental evidence must support its validity.} Assume for a moment the existence of some wave phenomena propagating in the atmosphere with \textcolor{gray}{mean} amplitude $\lambda_z$. As the wave is only a perturbation \textcolor{gray}{of density and pressure} existing on the \emph{isothermal hydrostatic} background, it is natural to compare its amplitude $\lambda_z$ and the scale height of an isothermal atmosphere  $H_s = \frac{RT}{g} = \frac{\rho_0}{\abs{\partial_{z}\rho_0}}$. We assume that the amplitude of the wave is \emph{many orders smaller} than the scale height, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{\lambda_z}{H_s} = \epsilon \ll 1.
\end{equations}

At this point, we introduce the so-called \emph{Boussinesq approximation}\sidenote{Joseph Valentin Boussinesq introduced his approximaton to tackle buoyancy driven flows. Lord Rayleigh famously used Boussinesq's approximation for his analysis of Bénard cells in the problem of thermal convection of fluids---the \emph{Rayleigh - Bénard} thermal convection.} \autocite[p.~4]{spiegelBoussinesqApproximationCompressible1960} \autocite[p.~26]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}; we assume that

\begin{compactenum}
\item the ratio of the density variation $\rho_1$ and the background density $\rho_0$ is of order $\epsilon$, \begin{equations}[single,*]
		\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} = \bigO{\epsilon},
	\end{equations}
\item the density variations affect the buoyancy term $\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0} \vb{g}$ substantially more than the inertial term $\frac{\rho_1}{\rho_{0}}\tdot{\vb{v}}.\sidenotemark$
\end{compactenum}
\sidenotetext{In the forthcoming section, we will show that buoyancy is in fact the driving force of internal gravity waves. Keeping the buoyancy term is essential.}

With these approximations, we carry out the following manipulation. In the balance of linear momentum \Cref{eq:boussinesq_momentum}, the inertial forces proportional to the density ratio on the left-hand side are dropped, whereas the buoyancy force at the right-hand side is kept present, leading to

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho} \vb{g}.
\end{equations}

The treatment of the continuity equation will require a more involved order-of-magnitude analysis. Denoting $\vb{v} = u \vb{e}_x + w \vb{e}_z$, and $U$ to be the characteristic scale of the velocity magnitude \textcolor{gray}{(\eg its mean)}, it holds

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\rho_0} &= \partial_t \rho_0 + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_0 = \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_0 \propto U \partial_{z} \rho_0, \\ 
	\tdot{\rho_1} &= \partial_t \rho_1 + \vb{v} \cdot \grad \rho_1 \propto \frac{U}{\lambda_z}\rho_1 + U \frac{\rho_1}{\lambda_z} = \rho_1 \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}},\\
	\div \vb{v} &= \partial_{x} u + \partial_{z} w \propto \frac{U}{\lambda_z} + \frac{U}{\lambda_z} = \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}}.
\end{equations}

Using these scaling relations, the balance of mass \Cref{eq:boussinesq_continuity} becomes

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\left(1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}\right) \div \vb{v} \propto& \left(1 + \epsilon\right)\bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}}, \\
	- \frac{\tdot{\rho_1}}{\rho_0} - \frac{\tdot{\rho_0}}{\rho_0} \propto& - \epsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}} - U\underbrace{\frac{\partial_{z} \rho_0}{\rho_0}}_{= \frac{1}{H_s}} \propto - \varepsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}} - \frac{U}{\lambda_z} \underbrace{\frac{\lambda_z}{H_s}}_{\propto \epsilon} \\
	\propto& - \epsilon \bigO{\frac{U}{\lambda_z}},
\end{equations}

and so we see that the \emph{in the leading term approximation} it holds

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\div \vb{v} = 0.
\end{equations}

In total, the simplified system reads as 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BOUSSINESQ-MOMENTUM}
\tdot{\vb{v}} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \grad p_1 + \frac{\rho_1}{\rho} \vb{g},\\
\label{eq:BOUSSINESQ-MASS}
\div \vb{v} &= 0.
\end{equations}

Equations \Cref{eq:BOUSSINESQ-MOMENTUM}, \Cref{eq:BOUSSINESQ-MASS} are the standard form of the Boussinesq approximation of the Euler system used throughout the atmosphere-dynamics and geophysical-fluid-dynamics literature \autocite{vallisAtmosphericOceanicFluid2017,holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013,sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010,nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013,durranNumericalMethodsFluid2010}. Note that under the deployed assumptions, the flow of a \emph{compressible} fluid is effectively described as the flow of an \emph{incompressible} fluid. 
Notice moreover that the incompressibility condition $\div \vb{v} = 0$ immediately \emph{filters out} sound waves---acoustic waves are longitudinal \emph{compression} waves, \ie they propagate only because the fluid parcel is allowed to locally compress/expand. The incompressibility condition enforces that the motion is isochoric, meaning there cannot be any local compression nor expansion. One could equivalently assume sound waves propagate at an infinite speed, leaving the domain instantly.

At the end of this section, we present a consequence of the Boussinesq approximation for the potential temperature \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL}. Through differentiation, we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z} \theta_0 = \frac{1}{T_0} \partial_{z} T_0 - \frac{R}{p_0 c_p} \partial_{z} p_0.
\end{equations}

Using the thermal equation of state \Cref{eq:THERMAL} we also see

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{T_0} \partial_{z} T_0 = \frac{1}{p_0} \partial_{z} p_0 - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z}\rho_0,
\end{equations}

and so in total

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z} \theta_0 = \frac{1}{\rho_0}\left(\frac{\rho_0}{p_0} \frac{c_V}{c_p} \partial_{z} p_0 - \partial_{z} \rho_0\right).
\end{equations}

Since it holds 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SOUND-SPEED}
\frac{c_V}{c_p} \frac{\rho_0}{p_0} = \gamma \frac{\rho_0}{p_0} = \frac{1}{c^{2}},
\end{equations}

where $c$ is the \emph{speed of sound} in the fluid, we can write

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_{z}\theta_0 =  \frac{1}{\rho_0}\left(\frac{1}{c^{2}}\partial_{z}p_0 - \partial_{z}\rho_0 \right).
\end{equations}

In the Boussinesq approximation, we have reasoned that $c = \infty$, and so we are left with

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:POT-TEMP-BOUSSINESQ}
\frac{1}{\theta_0} \partial_z \theta_0 = - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z}\rho_0.
\end{equations}

This simple relation will prove useful to us in the next section \Cref{sec:STRAT}. 

\section{Mountain waves}
\label{sec:MTN-WVS}

So far, our treatment of internal gravity waves has been rather formal; the goal of this section is to show that the system of governing equations \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM} allows for solutions in the form of \emph{waves.}\sidenote{Nappo reminds to stay aware of the limitations of the linear theory---\textbf{quotation:} \autocite[p.~30]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} \textit{\enquote{However, the possibility of waves is not a sufficient condition for waves.}} 
There are certain cases when linear theory predicts the emergence of waves, yet in the real \emph{nonlinear} world, there are none, and vice versa. \autocite[p.~30]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. } 
We remind the reader that the goal of this thesis lies in the development \emph{numerical methods}---not in meteorology nor PDE analysis. The following derivations will thus lack some mathematical rigor\sidenote{Mainly in the sense we assume a priori sufficient regularity (differentiability and integrability) of the functions.} and a few steps will be skipped completely. Mainly, we wish to demonstrate that the discussed model is in fact capable of capturing \emph{mountain waves}\sidenote{Keep in mind we have made a few (although common) simplifications along the way
	\begin{compactitem}
	\item not tracking any phase changes (\ie precipitation);
	\item no treatment of turbulence;
	\item not including the Coriolis force;
	\item assumption of adiabaticity of all processes.
\end{compactitem}}

\subsection{Stratification and the Brunt–Väisälä frequency}
\label{sec:STRAT}

Looking at the relations describing hydrostatic balance, we see that density, pressure, and potential temperature are functions of the \emph{altitude only}. In particular, they are constant on the horizontal planes. Physical quantities that depend on altitude are said to be \emph{stratified}.

In the case of isothermal hydrostatic balance, we moreover see

\begin{compactitem}
\item $\partial_z \rho < 0, \partial_{z} p < 0$: density and pressure \emph{decrease} with altitude;
\item $\partial_z \theta > 0$: potential temperature \emph{increases} with altitude.
\end{compactitem}

The atmosphere is said to be \emph{stably stratified} if the potential temperature increases with altitude \autocite[p.~25]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:STABLE-STRAT}
\partial_{z} \theta > 0.
\end{equations}

Immediately we observe that the isothermal hydrostatic atmosphere \emph{is} stably stratified. At this point, we present a quantity which will prove useful in the forthcoming chapters. In an isothermal hydrostatic atmosphere, 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\partial_{z}\theta(z) = \frac{R}{c_p H_s} T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right) = \frac{R}{c_p H_s} \theta(z) > 0,
\end{equations}

and so the quantity 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{1}{\theta} \partial_{z} \theta = \frac{R}{c_p H_s}.
\end{equations}

is positive and independent of altitude. It is thus sensible to define

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BRUINT}
N^{2} = \frac{g}{\theta}\partial_{z}\theta,
\end{equations}

as the \emph{Brunt–Väisälä} frequency. Meaning of this quantity will be made clearer in \Cref{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}. Notice finally that due to \Cref{eq:POT-TEMP-BOUSSINESQ}, under the Boussinesq approximation the Brunt–Väisälä frequency \Cref{eq:BRUINT} reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BV-BOUSSINESQ}
N^{2} = - \frac{g}{\rho_0}\partial_{z} \rho_0.
\end{equations}

\subsection{Buoyancy oscillations}
\label{sec:BUOYANCY}

When an air parcel hits a mountain barrier, the topography can act as a ramp and eject the air parcel into higher altitude. For a \emph{stably stratified atmosphere}, the buoyancy force decreases with altitude, and thus the net force acting on the air parcel points downwards. On its way to lower altitude, the air parcel overshoots its equilibrium position, which in turn leads to the \emph{increase} of buoyancy force, resulting in a total acceleration pointing upwards. This buoyancy-driven oscillatory motion can be thought of as a prototype of an \emph{internal gravity wave}. Schematically, the mechanism of a buoyancy oscillation is shown also in \Cref{fig:GW-diagram}.

When the initial displacement from the equilibrium state of the air parcel is caused by a flow over topography, the resulting gravity wave is called \emph{a mountain wave.} \autocite[p.~143]{sutherlandInternalGravityWaves2010}.\sidenote{A different type of gravity waves associated with orographic flows are \eg \emph{lee waves}: gravity waves trapped between the ground and upper levels of the atmosphere due to reflection \autocite[p.~60]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}.}

\subsection{The Taylor-Goldstein equation}
\label{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}

As usual, let us equip a two-dimensional domain with the coordinate system $\left(\vb{e}_x, \vb{e}_z\right)$. Denote the velocity field by $\vb{v} = u \vb{e}_x + w \vb{e}_z$ and introduce the splitting

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	u\left(t, x, z\right) &= u_0(z) + u_1(t, x, z), \\
	w\left(t, x, z\right) &= w_0(z) + w_1(t, x, z), \\
	\rho\left(t, x, z\right) &= \rho_0(z) + \rho_1(t, x, z), \\
	p\left(t, x, z\right) &= p_0(z) + p_1(t, x, z). \\
\end{equations}

We invoke the following approximations \begin{inparaenum} \item the background state is hydrostatic, \item the Boussinesq approximation \end{inparaenum}. Under these the governing equations \Cref{eq:BOUSSINESQ-MOMENTUM} read as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:APPROX}
\partial_t u_1 + u_0 \partial_{x} u_1 + w_1 \dv{u_0}{z} &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0}\partial_{x}p_1,\\
\partial_t w_1 + u_0 \partial_{x} w_1 &= - \frac{1}{\rho_0} \partial_{z} p_1 - \frac{\rho_1}{\rho_0}g, \\
\partial_x u_1 + \partial_{z} w_1 &= 0, \\
\partial_t \rho_1 + u_0 \partial_x \rho_1 + w_1 \dv{\rho_0}{z} &= 0.
\end{equations}

Next, we perform the \emph{planar-wave ansatz} for the perturbed quantities

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ}
u_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{u}(z; \omega, k)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
w_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{w}(z; \omega, k)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
\rho_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{\rho}(z; \omega, k)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
p_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \tilde{p}(z; k, \omega)\exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right), \\
\end{equations}

where the quantities with tildes are \textcolor{gray}{complex} amplitudes, $\omega$ is the wave frequency and $k$ is the horizontal wavenumber; note that $\omega, k$ are free to choose. To stress this, we add the $\omega, k$ as parameters of the complex amplitudes.\sidenote{The ansatz \Cref{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ} can be thought of as performing a \emph{Fourier transform} in $x$ and $t$ and restricting to a single $(k, \omega)$ component, \ie a single mode. The transform turns $\partial_{x} \mapsto \I k$ and $\partial_t \mapsto −\I \omega$, reducing the PDE system to a coupled system of ODEs (some degenerating to algebraic relations) in z alone.} Inserting \Cref{eq:PLANAR-ANSATZ} into \Cref{eq:APPROX}, the following system of equations is obtained:

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ALG}
\I \left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{u} - \tilde{w} \dv{u_0}{z} &= \I\frac{1}{\rho_0}k \tilde{p},\\
\I\left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{w} &= \frac{1}{\rho_0} \dv{\tilde{p}}{z} + \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}g,\\
\I k \tilde{u} + \dv{\tilde{w}}{z} &= 0,\\
\I\left(\omega - u_0 k\right)\tilde{\rho} + \tilde{w} \dv{\rho_0}{z}  &= 0.
\end{equations}

Denote $\Omega = \omega - u_0 k$ as a type of \emph{reduced frequency.}\sidenote{In fact $\Omega$ is the frequency of the wave relative to the air flow.} Moreover, the \emph{Brunt–Väisälä} frequency can be introduced in the last equation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\dv{\rho_0}{z} = - \frac{\rho_0}{g}N^{2},
\end{equations}

owing to the employed Boussinesq approximation and the derived expression \Cref{eq:BV-BOUSSINESQ}. All in all, the system \Cref{eq:ALG} can be rewritten to the form

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ALG1}
\I \Omega\tilde{u} - \tilde{w} \dv{u_0}{z} &= \I\frac{1}{\rho_0}k \tilde{p},\\
\I \Omega\tilde{w} &= \frac{1}{\rho_0} \dv{\tilde{p}}{z} + \frac{\tilde{\rho}}{\rho_0}g,\\
\I k \tilde{u} + \dv{\tilde{w}}{z} &= 0,\\
\I \Omega \tilde{\rho} -\tilde{w} \frac{\rho_0}{g}N^{2} &= 0.
\end{equations}

We do not carry out the computations in detail, as it consists of algebraic manipulations mainly, and only outline the process. Further details can be found in \autocite[p.~34]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}. 

\begin{compactenum}
\item Observe that all equations in \Cref{eq:ALG1} contain the complex amplitude of the vertical velocity perturbation $\tilde{w}$;
\item rewrite the system \Cref{eq:ALG1} as a single ODE for $\tilde{w}(z; \omega, k)$ of second order;
\item employ the substitution
	\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
		\label{eq:W-HAT}
		\hat{w}(z; \omega, k) = \tilde{w}\left(z; \omega, k\right)\exp\left(-\frac{z}{2 H_s}\right);
	\end{equations}
	
\item rewrite the second order ODE in $z$ for $\tilde{w}(z; \omega ,k)$ as a second order ODE for $\hat{w}(z; \omega, k)$.
\end{compactenum}

After the proposed manipulation, we arrive at 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}
\hat{w}'' + \left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c- u_0\right)^{2}} + \frac{u_0''}{c - u_0} - \frac{1}{H_s}\frac{u_0'}{c-u_0} - \frac{1}{4 H_s^{2}} - k^{2}\right)\hat{w} = 0,
\end{equations}

where $c = \frac{\omega}{k} = \frac{\Omega}{k} + u_0$ is the phase speed of the wave. The equation \Cref{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN} is called the \emph{Taylor-Goldstein} equation. 
Observe the structure of the Taylor-Goldstein equation in a \emph{special case} when the bracketed term on the left-hand side is constant and equal to $m^{2} \in \C$, that is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c- u_0\right)^{2}} + \frac{u_0''}{c - u_0} - \frac{1}{H_s}\frac{u_0'}{c-u_0} - \frac{1}{4 H_s^{2}} - k^{2}\right) = m^{2}.
\end{equations}

In this case, the T-G \Cref{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN} equation becomes

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SIMPLE-TAYLOR}
\hat{w}'' + m^{2} \hat{w} = 0,
\end{equations}

with the known solutions

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\hat{w}(z; k, \omega) = C_{+} \exp\left(\I m z\right) + C_{-} \exp\left(-\I m z\right).
\end{equations}

Coming back to the physical space, the velocity perturbation can be reconstructed from the single-mode profile $\hat{w}\left(z; \omega, k\right)$ and the substitution \Cref{eq:W-HAT} using the \textcolor{gray}{inverse} Fourier transform.\sidenote{Since the Taylor Goldstein equation is linear and the background profile is homogeneous in $t$ and $x$ \textcolor{gray}{(since it is hydrostatic)}, the Fourier transform can informally be thought of as \enquote{summing over all modes $\left(\omega, k\right)$.}}

To clarify our convention, we define the Fourier transform as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\hat{f}(k) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}f(x)\exp(-\I kx) \dd{x},
\end{equations}

and the inverse Fourier transform as
\begin{equations}[single,*]
	f(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\hat{f}(k) \exp(\I kx) \dd{k}.
\end{equations}

The physical velocity perturbation thus reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:W-1-SOLUTION}
w_1(t, x, z) =\frac{1}{(2 \pi)^2}\int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{w}(z; \omega, k) \exp\left(\frac{z}{2H_s}\right) \exp\left(\I\left(kx - \omega t\right)\right) \dd{\omega} \dd{k}.
\end{equations}

If $m$ is a real number, then \Cref{eq:W-1-SOLUTION} describes a planar wave propagating in the $\vb{e}_x$ direction with the \emph{horizontal} wavenumber $k$, frequency $\omega$, whose vertical amplitude oscillates harmonically with the \emph{vertical} wavenumber $m$ in the $\vb{e}_z$ direction. This solution is known as a \emph{propagating gravity wave.} \autocite[p.~35]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} 

If $m$ is \emph{not a real number}\sidenote{If $m$ has a nonzero imaginary part, then $m = \Re m + \I \Im m$, and so $\exp(\pm\I mz) = \exp(\pm \I\Re m \. z) \exp(\mp \Im m \. z)$.}, the solution is still a planar wave propagating in the direction of $\vb{e}_x$, but the vertical amplitude of the velocity perturbation is exponentially damped; it can be shown that these waves do \emph{not} vertically transport energy and as such are called \emph{evanescent gravity waves.} \autocite[p.~35]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} 

\subsection{Flow over a periodic corrugation}
\label{sec:FLOW-CORRUGATION}

In the previous section \Cref{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}, we have derived \textcolor{gray}{under common simplifications} the \enquote{wave equation for gravity waves}, \ie the Taylor-Goldstein equation. The T-G equations governs the vertical perturbation of the velocity field, but we have not clarified the \emph{source} of any perturbations whatsoever; \nb up to this point, the mountain terrain has not yet entered any of our consideration. At last, we will show that the mountain terrain can in fact \emph{generate} propagating gravity waves.

Real physical topography is complicated. Our analysis of the mountain wave \emph{theory} in this section will be restricted to the case of a mere \emph{surface corrugation}, moreover a \emph{periodic one} with height many orders of magnitude lower than typical atmospheric scales. Such assumptions are massively restrictive and will not be able to describe real complex orographic flows. Realize, however, that we are dealing with a \emph{linear theory}, \ie small perturbations of the flow field. If one considers real structured topographical profiles of substantial height, the linear theory ceases to be applicable.

Assume a constant horizontal flow $u_0 \vb{e}_x, u_0 >0$ constant, in the $\vb{e}_x$ direction interacting with a corrugation, profile of which is given as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CORR}
h(x) = H \cos\left(k_s x\right), \;\rightsquigarrow\; h(x) =H \exp(\I k_s x)
\end{equations}

where $H > 0$ is the height of the corrugation and $k_s = \frac{2 \pi}{\lambda_s}$ is related to the spatial period of the corrugation peaks, \ie $k_s$ is the \enquote{corrugation wavenumber}. As usual, we proceed with complex numbers and at the end of the computation restrict ourselves to their real parts. The Taylor-Goldstein equation \Cref{eq:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN} reads as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\hat{w}'' + \left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c- u_0\right)^{2}} + \frac{u_0''}{c - u_0} - \frac{1}{H_s}\frac{u_0'}{c-u_0} - \frac{1}{4 H_s^{2}} - k^{2}\right)\hat{w} = 0.
\end{equations}

Observed properties of mountain waves support the following assumptions \autocite[p.~36]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013} 

\begin{itemize}
	\item for sufficiently short horizontal wavelengths $k$ it holds $\frac{1}{4H_s^{2}} \ll k^{2}$ and the term is dropped.
	\item the Brunt–Väisälä frequency $N$ is assumed to be constant;
	\item since $u_0$ is constant, $u_0' = 0 = u_0''$;
\end{itemize}

Under these approximations, the Taylor Goldstein equation yields

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\hat{w}'' +\left(\frac{N^{2}}{\left(c - u_0\right)^{2}} - k^{2} \right)\hat{w} = 0.
\end{equations}

Moreover, real life observed mountain waves are \emph{stationary} relative to the ground \autocite[p.~58]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}, meaning $\omega = 0$ and so $\Omega = - k u_0$. This realization gives us the Taylor Goldstein equation in the form

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:T-G-surrogation}
\hat{w}'' + \left(\frac{N^{2}}{u_0^{2}} - k^{2}\right)\hat{w} = 0.
\end{equations}

Since $N^{2}, u_0^{2}$ are constants, \Cref{eq:T-G-surrogation} is the equation of a \emph{linear harmonic oscillator} with the vertical wavenumber\sidenote{In the case of the harmonic oscillator, the variable is usually time, so the \enquote{periodicity coefficient} is the angular frequency $\omega$. Since our variable is the coordinate $z$, we prefer to call the coefficient vertical wavenumber.}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
m^{2} = \frac{N^{2}}{u_0^{2}} - k^{2}.
\end{equations}

The general solution of \Cref{eq:T-G-surrogation} has the form

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\hat{w}(z; 0, k) = C_{+}\exp\left(\I m z\right) + C_{-}\exp\left(-\I m z\right),
\end{equations}

where $C_{+}, C_{-} \in \R$ are constants to be determined using the boundary conditions. If $C_{+}$ were nonzero, it would correspond to gravity waves propagating downward, \ie \emph{energy propagating downward}. Since the top of the domain can in principle extend to the whole outer atmosphere, a nonzero $C_{+}$ would amount to allowing energy to arrive from infinity\sidenote{This assumption consequently entails the absence of wave reflections at the upper boundary.} ---something we consider unphysical--- and thus we set $C_{+} \equiv 0$. \autocite[p.~97]{smithInfluenceMountainsAtmosphere1979} This \enquote{boundary condition} is known as the \emph{radiation condition.} \autocite[p.~62]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}\autocite{klempUpperBoundaryCondition1983}. 

As for $C_{-}$, we see that 

\begin{equations}[single,!, numberline=all]
	\label{eq:C-}
	C_- = \eval{\hat{w}\left(z; 0, k\right)}_{z = 0},
\end{equations}

which motivates our need to study the bottom boundary condition.\sidenote{This chapter aims to provide some insight into the physics mainly. A well-posed problem should have the boundary conditions stated in its description.} In agreement with the notation used in the section \Cref{sec:TAYLOR-GOLDSTEIN}, we are denoting the solution of the T-G equation as $\hat{w}\left(z; \omega, k\right)$, so for mountain waves with $\omega = 0$ we denote the solution as $\hat{w}\left(z; 0, k\right)$.

At the bottom boundary, \ie at the mountain surface, it is most natural to assume the \emph{no-penetration}, also known as the \emph{free-slip} boundary condition 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\vb{v}\left(x, z\right) \cdot \vb{n}\left(x, z\right) = 0, \quad \text{on} \quad \left\{\left(x,z\right) \in \R^2 \;\middle|\; z = h(x)\right\},
\end{equations}

with $\vb{v} = \vb{e}_x u_0 + \vb{e}_z w_1\left(x, z\right).$ The bottom surface is given as $\Phi\left(x, z\right) = 0, $ with 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\Phi\left(x, z\right) = z - h(x),
\end{equations}

and so the unit normal can be computed as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\vb{n}\left(x, z\right) = \frac{\grad \Phi}{\abs{\grad \Phi}} = \frac{\vb{e}_z - \vb{e}_x h'(x)}{\sqrt{1 + \left(h'\right)^{2}}},
\end{equations}

meaning at the mountain surface $z = h(x)$ it must hold

\begin{equations}[single,*]
0 = \vb{v} \cdot \vb{n} = \frac{w_1(t, x, h(x)) -h'(x) u_0}{\sqrt{1 + \left(h'\right)^{2}}},
\end{equations}

and thus our bottom boundary condition is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\eval{w_1(t, x, z)}_{z = h(x)} = h'(x) u_0, t > 0.
\end{equations}

At this point, another important  simplification of the linear theory is needed \autocite[p.~96]{smithInfluenceMountainsAtmosphere1979}\autocite[p.~63]{nappoIntroductionAtmosphericGravity2013}: we impose the bottom boundary condition \emph{not at the mountain surface} $z = h(x)$, but \emph{at the line} $z = 0$, \ie on the flat ground. This is justified under the assumption that corrugation height is small. The bottom boundary condition we will use thus reads

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:BOTTOM-BC}
\eval{w_1\left(t, x, z\right)}_{z = 0} = u_0 h'(x), t > 0.
\end{equations}

To find $C_{-}$, \Cref{eq:C-} we need to apply the Fourier transform 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\eval{\hat{w}\left(z; 0, k\right)}_{z = 0} = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}u_0 h'(x) \exp\left(-\I kx\right)\dd{x},
\end{equations}

which using \Cref{eq:CORR} reads as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\eval{\hat{w}\left(z; 0, k\right)}_{z = 0} = - \I k_s H u_0 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \exp\left(-\I \left(k + k_s\right)x\right)\dd{x} = - 2 \pi \I k_s H u_0 \delta\left(k + k_s\right),
\end{equations}

thanks to the fact that inverse Fourier transform of $1$ is the Dirac's delta distribution

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\delta\left(k + k_s\right) = \frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\exp\left(-\I\left(k+k_s\right)x\right)\dd{x}.
\end{equations}

We have thus obtained $C_- = - 2 \pi \I k_s H u_0 \delta\left(k + k_s\right),$ and so the velocity has the form \Cref{eq:W-1-SOLUTION}

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	w_1\left(t, x, z\right) &= \Re\left(\frac{1}{2 \pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}C_{-}\exp\left(-\I mz\right) \exp\left(\I kx\right) \delta\left(k + k_s\right)\dd{k}\right) = \\
				&= \Re\left(\frac{-\I}{2 \pi} 2\pi k_s H u_0 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\exp\left(-\I \left(mz - kx\right)\right) \delta\left(k + k_s\right)\dd{k}\right) = \\
				&= -\Re\left(\I k_s H u_0\exp\left(-\I\left(m_s z + k_s x\right)\right)\right) \\
				&= - u_0 k_s H \sin\left(m_s z + k_s x\right),
\end{equations}

where 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
m_s^{2} = \frac{N^{2}}{u_0^{2}} - k_s^{2},
\end{equations}

is the vertical wavenumber. The solution we have obtained

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:W-1-CORRUGATION-SOLUTION}
	w_1\left(t, x, z\right) =  - u_0 k_s H \sin\left(m_sz + k_s x\right),
\end{equations}

is a monochromatic sinusoidal \emph{propagating gravity wave.} The solution is plotted in figure \Cref{fig:PROPAGATING}. Notice the phase front tilts upstream with altitude --- a sign of vertical energy propagation.
