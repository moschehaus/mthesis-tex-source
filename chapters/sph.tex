\section{Method overview}
\label{sec:SPH_OVERVIEW}

Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is a general use numerical method first used in astrophysical stellar models \autocite{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}. The authors required a solution to a grid resolution problem arising with their use of the finite difference method (FDM).\sidenote{They argued that the treatment of highly distorted continuum would require an extremely fine grid, hence substantial computational cost \autocite[p.~1]{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}.} The method has found use in a variety of fields, \viz free-surface flows \autocite{monaghanSimulatingFreeSurface1994a}, solid mechanics \autocite{liberskySmoothParticleHydrodynamics1991}, geomechanics \autocite{buiLagrangianMeshfreeParticles2008}, and is still successfully used in astrophysics \autocite{springelSimulationsFormationEvolution2005}. 

SPH is a particle (Lagrangian) method.\sidenote{In this sense grid/mesh methods such as the \begin{inparaenum} \item finite difference method; \item finite element method; \item finite volume method; \item discontinuous Galerkin method, \end{inparaenum} are called \emph{Eulerian}, since they approximate the evolution in the \emph{current, deformed} configuration. Particle methods like \begin{inparaenum} \item SPH; \item MPS (moving particle semi-implicit method) \autocite{koshizukaMovingParticleSemiImplicitMethod1996}; \item DPD (dissipative particle dynamics) \autocite{hoogerbruggeSimulatingMicroscopicHydrodynamic1992} \end{inparaenum} trace the evolution in the \emph{reference} configuration, and so particle methods are also called \emph{Lagrangian}.}   The continuum is partitioned into arbitrary \emph{material points}, in this context referred to as \emph{particles}, and basic physical properties such as density, velocity, entropy, and energy are assigned to them. Their mutual interaction is then prescribed,\sidenote{The interaction cannot be arbitrary. To capture the physics correctly, the interaction must in fact root in the discretisation of the governing equations.} and the state of each particle is advanced in time. 
We stress that \emph{SPH particles} are \emph{not} \textcolor{gray}{elementary} particles in the microscopic sense --- they are typically macroscopic fluid elements. Nonetheless, some intuition from the mechanics of point particles carries over naturally to SPH, as we shall see.

In what follows, we introduce the theoretical foundations of SPH and the specific formulation developed in this work.

\subsection{Kernel interpolation}
\label{sec:INTERPOLATION}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be the continuous medium and $\Phi: \Rplusnought \times \Omega \to \R$ an arbitrary scalar valued field, \eg density. Suppose further that $\forall t \in \Rplusnought$ the mapping $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi(t, \vb{x})$ is in $\Loneloc{\Omega}$, then

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY}
\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \left(\Phi \star \delta\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)\delta\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x'}},
\end{equations}

holds for almost all $\vb{x} \in \Omega$. A common assumption in the SPH literature is to assume $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \in \Czero{\Omega}$, and so the equality \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} holds pointwise in $\Omega$. To make the presentation simpler, throughout we always assume sufficient regularity of all quantities involved and do not pursue measure-theoretic rigor.

The main point of departure of the SPH method is to replace the Dirac delta distribution in \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} by an \emph{interpolation kernel} $w_h: \Omega \to \R$ \autocite[p.~305]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012}, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)\coloneq \left(\Phi \star w_h\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

The mapping $\Phi \mapsto \sphc{\Phi}$ is called \emph{continuous interpolation}. Certainly, the interpolation kernel $w_{h}$ cannot be arbitrary. We aim to approximate the function $\Phi$ by its continuous interpolation $\sphc{\Phi}$, that is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \approx \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}'\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

To obtain a good approximation, the kernel must satisfy \autocite[p.~60]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004} the conditions written out in \Cref{tab:kernel-properties}.

\begin{table}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{Properties of the interpolation kernel $w_h$.}
	\label{tab:kernel-properties}
	\begin{tabular}{@{}cll@{}}
		\toprule
		& Property & Condition \\
		\midrule
		(K1) & positivity & $w_h \geq 0$ \\
		(K2) & monotonicity & $w_h$ is a decreasing function \\
		(K3) & symmetry & $w_h$ is radial, \ie $w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x}'\right) = w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x} - \vb{x}'}\right)$ \\
		(K4) & normalization & $\int_{\Omega}w_h\left(\vb{r}\right)\dd{\vb{r}} = 1$ \\
		(K5) & compact support/decay & $\supp w_h$ is compact or $\lim_{\abs{\vb{r}} \to \infty}w\left(\vb{r}\right) = 0$ sufficiently fast \\
		(K6) & smoothness & $w_h$ be \emph{sufficiently smooth} \\
		(K7) & concentration & $w_h \xrightharpoonup{*} \delta$ as $h \to 0^+$ \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For compact kernels, $h$ is related to the size of the support radius as $\diam \supp = 2 \kappa h,$ where $\kappa$ is kernel-dependent. If $w_h$ is not compact, \eg for a Gaussian kernel \autocite[p.~8]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
w_h\left(\vb{r}\right) = \frac{1}{\left(h \sqrt{\pi}\right)^{d}}\exp\left(-\frac{\abs{\vb{r}}^{2}}{h^{2}}\right),
\end{equations}

$h$ is the characteristic width of the Gaussian.

Common choice for the interpolation kernel are \enquote{bell shaped} functions, such as the Gaussian, B-splines \autocite{monaghanExtrapolatingSplinesInterpolation1985} and other splines. An overview of the commonly used kernels can be found in \autocite[p.~62]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004}. 

In our application, we deployed the $\Ctwo$ Wendland polynomial kernels \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}\autocite{dehnenNewKernelsSPH2012}, \ie a special class of piecewise polynomial positive compactly supported functions.\sidenote{In the class of positive definite compactly supported radial univariate polynomials of prescribed smoothness and dimension, the Wendland functions are of minimal degree and moreover are unique. \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}} In particular, the quintic Wendland kernel has been used

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}
	w_h\left(q\right) = \begin{cases} \frac{7}{4 \pi h^{2}}\left(1 - \frac{q}{2}\right)^4\left(2q + 1\right),& 0 \leq q \leq 2, \\
		0,& q > 2,
	\end{cases}, q = \frac{\abs{\vb{r}}}{h}.
\end{equations}

The graph of the Wendland kernel is plotted in \Cref{fig:KERNEL}. It is evident that for the Wendland kernel \Cref{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}, $w_h\left(\frac{\vb{r}}{h}\right) = 0 $ for $\abs{\vb{r}} \leq 2 h$, meaning $4 h = \diam \supp w_h$ and we call $h$ the \emph{smoothing length}\sidenote{As opposed to \enquote{smoothing radius}, which might be confusing.}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{kernel}
	\caption{Wendland quintic kernel, $h = 1$.}
	\label{fig:KERNEL}
\end{figure}

Under the conditions \Cref{tab:kernel-properties} on the interpolation kernel $w_h$, it can be shown\sidenote{The derivation is a straightforward application of the Taylor's expansion.} \autocite[p.~307]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:C-APPROX}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{h^{2}}.
\end{equations}

This approximation property evidently heavily influences the numerical accuracy of the method. A more involved discussion of SPH accuracy and consistency will be presented in section \Cref{sec:ACCURACY}.


\subsection{Particle approximation}
\label{sec:PARTICLES}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be a continuous medium, which we partition into $N \in \N$ particles with coordinates $\{\vb{x}_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N \subset \R^{dN}$. Denote by $V_{\alpha}$ some \emph{estimator} of the particle volume, \eg

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MASS-DENSITY}
V_{\alpha} = \frac{\rho_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

with $\rho_{\alpha}$ being the particles density and $m_{\alpha}$ its mass. The choice \Cref{eq:MASS-DENSITY} is clearly \emph{not a unique} choice of the volume estimator; the freedom of choice will be invoked in 
% TODO: dodelat referenci
	Using this particle partition, we simplify the continuous interpolation \Cref{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION} by deploying a quadrature rule. Let $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ be the position of an arbitrary particle, then we approximate

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'} \approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta}, 
\end{equations}
that is, we approximate the integration by a \emph{quadrature rule} with nodes $\{\vb{x}_{\beta}\}_{\beta=1}^N$ and weights $\{V_{\beta}\}_{\beta=1}^N$. This procedure can be thought of as introducing a \emph{discrete interpolation} operator $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \mapsto \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right)$ given as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \coloneq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right) w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta} \equiv \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta}V_{\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have adopted the notation $\Phi_{\beta} \coloneq \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right),$ $w_{\alpha \beta} \coloneq w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)$. The quantity $\Phi_{\beta}$ can be naturally interpreted as the quantity $\Phi$, \ie density, of the particle $\vb{x}_{\beta}$. It should be clarified that $\Phi_{\beta} = \Phi_{\beta}(t)$ is still a function of time.
Notice also that since the kernel $w_h$ has a compact support, in practice the sum in \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION} typically does \emph{not} consist of $N$ summands.\sidenote{Note well, if it were the case, all particles would have to be located within each other's support radius, \ie very closely. In SPH, extreme proximity gives rise to a serious instability issue known as \emph{tensile instability} \Cref{sec:INSTABILITY}.}

Having introduced the \emph(discrete) interpolation, we deploy it to approximate the \emph{continuous} interpolation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \approx \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right),
\end{equations}

and one would immediately be interested in the quality of the approximation. It turns out that an involved discrete approximation error is a complicated \emph{open} problem \autocite[p.~3]{vacondioGrandChallengesSmoothed2021a}\autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. The main difficulty lies in the fact that the error analysis depends on the particle positions \textcolor{gray}(that change in time). 
If one considers particles evenly arranged in a Cartesian grid (which is a substantial simplification), it can be shown\sidenote{Deploying techniques from Fourier analysis and the Euler-MacLaurin formula.} that for the Wendland quintic kernel it holds

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIS-ERROR}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6},
\end{equations}
with $\dd{r}$ being the typical particle size, \ie $\dd{r} \propto V^{\frac{1}{d}}$, for particles of typical volume $V$.

\subsection{Accuracy of SPH}
\label{sec:ACCURACY}

A presentation of a numerical method would not be complete without a mention of the \emph{error estimation} --- that is, what accuracy the method achieves. 

There are two sources of errors in the SPH discretisation: \begin{inparaenum} \item continuous kernel interpolation, \item discrete particle approximation \end{inparaenum}, which we analysed in \Cref{eq:C-APPROX} and \Cref{eq:DIS-ERROR} respectively. Combining these, we obtain for a sufficiently smooth scalar field $\Phi$, the quintic Wendland kernel of smoothing length $h$, and particles of size $\dd{r}$ arranged on a regular Cartesian grid,

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ERR}
\sphd{\Phi} = \Phi + \bigO{h^{2}} + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6}.
\end{equations}

This relation shows that the SPH method is \emph{at least first order}. We say \enquote{at least} because for particular combinations of kernel, geometry and particle arrangement, the observed order can be higher \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. 
Moreover, the error decreases when both $h \to 0^+$ and $\dd{r}/h \to 0^+$, equivalently $h/\dd{r} \to \infty$. This reveals an interesting relationship between the smoothing length $h$ and the particle size $\dd{r}$: to improve accuracy, both must vanish\sidenote{This matches physical intuition: as the particles shrink and their number grows, the discrete particle system approaches continuum.}, but \emph{not independently} --- $\dd{r}$ must decay faster than $h$. The precise interplay of these two parameters remains an active area of analysis \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}.


Let us summarize the presented properties and situate SPH within the broader landscape of numerical methods for continuum mechanics. Mesh-based Eulerian methods such as the finite volume method and, in particular, the discontinuous Galerkin method can be constructed to achieve arbitrarily high order of convergence, at least in \emph{smooth regions of the solution}.\sidenote{Phenomena like shocks and other contact discontinuities decrease the order of convergence.} In this comparison, SPH --- which is often merely first order in practice --- is clearly less competitive.

However, accuracy should not be the only criterion. The mesh-free nature of SPH allows us to tackle problems that are notoriously difficult for mesh schemes: free-surface flows, large deformations, plasticity, flows in complex or evolving geometries. No meshing routines are required. The method is also remarkably simple to implement.

In summary, SPH trades numerical accuracy for flexibility and implementational simplicity. The choice of method is therefore dictated by the nature of the problem under study.

\subsection{Differential operators}
\label{sec:DIFF_OPS}

This section employs the presented particle approximation framework to discretise differential operators appearing in the governing equations of fluid dynamics. Even though we have, up to this point, assumed only scalar fields, the generalization to vector and tensor fields is straightforward.

\subsubsection{Gradient of a scalar field}
\label{sec:GRADIENT}

The gradient of a differentiable scalar field $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ can be in the nature of \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION} approximated as

\begin{equations}[columns]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_{\alpha} \Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \grad_{\alpha}\left(\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)\right)V_{\beta} =\\
						    &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta} =\\
						    &=\sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} V_{\beta},\label{eq:GRAD}
\end{equations}

since $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)$ is independent of $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$,

\subsubsection{Divergence of a vector field}
\label{sec:DIVERGENCE}

The discrete divergence of a continuously differentiable vector field $\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at a particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ is defined as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\grad_{\alpha} \Big(\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)\Big)V_{\beta}\right) = \\
						     &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right) V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

\section{Classical SPH}
\label{sec:WCSPH}

One of the pillars of SPH's success lies in its flexibility. Not only can SPH be used to discretise a broad range of PDEs, but the \emph{same} system can often be discretised in several different ways, giving the freedom to choose a problem-specific formulation.\sidenote{Typically, one devises \emph{conservative} formulations for problems with conserved quantities.}

In this and the following sections, we will focus on different ways to discretise the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}.


\subsection{Improved discretisation}
\label{sec:IMPROVED-DIFF-OPS}

In the previous, we have derived discrete versions of the $\gradop$ and $\div$ operators 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta},\label{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} \\
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}. \label{eq:NAIVE-DIV}
\end{equations}

However, this is not a unique way to discretise the differential operators --- and particularly \emph{not a good one} either. To demonstrate, take the constant fields $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = 1, \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \vb{e}_x,$ then

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}, \\
	\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{e}_x \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

The naive discretisation \Cref{eq:NAIVE-GRAD}, \Cref{eq:NAIVE-DIV} fails to approximate constant fields. To refine the approach, we draw inspiration \autocite[p.~10]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} from the identities

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:IDENTITIES}
\grad \Phi &= \phi \grad\left(\frac{\Phi}{\phi}\right) + \frac{\Phi}{\phi}\grad \phi, \\
\divergence{\vb{F}} &= \frac{1}{\phi}\divergence{\left(\phi \vb{F}\right)} - \frac{1}{\phi}\vb{F} \cdot \grad \phi,
\end{equations}

valid for a nonzero continuously differentiable function $\phi$.\sidenote{In what follows, $\phi$ will be the density $\rho$.} We can improve our approximation by discretising in the following manner

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi_{\alpha} &= \phi_{\alpha} \grad_{\alpha} \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}}\right) + \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}} \grad_{\alpha}\left(\phi_{\alpha}\right) \approx \\
		       &\approx \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} + \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} =\\
		       &= \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}} \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} + \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}}\phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \\
	       &= \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\phi_{\beta} V_{\beta},
\end{equations}

and 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}_{\alpha} &= \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \grad_{\alpha} \cdot \left(\phi_{\alpha} \vb{F}_{\alpha}\right) - \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \vb{F}_{\alpha} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} \phi_{\alpha} \approx \\
                       &\approx \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} - \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \vb{F}_{\alpha} \cdot \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} =\\
		       &= \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha}\right)  \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \phi_{\beta} V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

Thanks to the $\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha}$ term in the approximate divergence operator, we see that the discretised divergence operator correctly vanishes for constant fields. The alternate discretised gradient operator does \emph{not vanish} for constant fields, however, it possesses a symmetric property in the summands, which will prove useful in what follows.

All in all, the discretisation

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\phi_{\beta}\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\phi_{\beta}V_{\beta}, \label{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD} \\
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}}\sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha} \right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \phi_{\beta} V_{\beta}, \label{eq:IMPROVED-DIV}
\end{equations}

will be preferred.

\subsection{Discrete balance of mass}
\label{sec:MASS}
Using the improved divergence operators \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-DIV} with the auxiliary field $\phi$ taken as the density $\rho$ the balance of mass

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\rho} = - \rho\left(\divergence{\vb{v}}\right),
\end{equations}

can be rewritten as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \frac{\rho_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{v}_{\beta} - \vb{v}_{\alpha}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \rho_{\beta} V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right)\cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used \emph{a possible definition} of the particle's density $\rho_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha}}$. The discrete balance of mass

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DISCR-MASS}
\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right)\cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

is thus a system of $N$ ordinary differential equations, whose solution yields the densities $\rho_{\alpha}$. 

This is not the only way we can obtain the densities. An \emph{alternative} formulation defines the density directly as a kernel sum: notice that the properties of the interpolation kernel $w_h$ \Cref{tab:kernel-properties} allow us to interpret the sum

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-density-sum}
\rho_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

as a \emph{weighted average} of the particle masses.\sidenote{The closer the particles are to the particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$, the larger the weight.} With this approach, we replace the need to \textcolor{gray}{numerically} solve the ODEs \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS} by a summation procedure.\sidenote{Recall again that in practice, sums including an interpolation kernel \emph{do not} have $N$ nonzero summands, as $w_h$ is compactly supported.} This approach becomes particularly useful in formulations where the density field is not the primary quantity of interest; for diagnostic purposes, one can recover the densities at an arbitrary integration step with a single sum rather than solving for the densities all along. Moreover, \autocite{kinclGloballyTimereversibleFluid2023a} has shown that, with a suitable integrator, numerical simulations of isentropic flows in this formulation are \emph{time-reversible}.

Note that the two presented formulations are \emph{not equivalent} --- in general, they result in different densities.

\subsection{Discrete balance of linear momentum}
\label{sec:MOMENTUM}

Let us consider the \emph{improved} discrete versions of the gradient \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD} operator  and choose again the auxiliary field $\phi$ as the density $\rho$. Then the balance of momentum

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho}\grad p + \vb{g}, 
\end{equations}

can be recast to the form

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{\vb{v_{\alpha}}}{t} &= -\frac{1}{\rho_{\alpha}} \rho_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}}^{2}\right)\rho_{\beta} V_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}}^{2} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}}^{2}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used the fact $\rho_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha}}$. Since $\rho_{\alpha} = \rho_{\alpha}(t)$ is a function of time only, the material time derivative in fact becomes an \emph{ordinary} time derivative.\sidenote{This is a direct consequence of the Lagrangian nature of SPH. The time evolution of a particle's quantity $\Phi_{\alpha}(t)$ is essentially the time evolution of the quantity $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right)$ at a fixed \emph{material} point $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$.}

Notice the structure of the discretised balance of linear momentum. If we denote

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} &\coloneq - m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}, \\
	\vb{F}_{\alpha} &\coloneq m_{\alpha} \vb{g},
\end{equations}

then we can write

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - m_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} + \vb{g} = \\
			     &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} + \vb{g} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} + \vb{F}_{\alpha}.
\end{equations}

We see that $\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha}$ is the \emph{internal} pressure force through which the particle $\beta$ acts on the particle $\alpha$ and $\vb{F}_{\alpha}$ is the gravity force acting on the particle $\alpha$, \ie an \emph{external} force acting on the particle $\alpha$. Moreover, observe that it holds

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} = - m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} = m_{\beta} m_{\alpha}  \left(\frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}+ \frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\beta} w_{\beta\alpha} = - \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used $w_{\alpha\beta} = w_{\beta\alpha}, \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} = - \grad_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}$, both due to the radial symmetry of $w_h$.\sidenote{Recall that $w_{\alpha\beta} = w_h\left(\abs{\vb{r}_{\alpha} - \vb{r}_{\beta}}\right)$.} Therefore,

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} = - \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta},
\end{equations}

and we have recovered \emph{Newton's third law} at the \emph{discrete level.} In an isolated system, \ie in the absence of external forces, Newton's third law implies the \emph{conservation of total linear momentum.}\sidenote{The total momentum of the isolated system reads as \begin{equations}[single,*]
\sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha},
\end{equations}
and so
\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{t} \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} =\\
	&=\sum_{\alpha=1}^N \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} =  \sum_{\beta=1}^N \sum_{\alpha=1}^N \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta}\\
	&=- \sum_{\alpha=1}^N \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha},
\end{equations}
where we used Newton's second law in the absence of external forces. In total, we thus see it must hold
\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\dv{t} \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha} \equiv \vb{0},
\end{equations}
therefore, the total momentum is conserved.} This conservation property would \emph{not hold} had we used the naive discrete gradient \Cref{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} instead of the improved form \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD}.

\subsection{Complete system}
\label{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}

To close the discretised equations, we require an equation of state for the pressure $p = p\left(\rho, T\right)$. Consistent with our application in atmospheric dynamics, we deploy the thermal equation of a \textcolor{gray}{calorically} ideal gas \Cref{eq:THERMAL}. Also, since we are interested in \emph{isentropic} flows, we add the equation $\tdot{\theta_{\alpha}} = 0$ for the evolution of the potential temperature. Our complete system hence reads as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}}\right) + \vb{g}, \\
p_{\alpha}\left(\rho_{\alpha}, T_{\alpha}\right) &= R \rho_{\alpha} T_{\alpha}, \\
\theta_{\alpha} &= \, \text{const},
\end{equations}

together with an equation for density \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

or \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\rho_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}.
\end{equations}

In the above equations, the index $\alpha \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ runs over all particles.

\section{Hamiltonian formulation}
\label{sec:HAMILTON}

The conservation property we discovered in the previous section aligns with our intuition about the correspondence between SPH and the classical mechanics of point particles. Classical mechanics can be elegantly reframed in the language of \emph{Hamiltonian mechanics}, and we now develop the Hamiltonian formulation of SPH. This reformulation naturally motivates the use of a \emph{symplectic integrator} in the numerical solution of the system of ODEs obtained by the SPH discretisation.

\subsection{Poisson brackets}
\label{sec:BRACKETS}

Let us begin with a general description of continuum mechanics in the framework of Hamiltonian mechanics. We denote by $\vb{X}$ the elements of the Lagrangian, \ie reference, configuration manifold and by $\vb{x}$ the elements of the Eulerian, \ie current, configuration manifold. As usual, the deformation (motion) of the continuum is given as a $C^1$ diffeomorphism $\pmb{\chi}: \left(t, \vb{X}\right) \mapsto \vb{x}$, \ie $\vb{x} = \pmb{\chi}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ from the Lagrangian to the Eulerian configuration. Denote further $\vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ the Eulerian momentum density per Lagrangian volume.. The mappings $\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ will be called the \emph{Lagrangian state variables}.

The continuum counterpart to the canonical Poisson bracket of Hamiltonian mechanics of points is the \emph{canonical} Poisson bracket in the Lagrangian frame; given two arbitrary Fréchet differentiable functionals $F\left(t, \vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\right)$, $G\left(t, \vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\right)$ of the Lagrangian state variables $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$, we define \autocite[p.~2]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}
\pbr[L]{F}{G} \coloneq \bigint\left(\fdv{F}{x^i\left(\vb{X}\right)} \fdv{G}{M_i\left(\vb{X}\right)} - \fdv{F}{M_i(\vb{X})}\fdv{G}{x^i}\left(\vb{X}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

The derivatives appearing in \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET} are functional derivatives, \ie distributions representing the Fréchet differential. For a concise introduction, see \eg the appendix of \autocite{pavelkaMultiscaleThermoDynamicsIntroduction2018}.

In what follows, we will for brevity of notation omit the dependence of $\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ on $\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ and simply write $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$. One can check that the mapping $\left(F, G\right) \mapsto \pbr[L]{F}{G}$ is in fact a skew-symmetric bilinear form that satisfies the Jacobi identity and Leibniz rule.\sidenote{A bilinear form is skew symmetric provided
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F}{G} = - \pbr[L]{G}{F},
	\end{equations}
	Jacobi identity is the property
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F}{\pbr[L]{G}{H}} + \textrm{cycl.} = 0,
	\end{equations}
	and Leibniz rule holds when
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{FG}{H} = F\pbr[L]{G}{H} + \pbr[L]{F}{H}G.
	\end{equations}
}

Given an Fréchet differentiable functional $F = F\left(t, \vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$, its evolution in time is given by \autocite[p.~2]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:TIME-EVOL}
\tdot{F} = \partial_t F + \pbr[L]{F}{\totenL},
\end{equations}

where $\totenL$ is the functional of total energy expressed using the Lagrangian state variables $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$.\sidenote{To be precise, $E$ is the Hamilton's functional, \ie the Hamiltonian.} In particular, for a functional $F\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$ that explicitly does not depend on time, we can calculate using the chain rule

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{F}\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right) = \bigint\left(\fdv{F}{x^i} \partial_t x^i + \fdv{F}{M_i}\partial_t M_i\right)\dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}

on the other hand \Cref{eq:TIME-EVOL} dictates

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{F}\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right) = \bigint\left(\fdv{F}{x^i} \fdv{\totenL}{M_i} - \fdv{F}{M_i} \fdv{\totenL}{x^i}\right)\dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}

since $F$ were arbitrary, we conclude \autocite[p.~3]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020}that it must hold

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\partial_t x^i\left(t, \vb{X}\right) &= \fdv{\totenL}{M_i\left(t, \vb{X}\right)},\label{eq:HAMILTON-POSITION}\\
\partial_t M_i\left(t, \vb{X}\right) &= -\fdv{\totenL}{x^i\left(t, \vb{X}\right)}\label{eq:HAMILTON-MOMENTA}.
\end{equations}

The equations \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-POSITION}, \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-MOMENTA} are the Hamilton \emph{canonical} equations of continuum mechanics. The right-hand sides of the equations require the knowledge of the total energy of the continuum expressed using the Lagrangian state variables.

In general, the energy has a contribution from the kinetic energy $\abs{\vb{M}}^{2}/2\rhoL$ and the internal energy $\eL$, \ie so on a general level we can write

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY}
\totenL = \bigint\left(\frac{\abs{M}^{2}}{2\rhoL} + \eL\left(\vb{X}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

We briefly summarize the structure of what follows. We have noticed that for the description of the evolution of a functional of the Lagrangian state variables, we require \begin{inparaenum} \item the Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}, \item the total energy functional \Cref{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY}. \end{inparaenum} To obtain the time evolution of a functional of the \emph{SPH state variables}, we require \begin{inparaenum} \item a SPH Poisson bracket, \item the SPH energy \end{inparaenum}. The SPH bracket and energy are obtained by reduction and approximation of their continuum counterparts respectively.

\subsection{SPH discretisation}
\label{sec:SPH-discretisation}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be the region occupied by the continuum. Denote $\{\Omega_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N, N \in \N$ a pairwise disjoint \textcolor{gray}{finite} partition of $\Omega$, \ie $\Omega = \bigcup_{\alpha=1}^N \Omega_{\beta}$, $\Omega_{\alpha} \cap \Omega_{\beta} = \emptyset$ for $\alpha \neq \beta$. The sets $\Omega_{\alpha}$ will represent the \emph{SPH particles.} Moreover, with the aid of the normalized characteristic function of $\Omega_{\alpha}$,

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right) = \begin{cases}
	\frac{1}{V_{\alpha}}, \vb{X} &\in \Omega_{\alpha},\\
  0,  \vb{X} &\notin \Omega_{\alpha}
\end{cases}, V_{\alpha} = \int_{\Omega_{\alpha}} \dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}
we can define the \emph{SPH position} $\vb{x}_{\alpha}\left(t\right)$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-position}
\vb{x}_{\alpha}(t) = \int_{\Omega}\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right) \chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right)\dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}

and the \emph{SPH momentum} $\vb{M}_{\alpha}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-momentum}
\vb{M}_{\alpha}\left(t\right) = \int_{\Omega}\vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

Naturally, the SPH positions and momenta $\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ will replace the state variables $\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$ of the continuum mechanics, and so we will refer to them as the SPH state variables.

\subsubsection{SPH bracket}
\label{sec:SPH-bracket}

Let $F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right), G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ be two arbitrary \textcolor{gray}{sufficiently regular} functionals of the SPH state variables. From the definition of the SPH state variables \Cref{eq:SPH-position}, \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum} it follows that $F, G$ in fact also depend on the state variables of the continuum mechanics ---hence we are allowed to plug them into the Lagrangian Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}. The calculation reads as

\begin{equations}[lines]
\label{eq:TERM}
\pbr[L]{F}{G} = \int\left(\fdv{F}{x^i}\fdv{G}{M_i} - \fdv{F}{M_i} \fdv{G}{x^i}\right)\dd{\vb{X}} = \\ = \sum_{\alpha,\beta}\int\left(\left(\pdv{F}{x_{\alpha}^j} \fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{x^i} + \pdv{F}{M_{\beta,j}}\fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{x^i}\right)\left(\pdv{G}{M_{\beta,j}}\fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{M_i} + \pdv{G}{x^j_{\alpha}}\fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{M_i}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}} + \\ - \sum_{\alpha,\beta} \int\left(\left(\pdv{F}{M_{\beta,j}} \fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{M_i} + \pdv{F}{x_{\alpha}^j} \fdv{x_{\alpha}^j}{M_i}\right)\left(\pdv{G}{x^j_{\alpha}} \fdv{x_{\alpha}^j}{x^i} + \pdv{G}{M_{\beta,j}} \fdv{M_{\beta, j}}{x_i}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

Our goal is to simplify \Cref{eq:TERM} and obtain an expression depending only on the SPH state variables $\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}$. The procedure requires the evaluation of the functional derivatives of $x^j_{\alpha}, M_{\beta,j}$ w.r.t $M_i, x^i$, which is a straightforward computation using the definitions \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum}, \Cref{eq:SPH-position}, but rather a lengthy one and such it will be skipped. It can however be found in

The expression \Cref{eq:TERM} can be rewritten to obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)}{G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)} = \sum_{\alpha}\left(\pdv{F}{x^{i}_{\alpha}}\pdv{G}{M_{\alpha,i}} - \pdv{F}{M_{\alpha,i}}\pdv{G}{x^i_{\alpha}}\right).
\end{equations}

It is hence natural to define the \emph{SPH bracket} for any two functionals $F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right), G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ of the SPH state variables through.

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-bracket}
\pbr[SPH]{F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)}{G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)} \coloneq \sum_{\alpha}\left(\pdv{F}{x^i_{\alpha}}\pdv{G}{M_{\alpha,i}} - \pdv{F}{M_{\alpha,i}}\pdv{G}{x^i_{\alpha}}\right),
\end{equations}

\subsubsection{Pressure-entropy}
\label{sec:HOPKINS_PE}
Recall that the entropy of an isentropic flow of an ideal gas has the form\sidenote{This is a test side note.}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\eta = \eta_0 + c_v \log\left(\frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right)
\end{equations}
and $\eta = \, \text{const} \,.$ We see that the quantity $A$ defined as \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
A = \frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}},
\end{equations}
is in fact equal to

\begin{equations}[single,*]
A = \exp\left(\frac{\eta - \eta_0}{c_v}\right),
\end{equations}
meaning that the flow is \emph{isentropic,} if and only if the quantity $A$ is \emph{conserved} under the flow. Thus $A$ is an \emph{entropy-like} variable: conserved under isentropic flows.

The choice

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:x_PE}
	x_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} A_{\alpha}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}
gives the pressure $P_{\alpha}$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:P_PE}
	P_{\alpha} = \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} A_{\beta}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha})\right)^{\gamma}
\end{equations}
and the evolution equations as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:v_PE}
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(A_{\alpha} A_{\beta}\right)^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha}P_{\alpha}}{P_{\alpha}^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta}P_{\beta}}{P^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}_{\beta}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right),
\end{equations}
with

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\label{eq:f_PE}
	f_{\alpha} =  \left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 n_{\alpha}} \pdv{n_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}
\end{equations}

\subsubsection{Pressure-potential temperature}
\label{sec:HOPKINS_theta}


Recall the \emph{potential temperature} $\theta$

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T \left(\frac{P_0}{P}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}},
\end{equations}

where $T$ is the gas's \textcolor{gray}{thermodynamic} temperature, and $P_0$ is the reference pressure. \sidenote{Potential temperature is the thermodynamic temperature the air parcel would possess if \emph{adiabatically} moved from the thermodynamic state $\left(T, P\right)$ to the state $\left(\theta, P_0\right)$.} Using the thermal state equation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
P = R \rho T,
\end{equations}
together with the fact 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{R}{c_p} = \frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma},
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = \frac{P}{\rho R} P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}} P^{\frac{1 - \gamma}{\gamma}} = \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{P^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}}{\rho}\right) = \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right)^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} = \frac{P_{0}^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R} A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

so setting 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\tilde{\theta}_0 \coloneq \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single, !,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:theta_A}
	\theta = \tilde{\theta}_0 A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}.
\end{equations}

This implies the potential temperature $\theta$ is also \emph{an entropy-like} variable - it is \emph{conserved} under isentropic flows.

Rewriting \ref{eq:x_PE} using \ref{eq:theta_A} we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:x_Ptheta}
x_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{\tilde{\theta}_0} \theta_{\alpha}.
\end{equations}

Using this quantity the pressure \ref{eq:P_PE} can then be expressed as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:P_Ptheta}
P_{\alpha} = \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{m_{\beta}}{\tilde{\theta_0}} \theta_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)\right)^{\gamma}.
\end{equations}

From \ref{eq:v_PE} we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:v_Ptheta}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \frac{\theta_{\alpha} \theta_{\beta}}{\tilde{\theta}_0^{2}}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha}P_{\alpha}}{P_{\alpha}^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta}P_{\beta}}{P_{\beta}^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right),
\end{equations}

with \ref{eq:f_PE} unchanged
\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:f_Ptheta}
	f_{\alpha} =  \left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 n_{\alpha}} \pdv{n_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equations}
