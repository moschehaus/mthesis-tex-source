\chapter{Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics}
\label{chap:SPH}

This chapter presents the main numerical method used --- the \emph{Smoothed Particle Method}. 

\section{Method overview}
\label{sec:SPH_OVERVIEW}

Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is a general purpose numerical method first used in astrophysical stellar models \autocite{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}. The authors sought to overcome a grid-resolution problem arising with their use of the finite difference method (FDM).\sidenote{They argued that the treatment of highly distorted continuum would require an extremely fine grid, hence substantial computational cost \autocite[p.~1]{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}.} The method has found use in a variety of fields, \viz free-surface flows \autocite{monaghanSimulatingFreeSurface1994a}, solid mechanics \autocite{liberskySmoothParticleHydrodynamics1991}, geomechanics \autocite{buiLagrangianMeshfreeParticles2008}, and is still successfully used in astrophysics \autocite{springelSimulationsFormationEvolution2005}. 

SPH is a particle (Lagrangian) method.\sidenote{In this sense grid/mesh methods such as the \begin{inparaenum} \item finite difference method; \item finite element method; \item finite volume method; \item discontinuous Galerkin method, \end{inparaenum} are called \emph{Eulerian}, since they approximate the evolution in the \emph{current, deformed} configuration. Particle methods like \begin{inparaenum} \item SPH; \item MPS (moving particle semi-implicit method) \autocite{koshizukaMovingParticleSemiImplicitMethod1996}; \item DPD (dissipative particle dynamics) \autocite{hoogerbruggeSimulatingMicroscopicHydrodynamic1992} \end{inparaenum} trace the evolution in the \emph{reference} configuration, and so particle methods are also called \emph{Lagrangian}.}   The continuum is partitioned into arbitrary \emph{material points}, in this context referred to as \emph{particles}, and basic physical properties such as density, velocity, entropy, and energy are assigned to them. Their mutual interaction is then prescribed,\sidenote{The interaction cannot be arbitrary. To capture the physics correctly, the interaction must in fact be rooted in the discretisation of the governing equations.} and the state of each particle is advanced in time. 
We stress that \emph{SPH particles} are \emph{not} \textcolor{gray}{elementary} particles in the microscopic sense --- they are typically macroscopic fluid elements. Nonetheless, some intuition from the mechanics of point particles carries over naturally to SPH, as we shall see.

In what follows, we introduce the theoretical foundations of SPH and the specific formulation developed in this work.

\section{Cornerstones of SPH}
\label{sec:CORNERSTONES}

In this section, we outline the standard approach to SPH discretisation.

\subsection{Kernel interpolation}
\label{sec:INTERPOLATION}

% TODO: DROP the _H totally

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be the continuous medium and $\Phi: \Rplusnought \times \Omega \to \R$ an arbitrary scalar valued field, \eg density. Suppose further that $\forall t \in \Rplusnought$ the mapping $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi(t, \vb{x})$ is in $\Loneloc{\Omega}$, then

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY}
\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \left(\Phi \star \delta\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)\delta\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x'}},
\end{equations}

holds for almost all $\vb{x} \in \Omega$. A common assumption in the SPH literature is to assume $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \in \Czero{\Omega}$, and so the equality \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} holds pointwise in $\Omega$. To make the presentation simpler, throughout we always assume sufficient regularity of all quantities involved and do not pursue measure-theoretic rigor.

The main point of departure of the SPH method is to replace the Dirac delta distribution in \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} by an \emph{interpolation kernel} $w_h: \Omega \to \R$ \autocite[p.~305]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012}, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)\coloneq \left(\Phi \star w_h\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

The mapping $\Phi \mapsto \sphc{\Phi}$ is called \emph{continuous interpolation}. Certainly, the interpolation kernel $w_{h}$ cannot be arbitrary. We aim to approximate the function $\Phi$ by its continuous interpolation $\sphc{\Phi}$, that is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \approx \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}'\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

To obtain a good approximation, the kernel must satisfy \autocite[p.~60]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004} the conditions written out in \Cref{tab:kernel-properties}.

\begin{table}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{Properties of the interpolation kernel $w_h$.}
	\label{tab:kernel-properties}
	\begin{tabular}{@{}cll@{}}
		\toprule
		& Property & Condition \\
		\midrule
		(K1) & positivity & $w_h \geq 0$ \\
		(K2) & monotonicity & $w_h$ is a decreasing function \\
		(K3) & symmetry & $w_h$ is radial, \ie $w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x}'\right) = w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x} - \vb{x}'}\right)$ \\
		(K4) & normalization & $\int_{\Omega}w_h\left(\vb{r}\right)\dd{\vb{r}} = 1$ \\
		(K5) & compact support/decay & $\supp w_h$ is compact or $\lim_{\abs{\vb{r}} \to \infty}w_h\left(\vb{r}\right) = 0$ sufficiently fast \\
		(K6) & smoothness & $w_h$ is \emph{sufficiently smooth} \\
		(K7) & concentration & $w_h \xrightharpoonup{*} \delta$ as $h \to 0^+$ \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The parameter $h$ entering the kernel properties controls its width. For compact kernels, $h$ is related to the size of the support radius as $\diam \supp = 2 \kappa h,$ where $\kappa$ is kernel-dependent. If $w_h$ is not compact, \eg for a Gaussian kernel \autocite[p.~8]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
w_h\left(\vb{r}\right) = \frac{1}{\left(h \sqrt{\pi}\right)^{d}}\exp\left(-\frac{\abs{\vb{r}}^{2}}{h^{2}}\right),
\end{equations}

$h$ is the characteristic width of the Gaussian.

Common choices for the interpolation kernel are \enquote{bell shaped} functions, such as the Gaussian, B-splines \autocite{monaghanExtrapolatingSplinesInterpolation1985} and other splines. An overview of the commonly used kernels can be found in \autocite[p.~62]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004}. 

In our application, we used the $\Ctwo$ Wendland polynomial kernels \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}\autocite{dehnenNewKernelsSPH2012}, \ie a special class of piecewise polynomial positive compactly supported functions.\sidenote{In the class of positive definite compactly supported radial univariate polynomials of prescribed smoothness and dimension, the Wendland functions are of minimal degree and moreover are unique. \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}} Specifically, we used the quintic Wendland kernel 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}
	w_h\left(q\right) = \begin{cases} \frac{7}{4 \pi h^{2}}\left(1 - \frac{q}{2}\right)^4\left(2q + 1\right),& 0 \leq q \leq 2, \\
		0,& q > 2,
	\end{cases}, q = \frac{\abs{\vb{r}}}{h}.
\end{equations}

The graph of the Wendland kernel is plotted in \Cref{fig:KERNEL}. It is evident that for the Wendland kernel \Cref{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}, $w_h\left(\frac{\vb{r}}{h}\right) = 0 $ for $\abs{\vb{r}} \geq 2 h$, meaning $4 h = \diam \supp w_h$, \ie $\kappa = 2 $ for this kernel and we call $h$ the \emph{smoothing length}.\sidenote{As opposed to \enquote{smoothing radius}, which might be confused with the support radius $2 h$.}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{kernel}
	\caption{Wendland quintic kernel, $h = 1$.}
	\label{fig:KERNEL}
\end{figure}

Under the conditions \Cref{tab:kernel-properties} on the interpolation kernel $w_h$, it can be shown\sidenote{The derivation is a straightforward application of Taylor's expansion.} \autocite[p.~307]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:C-APPROX}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{h^{2}}.
\end{equations}

This approximation property evidently heavily influences the numerical accuracy of the method. A more involved discussion of SPH accuracy and consistency will be presented in \Cref{sec:ACCURACY}.


\subsection{Particle approximation}
\label{sec:PARTICLES}

Let $\Omega_{0} \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be the \emph{reference configuration} of a continuous medium, which we partition into $N \in \N$ particles with coordinates $\{\vb{x}_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N \subset \R^{d}$. Denote by $V_{\alpha}$ some \emph{approximation} of the particle volume, \eg

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MASS-DENSITY}
V_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

with $\rho_{\alpha}$ being the particle's density and $m_{\alpha}$ its mass. The choice \Cref{eq:MASS-DENSITY} is clearly \emph{not a unique} choice of the volume estimator; the freedom of choice will be invoked in \Cref{sec:HOPKINS}
	Using this particle partition, we simplify the continuous interpolation \Cref{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION} by applying a quadrature rule. Let $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ be the position of an arbitrary particle, then we approximate

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'} \approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta}, 
\end{equations}
that is, we approximate the integration by a \emph{quadrature rule} with nodes $\{\vb{x}_{\beta}\}_{\beta=1}^N$ and weights $\{V_{\beta}\}_{\beta=1}^N$. This procedure can be thought of as introducing a \emph{discrete interpolation} operator $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \mapsto \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right)$ given as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \coloneq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right) w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta} \equiv \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta}V_{\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have adopted the notation $\Phi_{\beta} \coloneq \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right),$ $w_{\alpha \beta} \coloneq w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)$. The quantity $\Phi_{\beta}$ can be naturally interpreted as the quantity $\Phi$, \ie density, of the particle $\vb{x}_{\beta}$. Note that $\Phi_{\beta} = \Phi_{\beta}(t)$ remains a function of time.
Notice also that since the kernel $w_h$ has a compact support, in practice the sum in \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION} typically does \emph{not} consist of $N$ summands.

Having introduced the \emph{discrete} interpolation, we deploy it to approximate the \emph{continuous} interpolation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) \approx \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right),
\end{equations}

and one would immediately be interested in the quality of the approximation. A rigorous analysis of the discrete approximation error is a complicated \emph{open} problem \autocite[p.~3]{vacondioGrandChallengesSmoothed2021a}\autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. The main difficulty lies in the fact that the error analysis depends on the particle positions \textcolor{gray}{that change in time}. 
If one considers particles evenly arranged in a Cartesian grid (which is a substantial simplification), it can be shown\sidenote{Deploying techniques from Fourier analysis and the Euler-MacLaurin formula.} that for the Wendland quintic kernel it holds
% WARNING: MISSING CITATION

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIS-ERROR}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6},
\end{equations}
with $\dd{r}$ being the typical particle size, \ie $\dd{r} \propto V^{\frac{1}{d}}$, for particles of typical volume $V$.

\subsection{Accuracy of SPH}
\label{sec:ACCURACY}

A presentation of a numerical method would not be complete without a mention of the \emph{error estimation} --- that is, what accuracy the method achieves. 

There are two sources of errors in the SPH discretisation: \begin{inparaenum} \item continuous kernel interpolation, \item discrete particle approximation \end{inparaenum}, which we analysed in \Cref{eq:C-APPROX} and \Cref{eq:DIS-ERROR} respectively. Combining these, we obtain for a sufficiently smooth scalar field $\Phi$, the quintic Wendland kernel of smoothing length $h$, and particles of size $\dd{r}$ arranged on a regular Cartesian grid,

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ERR}
\sphd{\Phi} = \Phi + \bigO{h^{2}} + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6}.
\end{equations}

This relation shows that the SPH method is \emph{at least first order}. We say \enquote{at least} because for particular combinations of kernel, geometry and particle arrangement, the observed order can be higher \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. 
Moreover, the error decreases when both $h \to 0^+$ and $\dd{r}/h \to 0^+$, equivalently $h/\dd{r} \to \infty$. This reveals an interesting relationship between the smoothing length $h$ and the particle size $\dd{r}$: to improve accuracy, both must vanish\sidenote{This matches physical intuition: as the particles shrink and their number grows, the discrete particle system approaches the continuum.}, but \emph{not independently} --- $\dd{r}$ must decay faster than $h$. The precise interplay of these two parameters remains an active area of analysis \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}.


Let us summarise the presented properties and situate SPH within the broader landscape of numerical methods for continuum mechanics. Mesh-based Eulerian methods such as the finite volume method and, in particular, the discontinuous Galerkin method can be constructed to achieve arbitrarily high order of convergence, at least in \emph{smooth regions of the solution}.\sidenote{Phenomena like shocks and other contact discontinuities decrease the order of convergence.} In this comparison, SPH --- which is often merely first order in practice --- is clearly less competitive.

However, accuracy should not be the only criterion. The mesh-free nature of SPH allows us to tackle problems that are notoriously difficult for mesh schemes: free-surface flows, large deformations, plasticity, flows in complex or evolving geometries. No meshing routines are required. The method is also remarkably simple to implement.

In summary, SPH trades numerical accuracy for flexibility and implementational simplicity. The choice of method is therefore dictated by the nature of the problem under study.

\subsection{Differential operators}
\label{sec:DIFF_OPS}

This section uses the particle approximation framework to discretise differential operators appearing in the governing equations of fluid dynamics. Even though we have, up to this point, assumed only scalar fields, the generalization to vector and tensor fields is straightforward.

\subsubsection{Gradient of a scalar field}
\label{sec:GRADIENT}

The gradient of a differentiable scalar field $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ can be approximated, in the spirit of \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION}, as

\begin{equations}[columns]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_{\alpha} \Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \grad_{\alpha}\left(\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)\right)V_{\beta} =\\
						    &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)V_{\beta} =\\
						    &=\sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} V_{\beta},\label{eq:GRAD}
\end{equations}

since $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)$ is independent of $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$.

\subsubsection{Divergence of a vector field}
\label{sec:DIVERGENCE}

The discrete divergence of a continuously differentiable vector field $\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at a particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ is defined as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \Big(\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)\Big)V_{\beta}\right) = \\
						     &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\beta}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right) V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

\section{Classical SPH}
\label{sec:WCSPH}

One of the pillars of SPH's success lies in its flexibility. Not only can SPH be used to discretise a broad range of PDEs, but the \emph{same} system can often be discretised in several different ways, giving the freedom to choose a problem-specific formulation.\sidenote{Typically, one devises \emph{conservative} formulations for problems with conserved quantities.}

In this and the following sections, we will focus on different ways to discretise the Euler system \Cref{eq:GOVERNING-SYSTEM}.


\subsection{Improved discretisation}
\label{sec:IMPROVED-DIFF-OPS}

Previously, we have derived discrete versions of the $\gradop$ and $\div$ operators 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta},\label{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} \\
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}. \label{eq:NAIVE-DIV}
\end{equations}

However, this is not a unique way to discretise the differential operators --- and particularly \emph{not a good one} either. To demonstrate, take constant fields, for instance $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = 1, \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \vb{e}_x,$ then

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \Phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}, \\
	\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{e}_x \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

The naive discretisations \Cref{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} and \Cref{eq:NAIVE-DIV} fails to approximate constant fields. To refine the approach, we draw inspiration \autocite[p.~10]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} from the identities

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:IDENTITIES}
\grad \Phi &= \phi \grad\left(\frac{\Phi}{\phi}\right) + \frac{\Phi}{\phi}\grad \phi, \\
\divergence{\vb{F}} &= \frac{1}{\phi}\divergence{\left(\phi \vb{F}\right)} - \frac{1}{\phi}\vb{F} \cdot \grad \phi,
\end{equations}

valid for a continuously differentiable non-vanishing function $\phi$.\sidenote{In what follows, $\phi$ will be the density $\rho$.} We can improve our approximation by discretising in the following manner

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_{\alpha} \Phi_{\alpha} &= \phi_{\alpha} \grad_{\alpha} \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}}\right) + \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}} \grad_{\alpha}\left(\phi_{\alpha}\right) \approx \\
		       &\approx \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} + \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} =\\
		       &= \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}} \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} + \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}}\phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}V_{\beta} = \\
	       &= \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\phi_{\beta} V_{\beta},
\end{equations}

and 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}_{\alpha} &= \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \grad_{\alpha} \cdot \left(\phi_{\alpha} \vb{F}_{\alpha}\right) - \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \vb{F}_{\alpha} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} \phi_{\alpha} \approx \\
                       &\approx \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \vb{F}_{\beta} \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} - \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \vb{F}_{\alpha} \cdot \sum_{\beta=1}^N \phi_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} V_{\beta} =\\
		       &= \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha}\right)  \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \phi_{\beta} V_{\beta}.
\end{equations}

Thanks to the $\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha}$ term in the approximate divergence operator, we see that the discretised divergence operator correctly vanishes for constant fields. The alternate discretised gradient operator does \emph{not vanish} for constant fields, however, summands are symmetric in the $\alpha, \beta$ indices, which will prove crucial for momentum conservation.

All in all, the discretisation

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad_{\alpha} \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \phi_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\frac{\Phi_{\alpha}}{\phi_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{\Phi_{\beta}}{\phi_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\phi_{\beta}V_{\beta}, \label{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD} \\
\grad_{\alpha} \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right) &\approx \frac{1}{\phi_{\alpha}}\sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_{\beta} - \vb{F}_{\alpha} \right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \phi_{\beta} V_{\beta}, \label{eq:IMPROVED-DIV}
\end{equations}

will be preferred.

\subsection{Discrete balance of mass}
\label{sec:MASS}
Using the improved divergence operators \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-DIV} with the auxiliary field $\phi$ taken as the density $\rho$ the balance of mass

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\rho} = - \rho\left(\divergence{\vb{v}}\right),
\end{equations}

can be rewritten as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \frac{\rho_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{v}_{\beta} - \vb{v}_{\alpha}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} \rho_{\beta} V_{\beta} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right)\cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used \emph{a possible definition} of the particle's density $\rho_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha}}$. Since $\rho_{\alpha} = \rho_{\alpha}(t)$ is a function of time only, the material time derivative in fact becomes an \emph{ordinary} time derivative.\sidenote{This is a direct consequence of the Lagrangian nature of SPH. The time evolution of a particle's quantity $\Phi_{\alpha}(t)$ is essentially the time evolution of the quantity $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_{\alpha}\right)$ at a fixed \emph{material} point $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$.} The discrete balance of mass

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DISCR-MASS}
\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right)\cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

is thus a system of $N$ ordinary differential equations, whose solution yields the densities $\rho_{\alpha}$. 

This is not the only way we can obtain the densities. An \emph{alternative} formulation defines the density directly as a kernel sum: notice that the properties of the interpolation kernel $w_h$ \Cref{tab:kernel-properties} allow us to interpret the sum

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-density-sum}
\rho_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

as a \emph{weighted average} of the particle masses.\sidenote{The closer the particles are to the particle $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$, the larger the weight.} With this approach, we replace the need to \textcolor{gray}{numerically} solve the ODEs \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS} by a summation procedure.\sidenote{Recall again that in practice, sums including an interpolation kernel \emph{do not} have $N$ nonzero summands, as $w_h$ is compactly supported.} This approach becomes particularly useful in formulations where the density field is not the primary quantity of interest; for diagnostic purposes, one can recover the densities at an arbitrary integration step with a single sum rather than solving for the densities all along. Moreover, in section \Cref{sec:HAMILTONIAN-SYSTEM} we will show that the classical SPH with this choice of density is a symplectic system of ODEs. Furthermore,  \autocite{kinclGloballyTimereversibleFluid2023a} has shown that, with a suitable integrator, numerical simulations of isentropic flows in this formulation are \emph{time-reversible}.

Note that the two presented formulations are \emph{not equivalent} --- in general, they result in different densities.

\subsection{Discrete balance of linear momentum}
\label{sec:MOMENTUM}

Let us apply the \emph{improved} discrete gradient \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD} operator, again with $\phi = \rho$ to the balance of momentum

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho}\grad p + \vb{g}, 
\end{equations}

can be recast to the form

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{\vb{v_{\alpha}}}{t} &= -\frac{1}{\rho_{\alpha}} \rho_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\rho_{\beta} V_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} =\\
				&= -\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used the fact $\rho_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha}}$. Notice the structure of the discretised balance of linear momentum. If we denote

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} &\coloneq - m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}, \\
	\vb{F}_{\alpha} &\coloneq m_{\alpha} \vb{g},
\end{equations}

then we can write

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - m_{\alpha} \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} + \vb{g} = \\
			     &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} + \vb{g} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} + \vb{F}_{\alpha}.
\end{equations}

We see that $\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha}$ is the \emph{internal} pressure force through which the particle $\beta$ acts on the particle $\alpha$ and $\vb{F}_{\alpha}$ is the gravity force acting on the particle $\alpha$, \ie an \emph{external} force acting on the particle $\alpha$. Moreover, observe that it holds

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} = - m_{\alpha} m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} = m_{\beta} m_{\alpha}  \left(\frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}+ \frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\beta} w_{\beta\alpha} = - \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta},
\end{equations}

where we have used $w_{\alpha\beta} = w_{\beta\alpha}, \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta} = - \grad_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}$, both due to the radial symmetry of $w_h$.\sidenote{Recall that $w_{\alpha\beta} = w_h\left(\abs{\vb{r}_{\alpha} - \vb{r}_{\beta}}\right)$.} Therefore,

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} = - \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta},
\end{equations}

and we have recovered \emph{Newton's third law} at the \emph{discrete level.} In an isolated system, \ie in the absence of external forces, Newton's third law implies the \emph{conservation of total linear momentum.}\sidenote{The total momentum of the isolated system reads \begin{equations}[single,*]
\sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha},
\end{equations}
and so
\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{t} \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} =\\
	&=\sum_{\alpha=1}^N \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha} =  \sum_{\beta=1}^N \sum_{\alpha=1}^N \vb{F}_{\alpha \to \beta}\\
	&=- \sum_{\alpha=1}^N \sum_{\beta=1}^N \vb{F}_{\beta \to \alpha},
\end{equations}
where we used Newton's second law in the absence of external forces. In total, we thus see it must hold
\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\dv{t} \sum_{\alpha=1}^N m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha} \equiv \vb{0},
\end{equations}
therefore, the total momentum is conserved.} This conservation property would \emph{not hold} had we used the naive discrete gradient \Cref{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} instead of the improved form \Cref{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD}.

\subsection{Complete system}
\label{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}

To close the discretised equations, we require an equation of state for the pressure $p = p\left(\rho, T\right)$. Consistent with our application in atmospheric dynamics, we use the thermal equation of a \textcolor{gray}{calorically} ideal gas \Cref{eq:THERMAL}. Also, since we are interested in \emph{isentropic} flows, we add the equation $\tdot{\theta_{\alpha}} = 0$ for the evolution of the potential temperature. Our complete system hence reads 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \vb{g}, \\
p_{\alpha}\left(\rho_{\alpha}, T_{\alpha}\right) &= R \rho_{\alpha} T_{\alpha}, \\
\theta_{\alpha} &= \, \text{const},
\end{equations}

together with an equation for density \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right) \cdot \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta},
\end{equations}

or \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\rho_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}.
\end{equations}

In the above equations, the index $\alpha \in \{1, \dots, N\}$ runs over all particles.

\section{Hamiltonian formulation}
\label{sec:HAMILTON}

The conservation property we discovered in the previous section aligns with our intuition about the correspondence between SPH and the classical mechanics of point particles. Classical mechanics can be elegantly reframed in the language of \emph{Hamiltonian mechanics}, and we now develop the Hamiltonian formulation of SPH. This reformulation naturally motivates the use of a \emph{symplectic integrator} in the numerical solution of the system of ODEs obtained by the SPH discretisation.

\subsection{Poisson brackets}
\label{sec:BRACKETS}

To set up the Hamiltonian formulation of SPH, we first review the Hamiltonian framework for continuum mechanics in the Lagrangian frame, which we will then discretise. In contrast to the previous sections, where $\Omega$ denoted simply the region occupied by the continuum and $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$ the particle positions, the Hamiltonian formulation requires careful distinction between the reference and current configurations.
We denote by $\vb{X} \in \Omega_0$ the elements of the Lagrangian, \ie reference, configuration manifold $\Omega_0$ and by $\vb{x}$ the elements of the Eulerian, \ie current, configuration manifold. As usual, the deformation (motion) of the continuum is given as a $C^1$ diffeomorphism\sidenote{Technically, $\forall t \in \Rplusnought$ the map $\pmb{\chi}\left(t, \cdot\right): \Omega_0 \to \Omega(t)$ is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism. The set $\Omega(t)$ is the current configuration at time $t$.} $\pmb{\chi}: \left(t, \vb{X}\right) \mapsto \vb{x}$, \ie $\vb{x} = \pmb{\chi}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ from the Lagrangian to the Eulerian configuration. Denote further by $\vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ the Eulerian momentum density per Lagrangian volume.\sidenote{The mentioned momentum is a mixed object: it is the Eulerian momentum expressed using Lagrangian variables per unit Lagrangiam volume. It holds $\vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right) = \rho_0\left(\vb{X}\right)\vb{v}\left(t, \pmb{\chi}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\right)$, with $\rho_0$ being the reference density.} The mappings $\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ will be called the \emph{Lagrangian state variables}.

The continuum analogue to the canonical Poisson bracket of point-particle Hamiltonian mechanics is the \emph{canonical} Poisson bracket in the Lagrangian frame; given two arbitrary Fréchet differentiable functionals $F\left(t, \vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\right)$, $G\left(t, \vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\right)$ of the Lagrangian state variables $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$, we define \autocite[p.~2]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}
\pbr[L]{F}{G} \coloneq \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\fdv{F}{x^i\left(\vb{X}\right)} \fdv{G}{M_i\left(\vb{X}\right)} - \fdv{F}{M_i(\vb{X})}\fdv{G}{x^i\left(\vb{X}\right)}\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

The derivatives appearing in \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET} are functional derivatives, \ie distributions representing the Fréchet differential. For a concise introduction, see \eg the appendix of \autocite{pavelkaMultiscaleThermoDynamicsIntroduction2018}.Following standard convention in continuum mechanics on manifolds, position components are written with upper indices and momentum components with lower indices.

In what follows, we will for brevity of notation omit the explicit dependence of $\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right), \vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ on $\left(t, \vb{X}\right)$ and simply write $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$. One can check that the mapping $\left(F, G\right) \mapsto \pbr[L]{F}{G}$ is a Poisson bracket: a skew-symmetric bilinear form that satisfies the Jacobi identity and Leibniz rule.\sidenote{A bilinear form is skew symmetric provided
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F}{G} = - \pbr[L]{G}{F},
	\end{equations}
	Jacobi identity reads
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F}{\pbr[L]{G}{H}} + \textrm{cycl.} = 0,
	\end{equations}
	and Leibniz rule holds when
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{FG}{H} = F\pbr[L]{G}{H} + \pbr[L]{F}{H}G.
	\end{equations}
}

Given a Fréchet differentiable functional $F = F\left(t, \vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$, its evolution in time is given by \autocite[p.~2]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:TIME-EVOL}
\tdot{F} = \partial_t F + \pbr[L]{F}{\totenL},
\end{equations}

where $\totenL$ is the functional of total energy expressed using the Lagrangian state variables $\vb{x}, \vb{M}$.\sidenote{As an immediate consequence, we see that
	\begin{equations}[single,*]
		\tdot{\totenL} = \partial_t \totenL + \pbr[L]{\totenL}{\totenL} = \partial_t \totenL,
\end{equations}
the bracket vanishes as it is skew-symmetric. If the system is \emph{autonomous}, \ie $\partial_t \totenL$, we see that the energy is conserved.}\sidenote{To be precise, $E$ is Hamilton's functional, \ie the Hamiltonian.} In particular, for a functional $F\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$ that does not depend explicitly on time, we can calculate using the chain rule

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{F}\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right) = \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\fdv{F}{x^i} \partial_t x^i + \fdv{F}{M_i}\partial_t M_i\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

On the other hand, \Cref{eq:TIME-EVOL} dictates

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{F}\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right) = \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\fdv{F}{x^i} \fdv{\totenL}{M_i} - \fdv{F}{M_i} \fdv{\totenL}{x^i}\right)\dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}

since $F$ was arbitrary, by the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations we conclude \autocite[p.~3]{pavelkaHamiltonianContinuumMechanics2020} that 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\partial_t x^i\left(t, \vb{X}\right) &= \fdv{\totenL}{M_i\left(t, \vb{X}\right)},\label{eq:HAMILTON-POSITION}\\
\partial_t M_i\left(t, \vb{X}\right) &= -\fdv{\totenL}{x^i\left(t, \vb{X}\right)}.\label{eq:HAMILTON-MOMENTA}
\end{equations}

The equations \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-POSITION}, \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-MOMENTA} are the Hamilton \emph{canonical} equations of continuum mechanics. The right-hand sides of the equations require the knowledge of the total energy of the continuum expressed using the Lagrangian state variables.

In general, the energy has a contribution from the kinetic energy $\abs{\vb{M}}^{2}/2\rho_0$, $\rho_0$ being the reference density, and the \emph{volumetric density} of internal energy $e$. The fundamental thermodynamic relation $e = e\left(\rho, \eta\right)$, $\eta$ being the entropy density per Eulerian volume is naturally expressed in terms of \emph{Eulerian} volume densities $\rho$ and $\eta$. To incorporate it into the \emph{Lagrangian} energy functional, we transform to the Lagrangian frame using the Jacobian $J\left(\vb{X}\right) = \det_{\vb{X}} \grad \pmb{\chi}\left(\vb{X}\right)$

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY}
\totenL = \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\frac{\abs{\vb{M}}^{2}}{2\rho_0} + Je\left(\rho\left(\vb{x}\left(\vb{X}\right)\right)\right), \eta\left(\vb{x}\left(\vb{X}\right)\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}


We briefly summarise the structure of what follows. We have noticed that for the description of the evolution of a functional of the Lagrangian state variables, we require: \begin{inparaenum} \item the Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}, \item the total energy functional \Cref{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY}. \end{inparaenum} To obtain the time evolution of a functional of the \emph{SPH state variables}, we require \begin{inparaenum} \item a SPH Poisson bracket, \item the SPH energy \end{inparaenum}. The SPH bracket is obtained by reducing the Lagrangian bracket to the SPH state variables; SPH energy is obtained from the Lagrangian energy via the particle approximation.

\subsection{SPH discretisation}
\label{sec:SPH-discretisation}

Denote $\{\Omega_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N, N \in \N$ a pairwise disjoint \textcolor{gray}{finite} partition of the reference manifold $\Omega_0$, \ie $\Omega_0 = \bigcup_{\alpha=1}^N \Omega_{\alpha}$, $\Omega_{\alpha} \cap \Omega_{\beta} = \emptyset$ for $\alpha \neq \beta$. The sets $\Omega_{\alpha}$ will represent the \emph{SPH particles.} Moreover, with the aid of the normalized characteristic function of $\Omega_{\alpha}$,\autocite[p.~5]{pavelkaApproachesConservativeSmoothed2025}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right) = \begin{cases}
	\frac{1}{V_{\alpha}}, &\vb{X} \in \Omega_{\alpha},\\
	0,  &\vb{X} \notin \Omega_{\alpha}
\end{cases}, V_{\alpha} = \int_{\Omega_{\alpha}} \dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}
we can define the \emph{SPH position} $\vb{x}_{\alpha}\left(t\right)$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-position}
\vb{x}_{\alpha}(t) = \int_{\Omega_0}\vb{x}\left(t, \vb{X}\right) \chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right)\dd{\vb{X}},
\end{equations}

and the \emph{SPH momentum} $\vb{M}_{\alpha}\left(t\right)$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-momentum}
\vb{M}_{\alpha}\left(t\right) = \int_{\Omega_0}\vb{M}\left(t, \vb{X}\right)\chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right)V_{\alpha}\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

Naturally, the SPH positions and momenta $\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ will replace the state variables $\left(\vb{x}, \vb{M}\right)$ of the continuum mechanics, and so we will refer to them as the SPH state variables.

\subsubsection{SPH bracket}
\label{sec:SPH-bracket}

Let $F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right), G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ be two arbitrary Fréchet differentiable functionals of the SPH state variables. From the definition of the SPH state variables \Cref{eq:SPH-position}, \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum} it follows that $F, G$ in fact also depend on the state variables of the continuum mechanics---hence we are allowed to substitute them into the Lagrangian Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:LAGR-BRACKET}. The calculation reads

\begin{equations}[lines,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:TERM}
	\pbr[L]{F}{G} = \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\fdv{F}{x^i}\fdv{G}{M_i} - \fdv{F}{M_i} \fdv{G}{x^i}\right)\dd{\vb{X}} = \\ = \sum_{\alpha,\beta}\bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\left(\pdv{F}{x_{\alpha}^j} \fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{x^i} + \pdv{F}{M_{\beta,j}}\fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{x^i}\right)\left(\pdv{G}{M_{\beta,j}}\fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{M_i} + \pdv{G}{x^j_{\alpha}}\fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{M_i}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}} + \\ - \sum_{\alpha,\beta} \bigint_{\Omega_0}\left(\left(\pdv{F}{M_{\beta,j}} \fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{M_i} + \pdv{F}{x_{\alpha}^j} \fdv{x_{\alpha}^j}{M_i}\right)\left(\pdv{G}{x^j_{\alpha}} \fdv{x_{\alpha}^j}{x^i} + \pdv{G}{M_{\beta,j}} \fdv{M_{\beta, j}}{x_i}\right)\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

Our goal is to simplify \Cref{eq:TERM} and obtain an expression depending only on the SPH state variables $\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}$. The procedure requires the evaluation of the functional derivatives of $x^j_{\alpha}, M_{\beta,j}$ w.r.t $M_i, x^i$. From the definition \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum}, we obtain for any $\varphi \in \Ccinf{\Omega_0}$

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\left\langle \fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{x^i\left(\vb{X}\right)}, \varphi \right\rangle	&= \bigint_{\Omega_0} \fdv{x_{\alpha}^j}{x^i} \varphi \dd{\vb{X}} = \eval{\dv{s}x^j_{\alpha}(x^i + s \varphi)}_{s=0} =\\ 
											&=\dv{s}\eval{\int_{\Omega_0}\delta^j_i(x^i + s \varphi)\chi_{\alpha}\dd{\vb{X}}}_{s=0} = \int_{\Omega_0}\delta^j_i \chi_{\alpha} \varphi\dd{\vb{X}}, 
\end{equations}

where $\langle,\rangle$ stand for the duality in the sense of distributions, and so the functional derivative takes the form

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{x^i} = \delta^j_i \chi_{\alpha}.
\end{equations}

In the same manner we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{M_{i}} = \delta^{j}_i \chi_{\beta} V_{\beta},
\end{equations}

and the cross terms $\fdv{x^j_{\alpha}}{M_i}, \fdv{M_{\beta,j}}{x^i}$ are zero, as the definition of the SPH position \Cref{eq:SPH-position} and SPH momentum \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum} include only the continuous position $\vb{x}$ and continuous momentum $\vb{M}$ respectively. Using these results and the normalisation of the characteristic functions the expression \Cref{eq:TERM} can be rewritten to obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pbr[L]{F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)}{G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)} = \sum_{\alpha}\left(\pdv{F}{x^{i}_{\alpha}}\pdv{G}{M_{\alpha,i}} - \pdv{F}{M_{\alpha,i}}\pdv{G}{x^i_{\alpha}}\right).
\end{equations}

It is hence natural to define the \emph{SPH bracket} for any two functionals $F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right), G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ of the SPH state variables through

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-bracket}
\pbr[SPH]{F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)}{G\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)} \coloneq \sum_{\alpha}\left(\pdv{F}{x^i_{\alpha}}\pdv{G}{M_{\alpha,i}} - \pdv{F}{M_{\alpha,i}}\pdv{G}{x^i_{\alpha}}\right),
\end{equations}

The SPH bracket is the restriction of the \emph{continuum bracket} to the subspace of functionals depending on the continuum fields \emph{only through} their SPH averages \Cref{eq:SPH-momentum} and \Cref{eq:SPH-position}.

\subsubsection{SPH energy functional}
\label{sec:SPH-functional}

Now we aim to rewrite \Cref{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY} in terms of the SPH state variables. The kinetic energy counterpart evidently is $\abs{\vb{M}_{\alpha}}^{2}/2 m_{\alpha}$, with 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-particle-mass}
m_{\alpha} = \int_{\Omega_{0}}\rho_0\left(\vb{X}\right) \chi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{X}\right)\dd{\vb{X}}.
\end{equations}

To recover the internal energy, we need to introduce the density of the particle in the \emph{Eulerian} frame $\rho_{\alpha}$. This can be done in a familiar way \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum} using \autocite[p.~7]{pavelkaApproachesConservativeSmoothed2025} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:SPH-density-sum-AGAIN}
\rho_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta},
\end{equations}

Since the transformation of density yields

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\rho = \frac{\rho_0}{J}, \, \text{i.e.} \, J = \frac{\rho_0}{\rho},
\end{equations}

we see that

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	J e\left(\rho, \eta\right) = \frac{\rho_0}{\rho} e.
\end{equations}

The SPH counterpart to \Cref{eq:LAGRANGIAN-ENERGY} is therefore

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-energy}
\totenSPH = \sum_{\alpha=1}^N\left(\frac{\abs{\vb{M}_{\alpha}}^{2}}{2 m_{\alpha}} + \frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha} \rho_{\alpha}} e\left(\rho_{\alpha}, \eta_{\alpha}\right)V_{\alpha}\right) = \sum_{\alpha=1}^N\left(\frac{\abs{\vb{M}_{\alpha}}^2}{2 m_{\alpha}} + \frac{m_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}}e\left(\rho_{\alpha}, \eta_{\alpha}\right)\right).
\end{equations}

\subsection{Classical SPH as a Hamiltonian system}
\label{sec:HAMILTONIAN-SYSTEM}

We are in a position to write down the evolution equation of an arbitrary functional $F\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$ of the SPH variables: if $F$ does not explicitly depend on time we have

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\tdot{F} = \pbr[SPH]{F}{\totenSPH} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^N\left(\pdv{F}{x_{\alpha}^i} \pdv{\totenSPH}{M_{\alpha, i}}- \pdv{F}{M_{\alpha, i}} \pdv{\totenSPH}{x_{\alpha}^i}\right).
\end{equations}

The partial derivative of \Cref{eq:SPH-energy} with respect to the momentum reads

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pdv{\totenSPH}{M_{\beta,j}} = \pdv{M_{\beta, j}} \sum_{\gamma=1}^N \left(\frac{\abs{\vb{M}_{\gamma}}^{2}}{2 m_{\gamma}} + \frac{m_{\gamma}}{\rho_{\gamma}}e\right) = \sum_{\gamma=1}^N\sum_{k=1}^d \frac{1}{2 m_{\gamma}} 2 M_{\gamma, k}\delta_{jk} \delta_{\beta \gamma} = \frac{M_{\beta,j}}{m_{\beta}}.
\end{equations}

The derivative with respect to the position leads to a rather lengthy, but straightforward computation. We only state the resulting form; details can be found in \autocite[p.~8]{pavelkaApproachesConservativeSmoothed2025}:

% WARNING: WRONG K INDICES - MAYBE add delta?
\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:derivative}
	\grad_{\alpha}\totenSPH &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(m_{\beta} \frac{m_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}}\left(- e +  \rho_{\alpha}\pdv{e}{\rho_{\alpha}} + \eta_{\alpha}\pdv{e}{\eta_{\alpha}}\right)\right) \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \\
			&+ \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(m_{\alpha} \frac{m_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\left(- e + \rho_{\beta} \pdv{e}{\rho_{\beta}} + \eta_{\beta} \pdv{e}{\eta_{\beta}}\right) \right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}.
\end{equations}

The Euler relation\sidenote{Recall that we are dealing with volumetric densities of energy and entropy.} \autocite[p.~61]{callenThermodynamicsIntroductionThermostatistics1985} \autocite[p.~42]{pavelkaApproachesConservativeSmoothed2025} 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
p = -e + \rho \pdv{e}{\rho} + \eta \pdv{e}{\eta},
\end{equations}

allows us to define the pressure of the particle $\alpha$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:particle-pressure}
p_{\alpha} = -e\left(\rho_{\alpha}, \eta_{\alpha}\right) + \rho_{\alpha} \pdv{e}{\rho_{\alpha}} + \eta_{\alpha}\pdv{e}{\eta_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

Using this definition, the derivative \Cref{eq:derivative} reads 


\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\grad_{\alpha}\totenSPH = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\alpha} m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}.
\end{equations}

The Hamilton canonical equations generated by the SPH bracket are thus

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\dv{x^i_{\alpha}}{t} &= \pdv{\totenSPH}{M_{\alpha, i}} = \frac{M_{\alpha, i}}{m_{\alpha}},\\
\dv{M_{\alpha, i}}{t} &= -\pdv{\totenSPH}{x^i_{\alpha}} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\alpha} m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\pdv{w_{\alpha \beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

If we denote $\vb{v}_{\alpha} \coloneq \dv{\vb{x}_{\alpha}}{t}$ the Hamilton canonical equations are equivalent to

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha} &= \vb{M}_{\alpha}, \\
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right) \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta},
\end{equations}

where the density is given as \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum-AGAIN}. We have thus shown that the system

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HAMILTON-SYSTEM}
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}, \\
	\rho_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^{N} m_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta},
\end{equations}

is symplectic, as it represents the Hamilton equations generated by a \emph{canonical} Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:SPH-bracket}.

To check consistency with the classical SPH formulation of \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}, we now evaluate the time derivative of the SPH density $\rho_{\alpha}$ \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum-AGAIN}
\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} &= \pbr[SPH]{\rho_{\alpha}}{\totenSPH} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{x^j_{\beta}} \pdv{\totenSPH}{M_{\beta, j}} - \pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{M_{\beta, j}} \pdv{\totenSPH}{x^j_{\beta}}\right) = \\ 
			      &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \frac{M_{\beta, j}}{m_{\beta}} \pdv{x^{j}_{\beta}} \sum_{\gamma=1}^N m_{\gamma} w_{\alpha \gamma} = \sum_{\beta, \gamma =1}^N \left(\frac{M_{\beta, j}}{m_{\beta}} m_{\gamma}\pdv{w_{\alpha \gamma}}{x^j_{\beta}}\right) = \\ 
			      &= \sum_{\beta, \gamma = 1}^N \sum_{k=1}^d \delta^j_k \left(\frac{M_{\beta, j}}{m_{\beta}} m_{\gamma} w'_{\alpha \gamma}\left(\delta_{\alpha \beta} - \delta_{\gamma \beta}\right)\frac{x^k_{\alpha} - x^k_{\gamma}}{\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\gamma}}}\right) = \\ 
			      &= \sum_{\gamma=1}^N \left(\frac{M_{\alpha, j}}{m_{\alpha}} - \frac{M_{\gamma, j}}{m_{\gamma}}\right)m_{\gamma} w'_{\alpha \gamma} \frac{x^j_{\alpha} - x^j_{\gamma}}{\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\gamma}}},
\end{equations}

and so if we use the chain rule and $\vb{M}_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} \vb{v}_{\alpha}$ we find

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\dv{\rho_{\alpha}}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \left(\vb{v}_{\alpha} - \vb{v}_{\beta}\right) m_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta},
\end{equations}

which is exactly the classical discretisation of the continuity equation \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS}.

Using the framework of Hamiltonian continuum mechanics, we were thus able to derive the same classical SPH equations as in \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}. Moreover, we have been able to show that the system \Cref{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH} with the kernel sum density \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum} is symplectic, as it represents the Hamilton equations generated by a canonical Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:SPH-bracket}. This symplectic structure underlies the time-reversibility result of \autocite{kinclGloballyTimereversibleFluid2023a} cited earlier.

Notice that we \emph{did not} prove the symplecticity of the system \Cref{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH} with the discrete continuity equation \Cref{eq:DISCR-MASS}, as the Poisson bracket \Cref{eq:SPH-bracket} is canonical in the SPH state variables $\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}$, \emph{not} including $\rho_{\alpha}$. It is possible to show that this system is Hamiltonian but \emph{not symplectic}, as it is generated by the \emph{non-canonical} Poisson bracket\autocite[p.~9]{pavelkaApproachesConservativeSmoothed2025} 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\pbr[SPH-\rho]{F}{G} &= \pbr[SPH]{F}{G} + \\ 
+& \sum_{\alpha, \beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \sum_{i=1}^d e^i_{\alpha \beta}\left(\pdv{F}{\rho_{\alpha}}\left(\pdv{G}{M_{\alpha, i}} - \pdv{G}{M_{\beta, i}}\right) - \pdv{G}{\rho_{\alpha}}\left(\pdv{F}{M_{\alpha, i}} - \pdv{F}{M_{\beta, i}}\right)\right),
\end{equations}

where 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
e^i_{\alpha \beta} = \frac{x^i_{\alpha} - x^i_{\beta}}{\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}}.
\end{equations}

We will not pursue this here.

\subsection{Symplectic integrators}
\label{sec:INTEGRATORS}

Having established the Hamiltonian/symplectic structure of SPH, we now describe the time integrator used in our simulations. Symplectic systems are of interest because they admit specialised numerical methods --- \emph{symplectic integrators} --- with favourable long-term conservation properties. In particular, the energy error of a symplectic integrator of order $p$ with time step $\Delta t$ remains bounded by $\bigO{\Delta t^p}$ over exponentially long simulation times \autocite{hairerGeometricNumericalIntegration2006, leimkuhlerSimulatingHamiltonianDynamics2004}. Moreover, \autocite{kinclGloballyTimereversibleFluid2023a} has shown that, with a suitable symplectic integrator and a correction to the initial conditions, the resulting numerical simulation is \emph{time-reversible}.

In our simulations, we use the \emph{Störmer--Verlet} method, which approximates the solution of the system 
\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\tdot{\vb{q}} &= \vb{v}, \\
	\tdot{\vb{v}} &= \vb{f}(\vb{q}),
\end{equations}
by the one-step procedure $(\vb{q}_n, \vb{v}_n) \mapsto (\vb{q}_{n+1}, \vb{v}_{n+1})$ defined as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:STORMER-VERLET}
\vb{v}_{n+\frac{1}{2}} &= \vb{v}_n + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \vb{f}(\vb{q}_n), \\
\vb{q}_{n+1} &= \vb{q}_n + \Delta t \, \vb{v}_{n+\frac{1}{2}}, \\
\vb{v}_{n+1} &= \vb{v}_{n+\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{\Delta t}{2} \vb{f}(\vb{q}_{n+1}),
\end{equations}
where $\Delta t$ denotes the time step. The method is explicit, of second order, and symplectic for separable Hamiltonians \autocite{Hairer_2003}. Our SPH Hamiltonian \Cref{eq:SPH-energy} is separable\sidenote{In this sense, separability means that the kinetic energy depends only on the momenta $\vb{M}_{\alpha}$ and the potential energy only on the positions $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$.} in $\left(\vb{x}_{\alpha}, \vb{M}_{\alpha}\right)$, so Störmer–Verlet is symplectic in our setting.

	In the context of SPH, the role of $\vb{q}$ is identified with the particle positions $\{\vb{x}_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N$, $\vb{v}$ with the velocities $\{\vb{v}_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N$, and $\vb{f}$ by the right-hand side of the discrete momentum equation \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-SYSTEM}. 
	This integrator is used throughout the numerical experiments in \Cref{chap:EXPERIMENTS}.

\section{Density-independent formulations}
\label{sec:DENSITY-FREE}

We have introduced the classical SPH formulation \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE} and decided to use the kernel-summed density \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum}, which yielded a symplectic system \Cref{eq:HAMILTON-SYSTEM} in the Hamiltonian formulation. However, the use of the kernel-summed density becomes problematic in situations relevant to stratified atmospheric flows.

The first complication arises with the need for \emph{adaptive smoothing lengths.} We have mentioned in \Cref{sec:ADAPTIVE-NEED} how in a stratified atmosphere, the background density varies by an order of magnitude across the simulation domain. By using a fixed smoothing length $h$, we either over-resolve regions of high density or under-resolve regions of low density --- adaptive $h$ is hence essential for correctly resolving the background and for efficient computation.
However, naive implementations with adaptive $h$ \emph{break the conservative properties} of standard SPH \autocite{hernquistCautionaryRemarksSmoothed1993,nelsonVariableSmoothingLengths1994}. Conservation properties are among the most valuable advantages of SPH, and they are particularly important for our target application---\emph{isentropic flows}. An adaptive-$h$ conservative formulation is therefore needed. There are ways to account for the spatial changes of $h$ by adding the so-called $\grad h$ terms to the classical SPH system that restore conservation \autocite{nelsonThreedimensionalHydrodynamicSimulations1993,nelsonVariableSmoothingLengths1994}. The resulting system is however cumbersome and is rarely used in practice \autocite[p.~4]{springelCosmologicalSPHSimulations2002}. The density-independent formulation we present below \emph{incorporates adaptive smoothing lengths naturally}, without requiring explicit $\grad h$ correction terms.

The second complication lies within the kernel-summed density \emph{itself.} In problems where the density varies strongly, \eg at shocks or contact discontinuities, the kernel-summed approach suffers from \emph{artificial surface tension}, a boundary-like artifact \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}. Even though the density field of mountain waves is smooth, the strong background stratification produces qualitatively similar artefacts.

The class of \emph{density-independent} formulations \autocite{saitohDensityIndependentFormulation2013,hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} addresses both difficulties by removing the kernel-summed density from the formulation entirely, replacing it with an \emph{alternative volume estimator}. In what follows, we briefly review the original formulation and then present the $\theta$-reformulation developed in this work. 

\subsection{A general class of Lagrangian SPH methods}
\label{sec:GENERAL-CLASS}
In this section, we derive the SPH equations for an arbitrary choice of particle volume. In the following sections, we will obtain specific formulations---including the classical and density-independent variants---by selecting particular volume estimators.

A consistent and elegant way to derive \emph{conservative schemes} is to invoke the underlying structure. Previously we used the Hamiltonian framework; now we use the \emph{Lagrangian formulation} of classical mechanics, as it will easily allow us to treat the constraints we will impose on the smoothing lengths $\{h_{\alpha}\}$.

As opposed to fixed $h$, variable smoothing lengths must be treated as \emph{independent variables.} Denote $\{h_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N \subset \Rplus$ the smoothing lengths of particles at positions $\{\vb{x}_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N$. Our generalized coordinates therefore are $\left(\vb{x}_{1}, \dots, \vb{x}_N, h_1, \dots, h_N\right)\in \R^{N(d+1)}$. The SPH Lagrangian reads \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}\autocite[p.~4]{springelCosmologicalSPHSimulations2002} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-LAGRANGIAN}
L\left(\vb{x}_1, \dots, \vb{x}_N, h_1, \dots, h_N\right) = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \abs{\vb{v}_{\beta}}^{2} - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} e_{\beta},
\end{equations}

with $e_{\beta}$ being the internal energy per unit mass of the particle $\beta$. The evolution of the system is given by the Lagrange equations

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\dot{x}^i_{\alpha}} - \pdv{L}{x^i_{\alpha}} &= 0, \\
	\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\dot{h}_{\alpha}} - \pdv{L}{h_{\alpha}} &= 0, \alpha \in \{1, \dots, N\}, i \in \{1, \dots, d\}.
\end{equations}

Now if $h_{\alpha}$ vary, the derivatives of the Lagrangian w.r.t to $h_{\alpha}$ need to be evaluated---we need to describe how the smoothing lengths change. It is common \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a,saitohDensityIndependentFormulation2013,springelCosmologicalSPHSimulations2002} to describe the smoothing lengths implicitly by imposing the holonomic constraint on the \emph{effective neighbour number}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CONSTRAINT}
\Phi_{\alpha}\left(\vb{x}_1, \dots, \vb{x}_{\alpha}, h_1, \dots, h_N\right) \coloneq \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}} - N_{\, \text{ngb} \,} = 0, \alpha \in \{1, \dots, N\},
\end{equations}

where $\abs{B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)}$ is the $d-$ dimensional volume of the kernel support ball, $\tilde{V}_{\alpha}$ is \emph{some} estimator of the particle volume and $N_{\, \text{ngb} \,}$ is the chosen constant neighbor number. The kernel ball has radius $R_{\alpha} = \kappa h_{\alpha}$, with $\kappa$ being kernel dependent such that $2 \kappa h_{\alpha} = \diam \supp w_{h_{\alpha}}$, see \Cref{sec:INTERPOLATION}. This constraint determines $h_{\alpha}$ implicitly given the particle distribution $\{\vb{x}_{\alpha}\}$, by requiring that the kernel ball $B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)$ contains a fixed effective neighbour count $N_{\, \text{ngb} \,}$.

Introducing $N$ Lagrange multipliers $\{\lambda_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha=1}^N$ the system of governing equations thus reads

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:LAGRANGE-EQUATIONS}
\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\dot{x}^i_{\alpha}} - \pdv{L}{x^i_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta} \pdv{\Phi_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}}, \\
\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\dot{h}_{\alpha}} - \pdv{L}{h_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta} \pdv{\Phi_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}}, \alpha \in \{1, \dots, N\}, i \in \{1, \dots, d\}. \\
\end{equations}

To compute the derivatives of the Lagrangian \Cref{eq:SPH-LAGRANGIAN}, we require the knowledge of the derivatives of the internal energy $e$ w.r.t $\vb{x}_{\alpha}, h_{\alpha}$. Using the first law of thermodynamics

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	m\dd{e} = \delta q + \delta w,
\end{equations}

with $\delta q$ and $\delta w$ being the exchanged heat and work respectively. For an isentropic process\sidenote{Recall that all isentropic processes are adiabatic processes.} and an ideal gas the thermodynamic reads $\delta q = 0, \delta w = -p \dd{V}$, and so 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\dd{e} = - \frac{p}{m} \dd{V}.
\end{equations}

Therefore, the derivative of $e_{\alpha}$ w.r.t $x_{\alpha}^i$ is\sidenote{The differentials read
\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dd{e_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{i=1}^d \pdv{e_{\alpha}}{x^i_{\alpha}} \dd{x^i_{\alpha}},\\
	\dd{V_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{i=1}^d \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{x^i_{\alpha}} \dd{x^i_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}
and so by comparing both sides of the equation we see 
\begin{equations}[single,*]
\pdv{e_{\alpha}}{x^i_{\alpha}} = - \frac{p_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{x^i_{\alpha}}. 
\end{equations}
} \autocite[p.~2]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:E-X-DERIVATIVE}
\pdv{e_{\alpha}}{x_{\alpha}^i} = - \frac{p_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{x^i_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

and with respect to $h_{\alpha}$ the derivative is

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:E-H-DERIVATIVE}
\pdv{e_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}} = - \frac{p_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

with $V_{\alpha}$ being \emph{some} particle volume estimator. Notice that $V_{\alpha}$ and $\tilde{V}_{\alpha}$ can in general be different \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}; $V_{\alpha}$ enters the thermodynamics through \Cref{eq:E-X-DERIVATIVE} and \Cref{eq:E-H-DERIVATIVE}, whereas $\tilde{V}_{\alpha}$ enters the constraint \Cref{eq:CONSTRAINT}. Impacts of the choice $V_{\alpha} = \tilde{V}_{\alpha}$ are further discussed in \eg \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}.

First we solve for the Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_{\alpha}$. Since the Lagrangian \Cref{eq:SPH-LAGRANGIAN} does not depend on $\tdot{h_{\alpha}}$, the second set of the Lagrange equations \Cref{eq:LAGRANGE-EQUATIONS} read as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	- \pdv{L}{h_{\alpha}} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta} \pdv{\Phi_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

The left-hand side is

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	- \pdv{L}{h_{\alpha}} &= - \pdv{h_{\alpha}}\left(\frac{1}{2} \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \abs{\vb{v}_{\beta}}^{2} - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} e_{\beta}\right) = \\
			      &= + \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \pdv{e_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \pdv{e_{\beta}}{h_{\beta}} \underbrace{\pdv{h_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}}}_{= \delta_{\beta \alpha}} = \\
	&= - m_{\alpha}\frac{p_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}} = -p_{\alpha} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

where we used \Cref{eq:E-H-DERIVATIVE}. The right-hand side reads

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta}\pdv{\Phi_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta} \pdv{h_{\alpha}}\left(\frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}} - N_{\, \text{ngb} \,}\right) = \\
							&= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta}\left(\frac{1}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}} \pdv{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{h_{\alpha}} - \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}^{2}} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\beta}}{h_{\alpha}}\right) = \\
							&=\sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta}\left(\frac{\kappa}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}}\delta_{\beta \alpha}\eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{h_{\beta}}}_{R = R_{\beta}} - \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}^{2}} \delta_{\beta \alpha} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\beta}}{h_{\beta}}\right) = \\
							&= \lambda_{\alpha}\left(\frac{\kappa}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}} \eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{h_{\alpha}}}_{R = R_{\alpha}} - \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}^{2}} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right),
\end{equations}

thanks to the chain rule $\pdv{\abs{B\left(\kappa h_{\alpha}\right)}}{h_{\alpha}} = \kappa \eval{\pdv{\abs{B\left(R\right)}}{h_{\alpha}}}_{R = \kappa h_{\alpha}}$. The multiplier $\lambda_{\alpha}$ thus reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MULTIPLIER}
\lambda_{\alpha} = - p_{\alpha} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\left(\frac{\kappa}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}} \eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{h_{\alpha}}}_{R = R_{\alpha}} - \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}^{2}} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equations}

We now deal with the first set of equations \Cref{eq:LAGRANGE-EQUATIONS}. The left-hand consists of the terms


\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\tdot{x}^i_{\alpha}} &=\dv{t} \pdv{v^i_{\alpha}}\left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N \left( \frac{1}{2} m_{\beta}\abs{\vb{v}_{\beta}}^{2} - m_{\beta} e_{\beta}\right) \right) = \dv{t} \left(m_{\alpha} v_{\alpha}^i \right),
\end{equations}

and

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	- \pdv{L}{x^i_{\alpha}} &= - \pdv{x^i_{\alpha}} \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N \left( \frac{1}{2} m_{\beta}\abs{\vb{v}_{\beta}}^{2} - m_{\beta} e_{\beta}\right) \right) = \\
				&= + \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \pdv{e_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_\beta \pdv{V_{\beta}}{x^{i}_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

using \Cref{eq:E-X-DERIVATIVE}. In total, the left-hand side reads

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\dv{t} \pdv{L}{\tdot{x}^i_{\alpha}} - \pdv{L}{x^i_{\alpha}} = m_{\alpha} \dv{v_{\alpha}^i}{t} - \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_\beta \pdv{V_{\beta}}{x^{i}_{\alpha}}, 
\end{equations}

whereas the right-hand side is

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta} \pdv{\Phi_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N \lambda_{\beta}\pdv{x^i_{\alpha}}\left(\frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}} - N_{\, \text{ngb} \,}\right) = \\
									  &= \sum_{\beta=1}-\lambda_{\beta} \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}^{2}}\pdv{\tilde{V}_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}}. 
\end{equations}

Unlike the derivative with respect to $h_\alpha$ the derivative $\pdv{L}{x^i_\alpha}$ does not reduce to a single term, because $V_\beta$ depends on $\vb{x}_\alpha$ for all particles $\beta$ whose kernel support contains the particle $\alpha$.
Thus the first set of the Lagrange equations \Cref{eq:LAGRANGE-EQUATIONS} yields

\begin{equations}[single,*]
m_{\alpha} \dv{v_{\alpha}^i}{t} - \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_{\beta}\pdv{V_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N\lambda_{\beta} \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}^{2}}\pdv{\tilde{V}_{\beta}}{x^i_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

so upon rearranging we arrive at

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_{\beta} \grad_{\alpha} V_{\beta} - \sum_{\beta=1}^N\lambda_{\beta} \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\beta}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\beta}^{2}} \grad_{\alpha} \tilde{V}_{\beta}, \\
	\lambda_{\alpha} &=  - p_{\alpha} \pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\left(\frac{\kappa}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}} \eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{h_{\alpha}}}_{R = R_{\alpha}} - \frac{\abs{B\left(R_{\alpha}\right)}}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}^{2}} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equations}

We now eliminate $\lambda_\alpha$ from the momentum equation. Define the $\grad h$ \emph{factor}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:GRAD-H-FACTOR}
f_\alpha \coloneq \frac{\kappa \tilde V_\alpha}{\abs{B(R_\alpha)}}\,\eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{R}}_{R = R_\alpha} - \pdv{\tilde V_\alpha}{h_\alpha},
\end{equations}

which captures the response of the constraint to a change in $h_\alpha$. Substituting $\lambda_\beta$ from \Cref{eq:MULTIPLIER} into the momentum equation and simplifying, the factors of $\abs{B(R_\beta)} / \tilde V_\beta^2$ cancel within each summand, and the momentum equation reduces to

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DI-MOMENTUM}
m_\alpha \dv{\vb{v}_\alpha}{t} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_\beta \grad_\alpha V_\beta + \sum_{\beta=1}^N\frac{p_\beta}{f_\beta} \pdv{V_\beta}{h_\beta} \grad_\alpha \tilde V_\beta.
\end{equations}

The first term on the right-hand side is the standard pressure force; the second is the $\grad h$ \emph{correction} that restores conservation in the presence of variable smoothing lengths.

\paragraph{Specialisation to two dimensions and the Wendland quintic kernel.}
For our applications we work in $d = 2$ with the Wendland quintic kernel of \Cref{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}, for which $\kappa = 2$ and so $R_\alpha = 2 h_\alpha$. The volume of the kernel ball is $|B(R)| = \pi R^2$, giving

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\abs{B(R_\alpha)} &= 4\pi h_\alpha^2, \\
	\eval{\pdv{\abs{B(R)}}{R}}_{R = R_\alpha} &= 4\pi h_\alpha.
\end{equations}

The grad-h factor \Cref{eq:GRAD-H-FACTOR} simplifies to

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:GRAD-H-2D}
f_\alpha = \frac{2 \tilde V_\alpha}{h_\alpha} - \pdv{\tilde V_\alpha}{h_\alpha} = \frac{\tilde V_\alpha}{h_\alpha}\left(2 - \frac{h_\alpha}{\tilde V_\alpha}\pdv{\tilde V_\alpha}{h_\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

Since in the following we always work in a two-dimensional setting with the Wendland quintic kernel, let us state the equations explicitly. The momentum equation \Cref{eq:DI-MOMENTUM} with $f_\alpha$ given by \Cref{eq:GRAD-H-2D} reads

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DI-2D}
m_\alpha \dv{\vb{v}_\alpha}{t} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N p_\beta \grad_\alpha V_\beta + \sum_{\beta=1}^N\frac{p_\beta}{f_\beta} \pdv{V_\beta}{h_\beta} \grad_\alpha \tilde V_\beta, \\
f_{\alpha} &= \frac{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\left(2 - \frac{h_{\alpha}}{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}} \pdv{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right).
\end{equations}

Notice that in \Cref{eq:DI-2D}, and also in the previous equations, we have \emph{not yet specified} how to evaluate the particle pressure $p_{\alpha}$. The choice of thermodynamic variable, hence the pressure-computation rule, is \emph{free to choose}. We exploit this degree of freedom in the following subsections to derive the density-pressure formulation \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}, the pressure-entropy formulation \autocite[p.~4]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} and, as our main contribution, its pressure-potential temperature analogue.

\subsection{Kernel-sum volume estimators}
\label{sec:KERNEL-SUM-VOLUMES}

The general Lagrangian formulation of SPH \Cref{eq:DI-MOMENTUM}, \Cref{eq:GRAD-H-FACTOR} needs the volume estimators $V_{\alpha}, \tilde{V}_{\alpha}$. Note that there are numerous ways to choose the estimators;\sidenote{The simplest approaches include $V_{\alpha} = \tilde{V}_{\alpha} \propto h_{\alpha}^d$ or even $V_{\alpha} = \tilde{V}_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} / \rho_{0}$, $\rho_0$ being a fixed reference value.} in this section, we consider a class of volume estimators based on kernel sums \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}: given a scalar quantity $x$ carried by the particles, the $x_{\alpha}$-weighted average is the quantity $y_{\alpha}$ obtained as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:KERNEL-SUM}
y_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N x_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right),
\end{equations}

with the notation $w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) = w_{h_{\alpha}}\left(\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}\right)$. The volume estimator corresponding to the $x_{\alpha}$-weighted sum is then \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:KERNEL-SUM-VOLUME}
V_{\alpha} = \frac{x_{\alpha}}{y_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

For $x_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha}$ we obtain exactly the summation density \Cref{eq:SPH-density-sum}, \ie $y_{\alpha} = \rho_{\alpha}$ and $V_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} / \rho_{\alpha}$.

The derivatives of \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-VOLUME} can be computed directly. Since $x_{\alpha}$ is independent of $h_{\alpha}$\sidenote{Realize that $x_{\alpha}$ is a particle property.}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}} = - \frac{x_{\alpha}}{y_{\alpha}^{2}} \pdv{y_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}},
\end{equations}

To express the derivative of $y_\alpha$ we require the derivative of the kernel with respect to its smoothing length. A direct computation using the chain rule yields\sidenote{We utilize \begin{equations}[single,*]
w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) = \frac{1}{h^d} \tilde{w}\left(\frac{\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}}{h_{\alpha}}\right),
\end{equations}
with $\tilde{w}(q_{\alpha \beta}), q_{\alpha \beta} = \frac{\abs{\vb{x}_{\alpha} - \vb{x}_{\beta}}}{h_{\alpha}}$ being the dimensionless kernel shape, see \eg \Cref{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}.}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:KERNEL-H-DERIVATIVE}
\pdv{w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha)}{h_\alpha} = -\frac{1}{h_\alpha}\left(d\, w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha) + q_{\alpha\beta} \pdv{w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha)}{q_{\alpha\beta}}\right),
\end{equations}

where $q_{\alpha\beta} = \abs{\vb{x}_\alpha - \vb{x}_\beta} / h_\alpha$. Note that the derivative $\pdv{w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)}{q_{\alpha \beta}}$ on the right-hand side is taken at constant $h_{\alpha}$.

Therefore,

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:Y-H-DERIVATIVE}
\pdv{y_\alpha}{h_\alpha} = -\frac{1}{h_\alpha}\sum_{\beta=1}^N x_\beta \left(d\, w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha) + q_{\alpha\beta} \pdv{w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha)}{q_{\alpha\beta}}\right).
\end{equations}

The spatial derivative of $V_{\alpha}$ reads similarly

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\grad_\alpha V_\beta = -\frac{x_\alpha}{y_\beta^2}\grad_\alpha y_\beta,
\end{equations}

with

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:Y-X-DERIVATIVE}
\grad_\alpha y_\beta = x_{\alpha} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \delta_{\alpha \beta} \sum_{\gamma=1}^N x_{\gamma} \grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \gamma}\left(h_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

We stress that the kernel gradient is taken \emph{at fixed} $h_\alpha$, so the above equation reduces to the standard SPH gradient interpolation.\sidenote{The relation $\grad_\alpha w_{\alpha\beta} = -\grad_\beta w_{\alpha\beta}$ holds 
\emph{only at the same smoothing length}; with adaptive $h$ it is generally not valid. This is the reason why adaptive-$h$ schemes require the $\grad h$ correction terms.}

In the same manner, we can proceed for $\tilde{V}_{\alpha}$: choose the particle carried scalar $\tilde{x}_{\alpha}$, compute the $\tilde{x}_{\alpha}$-weighted kernel sum $\tilde{y}_{\alpha}$ and set

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:KERNEL-SUM-TILDE-VOLUME}
\tilde{V}_{\alpha} = \frac{\tilde{x}_{\alpha}}{\tilde{y}_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

The derivatives $\pdv{\tilde{V}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}, \grad_{\alpha} \tilde{V}_{\beta}$ have the exact same form as $\pdv{V_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}, \grad_{\alpha} V_{\beta}$. 

The above results can then be substituted into \Cref{eq:DI-2D} and after a lengthy algebraic manipulation one arrives at \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} 

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
m_{\alpha} \dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= -\sum_{\beta=1}^N x_\alpha x_\beta \left(\frac{p_\alpha}{y_\alpha^2} f_{\alpha\beta}\,\grad_\alpha w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\alpha) + \frac{p_\beta}{y_\beta^2}f_{\beta\alpha}\grad_\alpha w_{\alpha\beta}(h_\beta)\right), \label{eq:KERNEL-SUM-MOMENTUM} \\
f_{\alpha\beta} &\coloneq 1 - \frac{\tilde x_\beta}{x_\beta}\left(\frac{h_\alpha}{2\, \tilde y_\alpha}\pdv{y_\alpha}{h_\alpha}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_\alpha}{2\, \tilde y_\alpha}\pdv{\tilde y_\alpha}{h_\alpha}\right)^{-1}. \label{eq:KERNEL-SUM-CORRECTION}
\end{equations}

The factor $2$ in $f_{\alpha\beta}$ replaces the $3$ that appears in Hopkins's three-dimensional formulation \autocite[p.~3]{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a}, reflecting our two-dimensional setting. The system \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-MOMENTUM} is the starting point for the following subsections, where we make specific choices of the weights $x_\alpha, \tilde{x_\alpha}$.

\subsection{Density-entropy}
\label{sec:DI-density-entropy}

Recall the thermodynamics of an ideal gas in an isentropic process. The specific entropy is given as \Cref{eq:ENTROPY}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\eta = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{RT}{\rho^{\gamma -1}}\right) = \eta_{\text{ref}} + c_V \log\left(\frac{p}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right),
\end{equations}

so if $\eta = \, \text{const} \,$, then also the quantity

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:A-ENTROPY}
A = \frac{p}{\rho^{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

is constant $A = \, \text{const} \,$. This property motivates us to call $A$ an \emph{entropy-like} variable. 
We can \emph{choose} $A$ as our main thermodynamic variable used to express the particle pressure as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-PRESSURE}
p_{\alpha} = A_{\alpha} \rho_{\alpha}^{\gamma},
\end{equations}

with $A_{\alpha}$ being the entropy-like variable carried by the particle $\alpha$; since the flow is isentropic, $A_{\alpha}$ \emph{does not change in time.}

Choosing $x_{\alpha} = \tilde{x}_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha}$ as the kernel-sum weights in \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM}, we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	y_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right),
\end{equations}

and so $V_{\alpha} = \tilde{V}_{\alpha} = \frac{\rho_{\alpha}}{m_{\alpha}}$. Since we have chosen the weights $x_{\alpha}, \tilde{x}_{\alpha}$ equal to each other, the $\grad h$ correction term \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-CORRECTION} reads as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-CORRECTION}
	f_{\alpha \beta} = 1 - \frac{m_{\beta}}{m_{\beta}}\left(\frac{h_{\alpha}}{2\rho_{\alpha}} \pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \rho_{\alpha}} \pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1} = \left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \rho_{\alpha}}\pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1} \equiv f_{\alpha}
\end{equations}

and since it is in fact independent of $\beta$ we refer to it as just $f_{\alpha}$.

Together with \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-PRESSURE}, the governing equations \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-MOMENTUM}, \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-CORRECTION} read as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-TOTAL}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(f_{\alpha} A_{\alpha} \rho_{\alpha}^{\gamma-2} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + f_{\beta} A_{\beta}\rho_{\beta}^{\gamma- 2} \grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right), \\
			&= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha} p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta} p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}} \grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right), \\
f_{\alpha} &= \left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \rho_{\alpha}}\pdv{\rho_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}. 
\end{equations}

The system \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-TOTAL} is a \emph{generalization} of the \emph{classical SPH} system \Cref{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH} where the smoothing length $h$ is allowed to vary, \ie with the $\grad h$ terms $f_{\alpha}$ included. This observation serves only as a consistency check with the previous chapters; for to the reasons mentioned in the introduction to \Cref{sec:DENSITY-FREE}, our goal is to avoid using density.

\subsection{Pressure-entropy}
\label{sec:DI-pressure-entropy}

Let us now choose $\tilde{x}_{\alpha} = 1$, yielding 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:PE-Y-TILDE}
\tilde{y}_{\alpha} = \tilde{n}_{\alpha} \coloneq \sum_{\beta=1}^N w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

This quantity has the physical interpretation of \emph{particle number density}, and as such it will be denoted $\tilde{n}_{\alpha}$.
We aim to formulate the governing equations using $A$ as the thermodynamic variable while also avoiding the density. If we take the equation for pressure \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-PRESSURE}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
p_{\alpha} = A_{\alpha} \rho_{\alpha}^\gamma,
\end{equations}

and formally substitute $\rho_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} / V_{\alpha}$, we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:V-ALPHA-1}
	p_{\alpha} = A_{\alpha} \left(\frac{m_{\alpha}}{V_{\alpha}}\right)^{\gamma} = \left(\frac{m_{\alpha} A_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{V_{\alpha}}\right)^{\gamma}, \, \text{\ie} \, V_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}A_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{p^{1/ \gamma}_{\alpha}}.
\end{equations}

If we choose the weights $x_{\alpha}$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:x-PE}
	x_{\alpha} = m_{\alpha} A_{\alpha}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

we obtain 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
y_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} A_{\beta}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right),
\end{equations}

and so 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:V-ALPHA-2}
	V_{\alpha} = \frac{x_{\alpha}}{y_{\alpha}} = \frac{m A_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} A_{\beta}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)}.
\end{equations}

Comparing \Cref{eq:V-ALPHA-1} and \Cref{eq:V-ALPHA-2} we conclude

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:PRESSURE-ENTROPY-PRESSURE}
	p_{\alpha} = \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} A_{\beta}^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha})\right)^{\gamma}.
\end{equations}

The key insight here is that with our choice of $x_{\alpha}$, we are able to completely \emph{replace density}.

With these choices, the system of governing equations \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-MOMENTUM}, \Cref{eq:KERNEL-SUM-CORRECTION} reads as

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:PRESSURE-ENTROPY-TOTAL}
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(A_{\alpha} A_{\beta}\right)^{1/\gamma}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha \beta}p_{\alpha}}{p_{\alpha}^{2/\gamma}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta \alpha}p_{\beta}}{p^{2/\gamma}_{\beta}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right), \\
	f_{\alpha \beta} &= 1 - \left(\frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 A_{\beta}^{1/\gamma}m_{\beta} \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{p_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{\tilde{n}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}, \\
	p_{\alpha} &= \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}A_{\beta}^{1/\gamma}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)\right)^{\gamma}, \\
	\tilde{n}_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

\subsection{Pressure-potential temperature}
\label{sec:DI-pressure-theta}

The pressure-entropy formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-entropy} uses the entropy-like variable $A$ as its main thermodynamic quantity. This quantity is useful in astrophysical applications, where \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013a} originally developed the approach, but in atmospheric sciences, the universally used quantity is the \emph{potential temperature}. Recall we have introduced it in \Cref{sec:POTENTIAL_TEMP} as \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T \left(\frac{p_0}{p}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}},
\end{equations}

where $T$ is the thermodynamic temperature, and $p_0$ is the reference pressure. As discussed in \Cref{sec:POTENTIAL_TEMP} $\theta$ remains constant for isentropic flows---the same as for the entropy-like variable $A$ \Cref{eq:A-ENTROPY}. Using the thermal state equation \Cref{eq:THERMAL}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
p = R \rho T,
\end{equations}

together with the fact 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{R}{c_p} = \frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma},
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = \frac{P}{\rho R} p_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}} p^{\frac{1 - \gamma}{\gamma}} = \frac{p_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{p^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}}{\rho}\right) = \frac{p_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{p}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right)^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} = \frac{p_{0}^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R} A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

so setting 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
C_{\theta A} \coloneq \frac{p_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:theta-A}
	\theta = C_{\theta A} A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}.
\end{equations}

We see there exists a \emph{monotonic transformation} between the potential temperature $\theta$ and the entropy-like variable $A$---our adaptation is therefore \emph{mathematically equivalent} to the pressure-entropy formulation, it does not introduce any new physics.

In this section, we adapt the pressure-entropy formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-entropy} to use $\theta$ instead of $A$. The reformulation is straightforward, but arguably has practical advantages in atmospheric applications---$\theta$ is the standard thermodynamic variable in literature on atmospheric dynamics \autocite{holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013}, so comparisons between atmospheric simulations become direct.
To our best knowledge, this is the first formulation of density-independent SPH using potential temperature as the thermodynamic variable.

Rewriting the weights \Cref{eq:x-PE} using \Cref{eq:theta-A}, \ie setting

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-WEIGHTS}
x_{\alpha} = \frac{m_{\alpha}\theta_{\alpha}}{C_{\theta A}},
\end{equations}

the pressure \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-PRESSURE} becomes

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-PRESSURE}
p_{\alpha} = \sum_{\beta=1}^N\left(\frac{m_{\beta} \theta_{\beta}}{C_{\theta A}}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)\right)^{\gamma} = \frac{1}{C_{\theta A}^{\gamma}}\left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\beta} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)\right)^{\gamma},
\end{equations}

the correction terms \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-CORRECTION} read

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-CORRECTION}
f_{\alpha \beta} = 1 - \left(\frac{C_{\theta A}h_{\alpha}}{2 \theta_{\beta} m_{\beta} n_{\alpha}}\pdv{p_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 n_{\alpha}}\pdv{n_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1},
\end{equations}

and the momentum equation \Cref{eq:DENSITY-ENTROPY-TOTAL} is

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-MOMENTUM}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \frac{1}{C_{\theta A}^{2}}\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\alpha} \theta_{\beta}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha \beta}p_{\alpha}}{p_{\alpha}^{2/\gamma}}\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta \alpha}p_{\beta}}{p_{\beta}^{2/\gamma}}\grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right).
\end{equations}

The system \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-PRESSURE,eq:PRESSURE-THETA-CORRECTION,eq:PRESSURE-THETA-MOMENTUM} is the pressure-potential-temperature formulation of density-independent SPH developed in this work. Notice the potential temperature enters directly rather than through the fractional power $A^{1/\gamma}$, which is convenient in applications. We validate this formulation in \Cref{chap:EXPERIMENTS}.

\subsection{Exner-potential temperature}
\label{sec:DI-EXNER-THETA}

In a brief exposition, we introduce yet another \emph{reformulation} of the pressure-entropy formulation. As mentioned before, we are not introducing any new physics; our goal is to provide a reformulation in variables native to atmospheric sciences. 

The pressure expression \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-PRESSURE} suggests defining the kernel-summed quantity

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:POTENTIAL-DENSITY}
\hat{\rho}_{\alpha} \coloneq \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha}),
\end{equations}


which is the kernel-sum estimator for the \enquote{potential-temperature density} $\rho \theta$. With this notation, the pressure reads

\begin{equations}[single,*]
p_{\alpha} = \frac{\hat{\rho}_{\alpha}^{\gamma}}{C_{\theta A}^{\gamma}}.
\end{equations}

Substituting this into the momentum equation \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-MOMENTUM}, the constants combine to a prefactor 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
1/C_{\theta A}^{\gamma} = R^\gamma / p_{\text{ref}}^{\gamma-1}.
\end{equations}

Aiming to recognize another standard atmospheric variables, we introduce the \emph{Exner function} \autocite{holtonIntroductionDynamicMeteorology2013} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:EXNER}
\Pi \coloneq (p/p_{\text{ref}})^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}.
\end{equations}

A direct computation using \Cref{eq:POTENTIAL-DENSITY} gives

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:EXNER-CONNECTION}
\Pi_{\alpha} = \left(\frac{R\, \hat{\rho}_{\alpha}}{p_{\text{ref}}}\right)^{\gamma-1}.
\end{equations}

By rewriting the momentum equation in terms of this Exner function \Cref{eq:EXNER}, the terms containing $\hat{\rho}_{\alpha}$ are replaced, and the constants simplify further. Substituting $\hat{\rho}_{\alpha} = (p_{\text{ref}}/R) \Pi_{\alpha}^{1/(\gamma-1)}$ into the momentum equation \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-MOMENTUM} yields the final \emph{Exner-potential temperature} formulation:

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:EXNER-THETA}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \frac{R^2}{p_{\text{ref}}} \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\alpha} \theta_{\beta} \left[f_{\alpha\beta}\, \Pi_{\alpha}^{\frac{\gamma-2}{\gamma-1}}\, \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha}) + f_{\beta\alpha}\, \Pi_{\beta}^{\frac{\gamma-2}{\gamma-1}}\, \grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\beta})\right], \\
f_{\alpha\beta} &= 1 - \frac{1}{m_{\beta} \theta_{\beta}}\left(\frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{\hat{\rho}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{\tilde{n}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}, \\
\Pi_{\alpha} &= \left(\frac{R\, \hat{\rho}_{\alpha}}{p_{\text{ref}}}\right)^{\gamma-1}, \\
\hat{\rho}_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\beta} w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha}), \\
\tilde{n}_{\alpha} &= \sum_{\beta=1}^N w_{\alpha\beta}(h_{\alpha}).
\end{equations}

\section{Well-balanced SPH}
\label{sec:WELL-BALANCED-SPH}

% TODO: FIRST ONE IS NOT WELL BALANCED!!!!

The concept of well-balanced numerical methods was introduced in \Cref{sec:WELL-BALANCED}. In this section, we develop well-balanced formulations for the three SPH variants presented earlier: classical SPH \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}, the density-independent pressure-entropy formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-entropy}, and the pressure-potential-temperature formulation \Cref{sec:DI-pressure-theta}. To our knowledge, no well-balanced SPH scheme has been previously developed for any of these formulations in the context of stratified atmospheric flow; the constructions presented here are the central methodological contribution of this thesis.

\subsection{Failure of hydrostatic balance}
\label{sec:SPH-FAILURE}

Let us first demonstrate why standard SPH formulations fail to preserve the \emph{discrete} hydrostatic balance in a \emph{stratified atmosphere}. Notice that a general SPH discretisation of the balance of momentum \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-BALANCE} approximates the $\grad p$ term through kernel interpolation, take the classical SPH \Cref{eq:CLASSICAL-SPH} for instance: 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MOMENTUM-FAILURE}
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \vb{g}
\end{equations}

Suppose the simulation is initialised as an isothermal atmosphere in hydrostatic balance.\sidenote{This initial condition is used in our numerical experiments presented in \Cref{chap:EXPERIMENTS}.} The density, pressure and potential temperature are hence given as \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC}

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\rho(z) &= \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
p(z) &= p_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s} \right),\\
\theta(z) &= T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
T &= \, \text{const},\\
\vb{v} &= \vb{0}.
\end{equations}

During initial force evaluation at $t = 0$, the pressure entering \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-FAILURE} is proportional to the \emph{exponential of the particle's altitude.} Because the pressure drops exponentially, it varies non-linearly across a single smoothing length $h$. The kernel summation approximates $\grad p$ by evaluating the pressure over a discrete, finite set of neighbours, which \emph{inherently} introduces a truncation error $\vb{E}_{\alpha}$. While analytically the pressure gradient and gravitational acceleration cancel \emph{exactly}\sidenote{This is the \emph{definition} of hydrostatic balance: $\grad p = \rho \vb{g}$.}, the discrete SPH approximation yields a non-zero residual \emph{spurious force}. Consequently, at $t = 0$ the momentum balance evaluates to

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \vb{g} = \vb{E}_{\alpha} \neq \vb{0}.
\end{equations}

This artificial residual acceleration $\vb{E}_{\alpha}$ immediately induces spurious velocities, displacing particles from their equilibrium positions. Once the particles shift, the system is \emph{no longer} in \emph{analytical} hydrostatic balance---hence the gradient of pressure evaluated at these new, incorrect altitudes fundamentally \emph{cannot balance gravity}. This creates a destructive feedback loop: the initial truncation error causes particle disorder, which in turn amplifies the gradient errors. Within a few integration steps, this fundamental instability completely destroys the stratified hydrostatic background.

\subsection{Perturbation formulation of the classical SPH}
\label{sec:PERTURBATION-CLASSICAL-SPH}

As established in \Cref{sec:CLASSIC-COMPLETE}, the classical SPH discretisation of the balance of linear momentum for an inviscid fluid in gravitational field

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:MOMENTUM-ANALYTICAL}
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p + \vb{g},
\end{equations}

reads 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\label{eq:SPH-MOMENTUM}
	\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} &= - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\left(\frac{p_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \vb{g}. \\
\end{equations}

Evidently, the kernel sum on the right-hand side approximates the $-\frac{1}{\rho} \grad p$ term, while the gravitational acceleration \enquote{approximates} $\vb{g}$ is a global source term. As discussed in \Cref{sec:SPH-FAILURE}, this discretisation does not preserve hydrostatic balance.

A common approach in the FVM literature \autocite{bottaWellBalancedFinite2004} to resolve the truncation error is to subtract the background state \emph{before discretisation}. We stress that this procedure is \emph{not well-balancing} as discussed in \Cref{sec:WELL-BALANCED}---here the hydrostatic balance is preserved by a manipulation on the \emph{analytical level} and the discretisation is emph{standard}, whereas a well-balanced scheme solves the \emph{original system} but employs a \emph{special} discretisation.
Assume now, in correspondence to \Cref{sec:MTN-WVS}, that the density and pressure field have the form\sidenote{In the chapter \Cref{chap:FLOWS} we used the subscript zero and one for the background state and the perturbation respectively. Since we index particle-carried quantities with lower indices also, we switch to the bar $\overline{\rho}$ and prime $\rho'$ notation.}

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-SPH-SPLIT}
\rho\left(t, \vb{x}\right) &= \overline{\rho}(z) + \rho'\left(t, \vb{x}\right), \\
p\left(t, \vb{x}\right) &= \overline{p}(z) + p'\left(t, \vb{x}\right), \\
\end{equations}

where $\overline{\rho}(z), \overline{p}(z)$ are background density and pressure that \emph{solve the hydrostatic balance},\ie $\grad \overline{p} = \overline{\rho} \vb{g}$. Then the balance of linear momentum becomes

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\left(\overline{\rho} + \rho'\right)\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \grad \overline{p} - \grad p' + \overline{\rho} \vb{g} + \rho' \vb{g} = - \grad p' + \rho' \vb{g},
\end{equations}

and so 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-MOMENTUM-ANALYTICAL}
\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p' + \frac{\rho'}{\rho}\vb{g}.
\end{equations}

Comparing \Cref{eq:WB-MOMENTUM-ANALYTICAL} and \Cref{eq:MOMENTUM-ANALYTICAL}, we are inspired to write the approximation \Cref{eq:SPH-MOMENTUM} as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:SPH-PERTURBATION}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = - \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(\frac{p'_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}^{2}} + \frac{p'_{\beta}}{\rho_{\beta}^{2}}\right)\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta} + \frac{\rho'_{\alpha}}{\rho_{\alpha}} \vb{g},
\end{equations}

in which again the first term approximates $ - 1/\rho \grad p'$ and the second approximates $\rho'/\rho \vb{g}$ from \Cref{eq:WB-MOMENTUM-ANALYTICAL}. Since the forcing is proportional to the pressure perturbation $p'$, we will refer to the system \Cref{eq:SPH-PERTURBATION} as the \emph{perturbation SPH}.

Notice that at $t = 0$, the system is in hydrostatic balance, so the perturbations vanish $\rho' = 0, p' = 0$, therefore the whole right-hand side of \Cref{eq:SPH-PERTURBATION} vanishes---there is \emph{no residual velocity.}

\subsection{Well-balanced pressure-entropy formulation}
\label{sec:WB-DI-PA}

To achieve a well-balanced formulation within the density-independent SPH framework, see \Cref{sec:DENSITY-FREE}, we will first replace the \emph{analytical} gravitational source term $\vb{g}$ with the gradient of the \emph{analytical} background density. The procedure is as follows.

The momentum equation in our case reads as

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p + \vb{g},
\end{equations}

when the background is hydrostatic, then $\grad \overline{p} = \vb{g} \overline{\rho}$ and so

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:WB-INSPIRATION}
	\tdot{\vb{v}} = - \frac{1}{\rho} \grad p + \frac{1}{\rho} \grad \overline{p}.
\end{equations}

We have reformulated the momentum equation using the gradient of the \emph{total pressure} and the gradient of the \emph{background pressure}. Notice that we have \emph{not} introduced any perturbation splitting---we merely assumed the background is in hydrostatic balance. 

The key insight is that by discretising \emph{both the gradients} using the \emph{same kernel sum} formulae, the truncation errors \emph{subtract identically}. Let us show that in more detail.

Recall the entropy-like variable $A$ \Cref{eq:A-ENTROPY}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	A = \frac{p}{\rho^{\gamma}}.
\end{equations}

Assuming again a hydrostatic \emph{isothermal} background, the background pressure and density have the form \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC}

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\overline{\rho}(z) &= \rho_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
	\overline{p}(z) &= p_0 \exp\left(- \frac{z}{H_s} \right),\\
\end{equations}

and so the background state of $A$ reads

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:HYDROSTATIC-A}
\overline{A}(z) = A_0 \exp\left(- \left(1 - \gamma\right) \frac{z}{H_s}\right), 
\end{equations}

where $A_0 = \frac{p_0}{\rho_0^{\gamma}}$. Denote by $\overline{A}_{\alpha} = \overline{A}(z_{\alpha})$, $z_{\alpha}$ being the $z$ coordinate of $\vb{x}_{\alpha}$, the analytical background $A$ \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC-A} evaluated at the current altitude of particle $\alpha$. We define the \emph{discrete background pressure} $\overline{p}_{\alpha}$ and the corresponding \emph{background correction factor} $\overline{f}_{\alpha \beta}$ by substituting this background entropy into the kernel sums from \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-ENTROPY-TOTAL}:

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-BACKGROUND-STATE}
\overline{p}_{\alpha} &= \left(\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta}\overline{A}_{\beta}^{1/\gamma}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right)\right)^{\gamma}, \\
\overline{f}_{\alpha \beta} &= 1 - \left(\frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \overline{A}_{\beta}^{1/\gamma}m_{\beta} \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{\overline{p}_{\alpha}^{1/\gamma}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)\left(1 + \frac{h_{\alpha}}{2 \tilde{n}_{\alpha}}\pdv{\tilde{n}_{\alpha}}{h_{\alpha}}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equations}

Denote by $\vb{F}_{\alpha}$ the total SPH pressure gradient force from the pressure-entropy formulation \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-ENTROPY-TOTAL}, that is

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-PA-GRADIENT}
\vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(A_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}\right) =  \sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \left(A_{\alpha} A_{\beta}\right)^{1/\gamma}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha \beta}p_{\alpha}}{p_{\alpha}^{2/\gamma}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta \alpha}p_{\beta}}{p^{2/\gamma}_{\beta}} \nabla_{\alpha} w_{\alpha\beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right). 
\end{equations}

We obtain the well-balanced momentum equation by \emph{subtracting the discrete background gradient} instead of adding analytical gravity, \ie by discretising \Cref{eq:WB-INSPIRATION} using \Cref{eq:WB-PA-GRADIENT}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-PA-MOMENTUM}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = \vb{F}_{\alpha}(A_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}) - \vb{F}_{\alpha}(\overline{A}_{\alpha}, \overline{p}_{\alpha}).
\end{equations}

If the fluid is in the background hydrostatic balance, \ie $A_{\alpha} = \overline{A}_{\alpha}$ for \emph{all particles} $\alpha$ then the discrete total pressure and the correction terms perfectly matches their discrete background counterparts $p_{\alpha} = \overline{p}_{\alpha}$, $f_{\alpha \beta} = \overline{f}_{\alpha \beta}$, and so the discrete gradients \Cref{eq:WB-PA-GRADIENT} are equal  

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(A_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}\right) = \vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(\overline{A}_{\alpha}, \overline{p}_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

Consequently, the resulting acceleration in \Cref{eq:WB-PA-MOMENTUM} is \emph{exactly zero}: because the identical kernel is used with both states simultaneously, the truncation error induced by non-linear background profiles perfectly \emph{cancels itself}. This guarantees exact hydrostatic balance at machine precision while maintaining the fully compressible, non-linear thermodynamic equations required for atmospheric dynamics.


\subsection{Well-balanced pressure-potential temperature formulation}
\label{sec:WB-DI-PTH}

Similarly as in the previous chapter, we can derive a well-balanced version of the pressure-potential temperature formulation. We have shown in \Cref{sec:DI-density-entropy} that the pressure-potential temperature formulation is mathematically equivalent to the pressure-entropy formulation, therefore, the procedure will be exactly the same as in the previous.

Define the background isothermal hydrostatic potential temperature profile $\overline{\theta}$ \Cref{eq:HYDROSTATIC} and the corresponding particle-carried quantities

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-PTH-BACKGROUND}
\overline{\theta}(z) &= T \exp\left(\frac{R}{c_p}\frac{z}{H_s}\right),\\
\overline{\theta}_{\alpha} &= \overline{\theta}\left(z_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

Introduce $\vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(\theta_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}\right)$ as the pressure gradient force from \Cref{eq:PRESSURE-THETA-MOMENTUM}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WBTH-GRADIENT}
\vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(\theta_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}\right) = \frac{1}{C_{\theta A}^{2}}\sum_{\beta=1}^N m_{\beta} \theta_{\alpha} \theta_{\beta}\left(\frac{f_{\alpha \beta}p_{\alpha}}{p_{\alpha}^{2/\gamma}}\grad_{\alpha} w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\alpha}\right) + \frac{f_{\beta \alpha}p_{\beta}}{p_{\beta}^{2/\gamma}}\grad_{\alpha}w_{\alpha \beta}\left(h_{\beta}\right)\right).
\end{equations}

The \emph{well-balanced} momentum equation is then obtained similarly as in \Cref{eq:WB-PA-MOMENTUM} by subtracting the background pressure-force from the total pressure-force, \ie by discretising \Cref{eq:WB-INSPIRATION} via \Cref{eq:WBTH-GRADIENT}

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:WB-PTH-MOMENTUM}
\dv{\vb{v}_{\alpha}}{t} = \vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(\theta_{\alpha}, p_{\theta}\right) - \vb{F}_{\alpha}\left(\overline{\theta}_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha}\right).
\end{equations}

If the fluid parcel is in the stationary background state, \ie $\theta_{\alpha} = \overline{\theta}_{\alpha}, p_{\alpha} = \overline{p}_{\alpha},$ then also the discrete gradients \Cref{eq:WBTH-GRADIENT} are equal and so the resulting acceleration in \Cref{eq:WB-PTH-MOMENTUM} \emph{remains zero.}

Note that there are well-balanced FVM formulations utilizing the conserved potential temperature $\theta $\autocite{artianoStructurePreservingHighOrderMethods2025,chertockWellbalancedNumericalMethod2023}.
