\section{Method overview}
\label{sec:SPH_OVERVIEW}

Smoothed Particle Hydrodynamics (SPH) is a general use numerical method first used in astrophysical stellar models \autocite{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}. The authors required a solution to a grid resolution problem arising with their use of the finite difference method (FDM).\sidenote{They argued that the treatment of highly distorted continuum would require an extremely fine grid, hence substantial computational cost \autocite[p.~1]{gingoldSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics1977}.} The method has found use in a variety of fields, \viz free-surface flows \autocite{monaghanSimulatingFreeSurface1994a}, solid mechanics \autocite{liberskySmoothParticleHydrodynamics1991}, geomechanics \autocite{buiLagrangianMeshfreeParticles2008}, and is still successfully used in astrophysics \autocite{springelSimulationsFormationEvolution2005}. 

SPH is a particle (Lagrangian) method.\sidenote{In this sense grid/mesh methods such as the \begin{inparaenum} \item finite difference method; \item finite element method; \item finite volume method; \item discontinuous Galerkin method, \end{inparaenum} are called \emph{Eulerian}, since they approximate the evolution in the \emph{current, deformed} configuration. Particle methods like \begin{inparaenum} \item SPH; \item MPS (moving particle semi-implicit method) \autocite{koshizukaMovingParticleSemiImplicitMethod1996}; \item DPD (dissipative particle dynamics) \autocite{hoogerbruggeSimulatingMicroscopicHydrodynamic1992} \end{inparaenum} trace the evolution in the \emph{reference} configuration, and so particle methods are also called \emph{Lagrangian}.}   The continuum is partitioned into arbitrary \emph{material points}, in this context referred to as \emph{particles}, and basic physical properties such as density, velocity, entropy, and energy are assigned to them. Their mutual interaction is then prescribed,\sidenote{The interaction cannot be arbitrary. To capture the physics correctly, the interaction must in fact root in the discretization of the governing equations.} and the state of each particle is advanced in time. 
We stress that \emph{SPH particles} are \emph{not} \textcolor{gray}{elementary} particles in the microscopic sense --- they are typically macroscopic fluid elements. Nonetheless, some intuition from the mechanics of point particles carries over naturally to SPH, as we shall see.

In what follows, we introduce the theoretical foundations of SPH and the specific formulation developed in this work.

\subsection{Kernel interpolation}
\label{sec:INTERPOLATION}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be the continuous medium and $\Phi: \Rplusnought \times \Omega \to \R$ an arbitrary scalar valued field, \eg density. Suppose further that $\forall t \in \Rplusnought$ the mapping $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi(t, \vb{x})$ is in $\Loneloc{\Omega}$, then

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY}
\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \left(\Phi \star \delta\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)\delta\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x'}},
\end{equations}

holds for almost all $\vb{x} \in \Omega$. A common assumption in the SPH literature is to assume $\vb{x} \mapsto \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \in \Czero{\Omega}$, and so the equality \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} holds pointwise in $\Omega$. To make the presentation simpler, throughout we always assume sufficient regularity of all quantities involved and do not pursue measure-theoretic rigor.

The main point of departure of the SPH method is to replace the Dirac delta distribution in \Cref{eq:DIRAC-PROPERTY} by an \emph{interpolation kernel} $w_h: \Omega \to \R$ \autocite[p.~305]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012}, \ie

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)\coloneq \left(\Phi \star w_h\right)\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

The mapping $\Phi \mapsto \sphc{\Phi}$ is called \emph{continuous interpolation}. Certainly, the interpolation kernel $w_{h}$ cannot be arbitrary. We aim to approximate the function $\Phi$ by its continuous interpolation $\sphc{\Phi}$, that is

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) \approx \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}'\right)w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'}.
\end{equations}

To obtain a good approximation, the kernel must satisfy \autocite[p.~60]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004} the conditions written out in \Cref{tab:kernel-properties}.

\begin{table}[H]
	\centering
	\caption{Properties of the interpolation kernel $w_h$.}
	\label{tab:kernel-properties}
	\begin{tabular}{@{}cll@{}}
		\toprule
		& Property & Condition \\
		\midrule
		(K1) & positivity & $w_h \geq 0$ \\
		(K2) & monotonicity & $w_h$ is a decreasing function \\
		(K3) & symmetry & $w_h$ is radial, \ie $w_h\left(\vb{x} - \vb{x}'\right) = w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x} - \vb{x}'}\right)$ \\
		(K4) & normalization & $\int_{\Omega}w_h\left(\vb{r}\right)\dd{\vb{r}} = 1$ \\
		(K5) & compact support/decay & $\supp w_h$ is compact or $\lim_{\abs{\vb{r}} \to \infty}w\left(\vb{r}\right) = 0$ sufficiently fast \\
		(K6) & smoothness & $w_h$ be \emph{sufficiently smooth} \\
		(K7) & concentration & $w_h \xrightharpoonup{*} \delta$ as $h \to 0^+$ \\
		\bottomrule
	\end{tabular}
\end{table}

For compact kernels, $h$ is related to the size of the support radius as $\diam \supp = 2 \kappa h,$ where $\kappa$ is kernel-dependent. If $w_h$ is not compact, \eg for a Gaussian kernel \autocite[p.~8]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
w_h\left(\vb{r}\right) = \frac{1}{\left(h \sqrt{\pi}\right)^{d}}\exp\left(-\frac{\abs{\vb{r}}^{2}}{h^{2}}\right),
\end{equations}

$h$ is the characteristic width of the Gaussian.

Common choice for the interpolation kernel are \enquote{bell shaped} functions, such as the Gaussian, B-splines \autocite{monaghanExtrapolatingSplinesInterpolation1985} and other splines. An overview of the commonly used kernels can be found in \autocite[p.~62]{liuSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2004}. 

In our application, we deployed the $\Ctwo$ Wendland polynomial kernels \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}\autocite{dehnenNewKernelsSPH2012}, \ie a special class of piecewise polynomial positive compactly supported functions.\sidenote{In the class of positive definite compactly supported radial univariate polynomials of prescribed smoothness and dimension, the Wendland functions are of minimal degree and moreover are unique. \autocite{wendlandPiecewisePolynomialPositive1995}} In particular, the quintic Wendland kernel has been used

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}
	w_h\left(q\right) = \begin{cases} \frac{7}{4 \pi h^{2}}\left(1 - \frac{q}{2}\right)^4\left(2q + 1\right),& 0 \leq q \leq 2, \\
		0,& q > 2,
	\end{cases}, q = \frac{\abs{\vb{r}}}{h}.
\end{equations}

The graph of the Wendland kernel is plotted in \Cref{fig:KERNEL}. It is evident that for the Wendland kernel \Cref{eq:WENDLAND-KERNEL}, $w_h\left(\frac{\vb{r}}{h}\right) = 0 $ for $\abs{\vb{r}} \leq 2 h$, meaning $4 h = \diam \supp w_h$ and we call $h$ the \emph{smoothing length}\sidenote{As opposed to \enquote{smoothing radius}, which might be confusing.}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
	\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{kernel}
	\caption{Wendland quintic kernel, $h = 1$.}
	\label{fig:KERNEL}
\end{figure}

Under the conditions \Cref{tab:kernel-properties} on the interpolation kernel $w_h$, it can be shown\sidenote{The derivation is a straightforward application of the Taylor's expansion.} \autocite[p.~307]{violeauFluidMechanicsSPH2012} 

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:C-APPROX}
\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{h^{2}}.
\end{equations}

This approximation property evidently heavily influences the numerical accuracy of the method. A more involved discussion of SPH accuracy and consistency will be presented in section \Cref{sec:ACCURACY}.


\subsection{Particle approximation}
\label{sec:PARTICLES}

Let $\Omega \subset \R^d, d = 2, 3$ be a continuous medium, which we partition into $N \in \N$ particles with coordinates $\{\vb{x}_i\}_{i=1}^N \subset \R^{dN}$. Denote by $V_i$ some \emph{estimator} of the particle volume, \eg

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:MASS-DENSITY}
V_i = \frac{\rho_i}{m_i},
\end{equations}

with $\rho_i$ being the particles density and $m_i$ its mass. The choice \Cref{eq:MASS-DENSITY} is clearly \emph{not a unique} choice of the volume estimator; the freedom of choice will be invoked in 
% TODO: dodelat referenci
	Using this particle partition, we simplify the continuous interpolation \Cref{eq:CONT-INTERPOLATION} by deploying a quadrature rule. Let $\vb{x}_i$ be the position of an arbitrary particle, then we approximate

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) = \int_{\Omega}\Phi\left(t, \vb{x'}\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}'}\right)\dd{\vb{x}'} \approx \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)V_j, 
\end{equations}
that is, we approximate the integration by a \emph{quadrature rule} with nodes $\{\vb{x}_j\}_{j=1}^N$ and weights $\{V_j\}_{j=1}^N$. This procedure can be thought of as introducing a \emph{discrete interpolation} operator $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) \mapsto \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right)$ given as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_{i}\right) \coloneq \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right) w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)V_j \equiv \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi_j w_{ij}V_j,
\end{equations}

where we have adopted the notation $\Phi_j \coloneq \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right),$ $w_{ij} \coloneq w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)$. The quantity $\Phi_j$ can be naturally interpreted as the quantity $\Phi$, \ie density, of the particle $\vb{x}_j$. It should be clarified that $\Phi_j = \Phi_j(t)$ is still a function of time.
Notice also that since the kernel $w_h$ has a compact support, in practice the sum in \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION} typically does \emph{not} consist of $N$ summands.\sidenote{Note well, if it were the case, all particles would have to be located within each other's support radius, \ie very closely. In SPH, extreme proximity gives rise to a serious instability issue known as \emph{tensile instability} \Cref{sec:INSTABILITY}.}

Having introduced the \emph(discrete) interpolation, we deploy it to approximate the \emph{continuous} interpolation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) \approx \sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right),
\end{equations}

and one would immediately be interested in the quality of the approximation. It turns out that an involved discrete approximation error is a complicated \emph{open} problem \autocite[p.~3]{vacondioGrandChallengesSmoothed2021a}\autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. The main difficulty lies in the fact that the error analysis depends on the particle positions \textcolor{gray}(that change in time). 
If one considers particles evenly arranged in a Cartesian grid (which is a substantial simplification), it can be shown\sidenote{Deploying techniques from Fourier analysis and the Euler-MacLaurin formula.} that for the Wendland quintic kernel it holds

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:DIS-ERROR}
\sphd{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \sphc{\Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6},
\end{equations}
with $\dd{r}$ being the typical particle size, \ie $\dd{r} \propto V^{\frac{1}{d}}$, for particles of typical volume $V$.

\subsection{Accuracy of SPH}
\label{sec:ACCURACY}

A presentation of a numerical method would not be complete without a mention of the \emph{error estimation} --- that is, what accuracy the method achieves. 

There are two sources of errors in the SPH discretisation: \begin{inparaenum} \item continuous kernel interpolation, \item discrete particle approximation \end{inparaenum}, which we analysed in \Cref{eq:C-APPROX} and \Cref{eq:DIS-ERROR} respectively. Combining these, we obtain for a sufficiently smooth scalar field $\Phi$, the quintic Wendland kernel of smoothing length $h$, and particles of size $\dd{r}$ arranged on a regular Cartesian grid,

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:ERR}
\sphd{\Phi} = \Phi + \bigO{h^{2}} + \bigO{\left(\frac{\dd{r}}{h}\right)^6}.
\end{equations}

This relation shows that the SPH method is \emph{at least first order}. We say \enquote{at least} because for particular combinations of kernel, geometry and particle arrangement, the observed order can be higher \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}. 
Moreover, the error decreases when both $h \to 0^+$ and $\dd{r}/h \to 0^+$, equivalently $h/\dd{r} \to \infty$. This reveals an interesting relationship between the smoothing length $h$ and the particle size $\dd{r}$: to improve accuracy, both must vanish\sidenote{This matches physical intuition: as the particles shrink and their number grows, the discrete particle system approaches continuum.}, but \emph{not independently} --- $\dd{r}$ must decay faster than $h$. The precise interplay of these two parameters remains an active area of analysis \autocite{quinlanTruncationErrorMeshfree2006}.


Let us summarize the presented properties and situate SPH within the broader landscape of numerical methods for continuum mechanics. Mesh-based Eulerian methods such as the finite volume method and, in particular, the discontinuous Galerkin method can be constructed to achieve arbitrarily high order of convergence, at least in \emph{smooth regions of the solution}.\sidenote{Phenomena like shocks and other contact discontinuities decrease the order of convergence.} In this comparison, SPH --- which is often merely first order in practice --- is clearly less competitive.

However, accuracy should not be the only criterion. The mesh-free nature of SPH allows us to tackle problems that are notoriously difficult for mesh schemes: free-surface flows, large deformations, plasticity, flows in complex or evolving geometries. No meshing routines are required. The method is also remarkably simple to implement.

In summary, SPH trades numerical accuracy for flexibility and implementational simplicity. The choice of method is therefore dictated by the nature of the problem under study.

\subsection{Differential operators}
\label{sec:DIFF_OPS}

This section employs the presented particle approximation framework to discretize differential operators appearing in the governing equations of fluid dynamics. Even though we have, up to this point, assumed only scalar fields, the generalization to vector and tensor fields is straightforward.

\subsubsection{Gradient of a scalar field}
\label{sec:GRADIENT}

The gradient of a differentiable scalar field $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at particle $\vb{x}_i$ can be in the nature of \Cref{eq:DISC-INTERPOLATION} approximated as

\begin{equations}[columns]
	\grad_i \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_i \Phi}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) = \sum_{j=1}^N \grad_i\left(\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)\right)V_j =\\
						    &= \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right)\grad_i w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)V_j =\\
						    &=\sum_{j=1}^N \Phi_j \grad_i w_{ij} V_j,\label{eq:GRAD}
\end{equations}

since $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right)$ is independent of $\vb{x}_i$,

\subsubsection{Divergence of a vector field}
\label{sec:DIVERGENCE}

The discrete divergence of a continuously differentiable vector field $\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right)$ at a particle $\vb{x}_i$ is defined as

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \sphd{\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) = \sum_{j=1}^N\left(\grad_i \Big(\vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right)w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right)\Big)V_j\right) = \\
						     &= \sum_{j=1}^N \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_j\right) \cdot \grad_i w_h\left(\abs{\vb{x}_i - \vb{x}_j}\right) V_j = \sum_{j=1}^N \vb{F}_j \cdot \grad_i w_{ij} V_j.
\end{equations}

\subsubsection{Improved discretization}
\label{sec:IMPROVED-DIFF-OPS}

In summary, the straightforward way of discretizing the $\gradop$ and $\div$ operators give

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi_j \grad_i w_{ij}V_j,\label{eq:NAIVE-GRAD} \\
\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \sum_{j=1}^N \vb{F}_j \cdot \grad_i w_{ij}V_j. \label{eq:NAIVE-DIV}
\end{equations}

However, this is not a unique way to discretize the differential operators --- and particularly not a good one either. To demonstrate, take the constant fields $\Phi\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = 1, \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}\right) = \vb{e}_x,$ then

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
	\grad_i \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{j=1}^N \Phi_j \grad_i w_{ij}V_j = \sum_{j=1}^N \grad_i w_{ij}V_j, \\
	\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) = 0 &\neq \sum_{j=1}^N \vb{F}_j \cdot \grad_i w_{ij}V_j = \sum_{j=1}^N \vb{e}_x \cdot \grad_i w_{ij}V_j.
\end{equations}

The naive discretization \Cref{eq:NAIVE-GRAD}, \Cref{eq:NAIVE-DIV} fails to approximate constant fields. To account for constant fields, we draw inspiration \autocite[p.~10]{monaghanSmoothedParticleHydrodynamics2005} from the identities

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:IDENTITIES}
\grad \Phi &= \frac{1}{\rho} \grad\left(\rho \Phi\right) - \frac{\Phi}{\rho}\grad \rho, \\
\divergence{\vb{F}} &= \frac{1}{\rho}\divergence{\left(\rho \vb{F}\right)} - \frac{1}{\rho}\vb{F} \cdot \grad \rho,
\end{equations}

valid for a nonzero continuously differentiable function $\rho$.\sidenote{The notation is suggestive --- in what follows, $\rho$ will indeed be the density.} We can improve our approximation by discretizing in the following manner

% HACK: is this correct?

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\grad_i \Phi_i &= \frac{1}{\rho_i} \grad_i \left(\rho_i \Phi_i\right) - \frac{\Phi_i}{\rho_i} \grad_i\left(\rho_i\right) \approx \\ 
	       &\approx \frac{1}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \rho_j \Phi_j \grad_i w_{ij} V_j - \frac{\Phi_i}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \rho_j \grad_i w_{ij} V_j =\\
	       &= \frac{1}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \left(\Phi_j - \Phi_i\right)\rho_j \grad_i w_{ij}V_j,
\end{equations}

and 

\begin{equations}[columns,*]
\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}_i &= \frac{1}{\rho_i} \grad_i \cdot \left(\rho_i \vb{F}_i\right) - \frac{1}{\rho_i} \vb{F}_i \cdot \grad_i \rho_i \approx \\
                       &\approx \frac{1}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \rho_j \vb{F}_j \cdot \grad_i w_{ij} V_j - \frac{1}{\rho_i} \vb{F}_i \cdot \sum_{j=1}^N \rho_j \grad_i w_{ij} V_j =\\
		       &= \frac{1}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_j - \vb{F}_i\right) \rho_j  \cdot \grad_i w_{ij} V_j.
\end{equations}

Evidently, thanks to the $\Phi_j - \Phi_i$ and $\vb{F}_j - \vb{F}_i$ terms respectively, this discretization correctly vanishes for constant fields. 

All in all, the discretization

\begin{equations}[columns,!,numberline=all]
\grad \Phi\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \frac{1}{\rho_i} \sum_{j=1}^N \left(\Phi_j - \Phi_i\right) \rho_j \grad_i w_{ij} V_j ,\label{eq:IMPROVED-GRAD} \\
\grad_i \cdot \vb{F}\left(t, \vb{x}_i\right) &\approx \frac{1}{\rho_1}\sum_{j=1}^N \left(\vb{F}_j - \vb{F}_i \right)\rho_j \cdot \grad_i w_{ij}V_j, \label{eq:IMPROVED-DIV}
\end{equations}

will be preferred in the section \Cref{sec:WCSPH}.

\subsubsection{Pressure-entropy}
\label{sec:HOPKINS_PE}
Recall that the entropy of an isentropic flow of an ideal gas has the form\sidenote{This is a test side note.}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\eta = \eta_0 + c_v \log\left(\frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right)
\end{equations}
and $\eta = \, \text{const} \,.$ We see that the quantity $A$ defined as \autocite{hopkinsGeneralClassLagrangian2013}

\begin{equations}[single,*]
A = \frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}},
\end{equations}
is in fact equal to

\begin{equations}[single,*]
A = \exp\left(\frac{\eta - \eta_0}{c_v}\right),
\end{equations}
meaning that the flow is \emph{isentropic,} if and only if the quantity $A$ is \emph{conserved} under the flow. Thus $A$ is an \emph{entropy-like} variable: conserved under isentropic flows.

The choice

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:x_PE}
	x_i = m_i A_i^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}
gives the pressure $P_i$ as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:P_PE}
	P_i = \left(\sum_{j=1}^N m_j A_j^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} w_{ij}(h_i)\right)^{\gamma}
\end{equations}
and the evolution equations as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:v_PE}
	\dv{\vb{v}_i}{t} = - \sum_{j=1}^N m_j \left(A_i A_j\right)^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}\left(\frac{f_{i}P_i}{P_{i}^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_i w_{ij}\left(h_i\right) + \frac{f_{j}P_j}{P^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}_{j}} \nabla_i w_{ij}\left(h_j\right)\right),
\end{equations}
with

\begin{equations}[single,*]
	\label{eq:f_PE}
	f_{i} =  \left(1 + \frac{h_i}{2 n_i} \pdv{n_i}{h_i}\right)^{-1}
\end{equations}

\subsubsection{Pressure-potential temperature}
\label{sec:HOPKINS_theta}


Recall the \emph{potential temperature} $\theta$

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = T \left(\frac{P_0}{P}\right)^{\frac{R}{c_p}},
\end{equations}

where $T$ is the gas's \textcolor{gray}{thermodynamic} temperature, and $P_0$ is the reference pressure. \sidenote{Potential temperature is the thermodynamic temperature the air parcel would possess if \emph{adiabatically} moved from the thermodynamic state $\left(T, P\right)$ to the state $\left(\theta, P_0\right)$.} Using the thermal state equation

\begin{equations}[single,*]
P = R \rho T,
\end{equations}
together with the fact 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\frac{R}{c_p} = \frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma},
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\theta = \frac{P}{\rho R} P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}} P^{\frac{1 - \gamma}{\gamma}} = \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{P^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}}{\rho}\right) = \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}\left(\frac{P}{\rho^{\gamma}}\right)^{\frac{1}{\gamma}} = \frac{P_{0}^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R} A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}},
\end{equations}

so setting 

\begin{equations}[single,*]
\tilde{\theta}_0 \coloneq \frac{P_0^{\frac{\gamma - 1}{\gamma}}}{R}
\end{equations}

we obtain

\begin{equations}[single, !,numberline=all]
	\label{eq:theta_A}
	\theta = \tilde{\theta}_0 A^{\frac{1}{\gamma}}.
\end{equations}

This implies the potential temperature $\theta$ is also \emph{an entropy-like} variable - it is \emph{conserved} under isentropic flows.

Rewriting \ref{eq:x_PE} using \ref{eq:theta_A} we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:x_Ptheta}
x_i = \frac{m_i}{\tilde{\theta}_0} \theta_i.
\end{equations}

Using this quantity the pressure \ref{eq:P_PE} can then be expressed as

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:P_Ptheta}
P_i = \left(\sum_{j=1}^N \frac{m_j}{\tilde{\theta_0}} \theta_j w_{ij}\left(h_i\right)\right)^{\gamma}.
\end{equations}

From \ref{eq:v_PE} we obtain

\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:v_Ptheta}
\dv{\vb{v}_i}{t} = - \sum_{j=1}^N m_j \frac{\theta_i \theta_j}{\tilde{\theta}_0^{2}}\left(\frac{f_{i}P_i}{P_i^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_i w_{ij}\left(h_i\right) + \frac{f_{j}P_j}{P_j^{\frac{2}{\gamma}}} \nabla_i w_{ij}\left(h_j\right)\right),
\end{equations}

with \ref{eq:f_PE} unchanged
\begin{equations}[single,!,numberline=all]
\label{eq:f_Ptheta}
	f_{i} =  \left(1 + \frac{h_i}{2 n_i} \pdv{n_i}{h_i}\right)^{-1}.
\end{equations}
